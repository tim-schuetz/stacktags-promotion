--- HOOK (0:00–0:14) — the payoff up front ---

When North Korean hackers pulled off the biggest crypto heist in history — around
1.5 billion dollars — they ran straight into a problem most people never expect:
[A vault drained; a giant "$1.5B" glowing.]
in crypto, you can't actually hide the money. Every coin they stole was visible to
the entire world.
[The stolen funds lit up on a public ledger for everyone to see.]

--- THE MYTH (≈0:14–0:38) ---

Here's what most people get wrong. They think crypto is anonymous — perfect for
crime. It's almost the opposite.
[A "100% anonymous?" label getting stamped FALSE.]
Most blockchains are a public ledger: every transaction, every wallet, every
movement, recorded forever, for anyone on Earth to read.
[An endless transparent ledger scrolling.]
So when the group known as Lazarus drained that exchange, the whole world could
watch the stolen funds sitting in their wallets. Stealing it was the easy part.
Spending it is the hard part.

--- WHY THEY CAN'T JUST SPEND IT (≈0:38–1:00) ---

Because the moment they try to send that money to an exchange to cash out, it gets
flagged and frozen.
So to have any hope of using it, they try to break the trail. They do it
three ways.

--- THE THREE MOVES (≈1:00–1:30) ---

One: chain hopping.
[A coin leaping between separate blockchains, over and over.]
They bounce the money across different blockchains, again and again, so the trail
has to keep jumping networks.

Two: mixers.
[Thousands of coins poured into one tumbler and swirled together.]
They throw the coins into a pot with thousands of others and shuffle them, blurring
which coins came from where.

Three: splitting.
[One big pile shattering into a cloud of tiny transfers.]
They chop the haul into thousands of tiny transfers across countless wallets, so
there's no single stream left to follow.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:30–1:44) ---

And that's the irony. The only reason they need all of this is that crypto isn't
anonymous at all.
[The glowing public ledger, every move on record.]
It's the most transparent money ever made — a permanent, public record. So
stealing a billion is the easy part. Actually spending it is a problem most
criminals never saw coming.
