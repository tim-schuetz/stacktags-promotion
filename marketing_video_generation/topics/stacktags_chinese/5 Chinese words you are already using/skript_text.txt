THE 5 WORDS (loanwords English quietly took from Chinese)
==================================================================
1. Typhoon      — 台风  táifēng    — "great wind"
2. Tofu         — 豆腐  dòufu      — "curdled bean" (bean curd)
3. Yin & Yang   — 阴阳  yīn yáng   — "shady side & sunny side"
4. Taikonaut    — 太空  tàikōng    — "the great emptiness" (outer space)
5. Paper Tiger  — 纸老虎 zhǐ lǎohǔ  — "paper tiger" (all show, no bite)

Recurring visual device — "the reveal":
The familiar English word sits on screen, then flips away to reveal the Chinese
characters and pinyin hiding underneath it.

--- HOOK (0:00–0:10) — the payoff lands in the first 5 seconds ---

You already speak a little Chinese — you just don't know it yet.
[Five English words slam onto the screen one per beat as they're named:]
Typhoon. Tofu. Yin and yang. Taikonaut. Paper tiger.
[Each one flickers to show the Chinese characters behind it.]
Every one is an English word that came straight out of Chinese...

We all know English borrowed from Latin and French —
[Word-origin tags fly in: Latin, French, German...]
but it quietly pocketed a few from the other side of the world, with some wild
stories attached.
[The European tags get swept aside; a red Chinese tag drops in.]
Here are 5 Chinese words you're already using.

[ENUMERATION ANIMATION: the 5 words stack into a list 1–5, camera zooms into number 1.]

--- THE 5 (≈0:10–1:25) ---

First, the one nobody suspects — the weather.
1) Typhoon — 台风.
[REVEAL: a swirling storm → "TYPHOON" → flips to 台风 / táifēng.]
That monster storm off the Pacific? The word comes from Chinese 台风 — roughly
"the great wind."

From the weather to your lunch.
2) Tofu, or in Chinese: 豆腐.
[REVEAL: a block of tofu → "TOFU" → flips to 豆腐; 豆 and 腐 highlight separately.]
豆 means "bean," and 腐 means "curdled." So every time you order tofu, you're
literally asking for "curdled bean" — a dish coined in China over two thousand
years ago.

Next, an idea you've definitely seen.
3) Yin and Yang.
[REVEAL: the black-and-white swirl spins in → "YIN & YANG" → flips to 阴阳; 阴 darkens, 阳 brightens.]
阴, is the shady side of a hill; 阳, the sunny side — opposites that
need each other.
[Cut to the South Korean flag; the centre symbol zooms in.]
And yes — that's the symbol in the middle of the South Korean flag, which fuels
a quiet tug-of-war: an ancient Chinese concept that became a national emblem
next door.

4) Taikonaut — 太空.
[Three spacesuits line up with their flags: USA, Russia, China.]
Every space nation names its travelers for the heavens. America has astronauts —
Greek for "star." Russia, cosmonauts - "cosmos." And China?
[REVEAL: the Chinese suit steps forward → "TAIKONAUT" → flips to 太空 / tàikōng.]
Taikonauts — from 太空, literally "the great emptiness." Outer space,
named like a poem.

Our last one isn't a thing — it's an insult that went global.
5) Paper Tiger — 纸老虎.
[REVEAL: a roaring tiger crumples into origami paper → "PAPER TIGER" → flips to 纸老虎.]
A paper tiger looks terrifying — until you see it can't bite. The phrase
conquered English in 1946, when Mao Zedong called US imperialism "a paper
tiger." It got translated word-for-word, and it stuck.
