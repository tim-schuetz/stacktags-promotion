VIDEO SCRIPT
Title options (pick one): The plan to replace Chinese characters with the alphabet
Topic: Learn Chinese
Target length: ~95 seconds (fits the 30s–3min window)
Language of narration: English
Format: Spoken voiceover narration + [bracketed] visual cues. The brackets are NOT spoken —
they are stripped before TTS; the spoken text is exactly the un-bracketed words.

==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
Pinyin (1950s, committee led by Zhou Youguang, adopted 1958) was, for many reformers
— including Mao — envisioned as a BRIDGE to eventually replace characters with a
phonetic alphabet. Officially it was framed as an auxiliary (teach pronunciation,
aid literacy). The replacement goal was never realized and was quietly buried.
Ironic twist: Pinyin is now how people TYPE characters — the tool meant to kill them
keeps them alive. Learn: 拼音, Zhou Youguang.
Punchline: Pinyin missed its target and became the characters' life support.

NOTE (honesty): "kill the characters" is the provocative hook (like the "Mandarin is
broken" video) — resolve honestly: the abolition aspiration was real (esp. Mao/early
reformers) but never fully official end-policy, and was abandoned; Pinyin's real role
became auxiliary + input method.

VISUAL SPINE (anti-slop): TWO recurring heroes carry the whole video — a row of Latin
letters (the pinyin) and a single Chinese character (汉字). The story is told through their
changing RELATIONSHIP (size / dominance / who sits under whom), not a new scene per sentence.
The second half rides ONE metaphor straight from the script: a BRIDGE built of pinyin letters,
meant to lead AWAY from the characters — which ends up the bridge everyone uses to REACH them
(paid off literally by the phone IME: type pinyin → land on the character). On-screen text only
when it ADDS info: the vocab 拼音 (pīnyīn, literally "spell + sound") and 周有光 (Zhōu Yǒuguāng).
White grid bg, white + turquoise only. Swoosh SFX only when the grid moves / a default element
animates; a soft pop per vocab word.

==================================================================
SCRIPT
==================================================================

--- HOOK (0:00–0:14) — the payoff up front ---

[HERO: one big black character 字 centered on the white grid, the pinyin "zì" small beneath it
(the helper relationship we know today). Bottom subtitle only.]
Pinyin — the system that spells Chinese with the Latin alphabet — was originally
built to do one thing: replace Chinese characters completely.
[On "replace … completely": the pinyin letters swell up and slide over the character, which
shrinks and fades toward grey as the alphabet takes its place — the letters now own the frame.]
[The character is almost gone — a faint grey ghost behind the bold turquoise letters.]
It failed.
[Hard beat: the character SNAPS back to full size, solid black and centered; the letters get
shoved back down small. Springy pop.]
And today, it does the exact opposite.
[Settle: 字 big and alive on top, "zì" pinyin resting quietly underneath as its helper. Slow
camera push-in — grid converges (zoom-in). Swoosh.]

--- WHAT PINYIN IS (≈0:14–0:34) ---

Pinyin is the system that writes Chinese sounds using our familiar A-B-C letters.
[NOTE: the example words are SHOWN on screen, not spoken. A 拼音 (pīnyīn) header pops, then three
object vocab cards cascade in — each a real cropped photo + hanzi + pinyin + gloss, stacked above
one another: 米 mǐ (rice) + a rice-sack photo, 筷子 kuàizi (chopsticks) + a chopsticks photo, and the
comic punchline 拉肚子 lā dùzi (diarrhea) + a funny cartoon of someone on the toilet. Pop per card.]
It was created in the 1950s by a committee led by a man named Zhou Youguang, who's
often called the father of Pinyin.
[A real PORTRAIT photo of Zhou Youguang (cropped into a soft profile card) appears with a name card:
周有光 / "Zhōu Yǒuguāng" / "father of Pinyin · 1950s committee".]

--- THE HIDDEN GOAL (≈0:34–1:02) ---

But for many of the reformers behind it — including Mao himself — Pinyin wasn't meant
to just sit politely beside the characters. It was meant to gradually replace them.
[Depth transition. The REFORM WALL hero: a row of five real characters — 文 字 书 语 言 (wén zì shū
yǔ yán, "the written language") — each standing with its pinyin sitting politely BELOW it. On
"gradually replace them": a takeover wave sweeps left→right — the pinyin swells to the centre and the
characters are pushed out, cell by cell. An "ABC — an alphabet future" goal sits top-right.]
The thinking went: the characters were too hard, holding back literacy; the future
was a simple alphabet, like most of the world used. Pinyin was the bridge to get
there.
[On "too hard": the row hides and one monstrously complex character 龘 (48 strokes) zooms in with a
sweating, overwhelmed cartoon learner — "too many strokes". On "simple alphabet": the ABC goal lights
turquoise. On "the bridge to get there": the takeover wave pushes on toward ABC.]
Now, the honest part: that goal was never fully written into law — and it was
never officially declared dead, either. It was simply... buried.
[On "written into law": a big turquoise 法 / "make it law" stamp descends and HOVERS over the row —
but never falls. The takeover freezes with the last character (言) still standing. On "buried": the
stamp fades and the taken-over pinyin greys and sinks.]
As literacy rose and the country modernized, the appetite to throw out thousands of
years of characters faded, and Pinyin got quietly reclassified as a helper — a way to
teach pronunciation and sound out characters, not to replace them.
[The characters RESTORE (all five back). On "reclassified as a helper": the pinyin returns small,
tucked UNDER each character (a furigana wall, glowing turquoise) — exactly where it started, now its
official helper role.]

--- THE IRONY (≈1:02–1:22) ---

And here's the twist. Pinyin didn't kill the characters. It saved them.
[Depth transition → swoosh. A clean phone mock-up slides up centre-frame (CSS/SVG, white +
turquoise), empty chat/text field on top, a pinyin keyboard below.]
Because today, how do you type Chinese on a phone or a keyboard? You type the
Pinyin — and pick the character from a list.
[The IME in action: keys tap out "n-i-h-a-o" (tiny ticks), a candidate row pops up
你 / 您 / 你好 …, a finger taps 你好, and 你好 lands in the text field. Satisfying, real mechanic.]
The system built to replace the characters is now the main reason they now thrive in the
digital age.
[Pay off the bridge: the same letter-bridge re-forms, but now the figure walks ACROSS it the
other way and ARRIVES at the glowing 汉字 ledge — the path away has become the path back.]
