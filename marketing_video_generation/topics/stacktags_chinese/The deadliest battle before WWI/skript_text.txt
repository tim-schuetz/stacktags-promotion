Title: China's deadliest day before WWI: one general, 400,000 prisoners, 240 survivors
Topic: China / history

==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
The Battle of Changping (260 BCE), Qin vs Zhao. After Zhao's army surrendered, the Qin
general Bai Qi reportedly had ~400,000 prisoners executed in a single mass killing,
sparing only ~240 of the youngest to carry the terror home. Among the highest single-
event death tolls before WWI. In 1995 a farmer near Gaoping uncovered the mass graves.
Punchline: one of history's deadliest days happened over 2,000 years ago — and was dug
up by a farmer.

NOTE (honesty): the ~400,000 figure comes from Sima Qian and may be exaggerated — say
"reportedly / according to the records."

SCRIPT
==================================================================

--- HOOK (0:00–0:16) — the payoff up front ---

Over 2,000 years ago, in a single act, a Chinese general is said to have killed around
four hundred thousand surrendered soldiers — sparing just two hundred and forty. It may
be one of the deadliest days in all of human history before World War One. And it was
only confirmed when a farmer dug up the bones.

--- THE BATTLE (≈0:16–0:46) ---

This was the Battle of Changping, in 260 BC — a clash between two great warring states,
Qin and Zhao, for control of China's future. After a brutal, months-long standoff, the
Zhao army was cut off, starved, and finally forced to surrender. Hundreds of thousands
of soldiers laid down their weapons, expecting to be taken prisoner. The Qin commander
was Bai Qi — the most feared general of the age, a man who reportedly never lost a
battle.

--- THE MASSACRE (≈0:46–1:12) ---

And Bai Qi made a cold calculation. Feeding and guarding that many prisoners was a
burden. Releasing them just meant fighting them again later. So, according to the
ancient records, he had almost the entire surrendered army killed — around four hundred
thousand men, in one massive execution. He's said to have spared only the youngest —
about two hundred and forty boys — and sent them home, alive, for one reason: so they
would spread the terror of what Qin did to those who resisted. It worked. The
psychological shock helped pave Qin's road to conquering all of China.

--- THE BONES (≈1:12–1:30) ---

For centuries, the scale of it sounded almost too horrific to be real — maybe an
exaggeration. Then, in 1995, a farmer near the old battlefield was working his land and
turned up something chilling: pits full of human bones, layers of them, bearing marks
of violent death. The massacre, written down two thousand years earlier, was suddenly
very real, lying right under the fields.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:30–1:44) ---

We picture the worst slaughters of history as modern, mechanized things. But one of the
deadliest single days ever recorded happened in an age of bronze and chariots — and
slept under a farmer's field for two thousand years.
