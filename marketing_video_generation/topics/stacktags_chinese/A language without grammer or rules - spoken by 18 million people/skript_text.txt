Title: A language without grammer or rules - spoken by 18 million people

==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
The Zhuang (壮族), China's LARGEST ethnic minority (~18 million), have a traditional
script called Sawndip ("immature characters") — Chinese-character-like glyphs used to
write Zhuang. It was never officially standardized, so the SAME word can be written many
different ways (a dozen+ "spellings"). A Latin Zhuang alphabet was created in the 1950s,
but Sawndip persists informally.
Punchline: a 1,000-year-old script that never agreed on how to spell anything.

==================================================================
SCRIPT
==================================================================

--- HOOK (0:00–0:14) — the payoff up front ---

China's biggest ethnic minority has its own writing system that's over a thousand years
old — and in it, a single word might have a dozen different "spellings." Because nobody
ever standardized it. Ever.

--- WHO THEY ARE (≈0:14–0:36) ---

These are the Zhuang — and "minority" is a funny word for them, because there are around
eighteen million of them. They're the largest ethnic minority in all of China, mostly in
the south. And to write their own language, they didn't use plain Chinese characters.
They built their own script, called Sawndip — which translates, fittingly, to something
like "immature characters."

--- HOW IT WORKS (≈0:36–1:06) ---

Sawndip looks a lot like Chinese — it's made of similar strokes and even borrows and
recombines Chinese characters. But it was always a folk script, used by ordinary people,
priests, and singers, passed down informally. And it was never run through a central
authority that declared, "this is the one correct way to write this word." So it grew up
wild. One village might write a word one way; the next village, another. Over centuries,
the same single word piled up many competing forms — easily a dozen or more accepted
"spellings," all floating around at once. Imagine if English had no dictionary and every
town spelled "night" differently, forever. That's Sawndip.

--- THE MODERN TWIST (≈1:06–1:24) ---

In the 1950s, the government tried to fix the chaos by inventing a brand-new Zhuang
writing system — but using the Latin alphabet, letters instead of characters. It's
official now. But the old Sawndip never fully died; people still use it, in all its
messy, un-standardized glory.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:24–1:36) ---

So China's largest minority has carried a writing system for a thousand years that never
once settled on how to spell its own words. Living proof that not every script needs a
rulebook to survive — it just needs people who keep writing.
