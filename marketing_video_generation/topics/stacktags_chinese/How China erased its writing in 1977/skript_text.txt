THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
The "second round" of simplification (二简字) was made official in 1977 — this time
with genuinely newly invented forms (not just adopted handwriting shortcuts). It was
confusing/chaotic, widely rejected, and officially revoked on 24 June 1986. Writing as
reversible politics. Learn: 二简字.
Punchline: even a writing system can be edited by decree — and un-edited when it goes
too far.

NOTE (overlap): this zooms specifically into the 1977 二简字 round; the broader

NOTE (accuracy): Chinese has no "alphabet" — say "characters / writing," not alphabet.

SCRIPT
==================================================================

--- HOOK (0:00–0:14) — the payoff up front ---

In 1977, the Chinese government simplified its writing again — rolling out a whole set
of brand-new characters almost overnight. It was such a mess that nine years later,
they officially deleted the deletion, and took it all back.

--- THE BACKGROUND (≈0:14–0:36) ---

Decades earlier, China had already simplified its characters once — cutting strokes to
boost literacy. That first round mostly worked, and it's the Simplified Chinese the
mainland still uses today. So in 1977, they decided to do it again — a second round,
called 二简字.

--- WHAT WENT WRONG (≈0:36–1:06) ---

But this time was different. The first round had mostly just formalized shortcuts
people already wrote by hand. The second round invented genuinely new, even more
stripped-down forms — characters that looked unfamiliar, crude, and confusingly alike.
Words started losing the little visual clues that told them apart. And it was pushed
into newspapers, signs, and schoolbooks fast — so it caused chaos. People couldn't
read it comfortably. It clashed with everything already printed. Even names on
official documents got tangled. It felt less like progress, and more like someone had
garbled the language.

--- THE REVERSAL (≈1:06–1:28) ---

The backlash was so strong that the reform was suspended — and then, on June 24th,
1986, officially scrapped, wiped from the books. A rare moment of a government
formally undoing its own change to the written language. Which reveals something
unusual about Chinese: for most languages, you can't just decree a change to the
writing — it evolves on its own. China showed that writing could be government policy
— rolled out, and rolled back, like any other law.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:28–1:42) ---

So for about nine years, China had a second, official set of simplified characters —
and then made them vanish. Proof that even something as deep as a writing system can
be edited by decree... and un-edited, when it goes too far.
