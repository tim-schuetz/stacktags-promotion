VIDEO SCRIPT
Title: China invented the gun — so why did Europe out-gun it?
Topic: China / history
Target length: ~100 seconds (fits the 30s–3min window)
Language of narration: English
Format: Spoken voiceover narration only — no on-screen/visual cues.

==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
China invented gunpowder and early firearms/cannons, yet by the age of European
colonialism Europe's guns were far superior. Leading explanation: constant, existential
inter-state war in Europe drove a relentless firearms arms race (the "military
revolution"), while China's long periods of relative unity/stability reduced that
pressure — plus later Qing conservatism. Less competition → slower innovation.
Punchline: necessity, not genius, drives weapons — and divided, war-torn Europe simply
needed better guns more.

NOTE (honesty): this is a debated historiographical question; present the leading
"competition/constant-war" explanation and flag that scholars disagree.

SCRIPT
==================================================================

--- HOOK (0:00–0:16) — the payoff up front ---

China invented gunpowder. China built some of the first guns and cannons. So how did
Europe end up centuries ahead — showing up in Asia with vastly better firearms, and
colonizing much of it? The answer isn't that Europe was smarter. It's that Europe was
more desperate.

--- CHINA'S HEAD START (≈0:16–0:40) ---

Let's be clear about the head start. Chinese alchemists discovered gunpowder over a
thousand years ago — chasing, of all things, a potion for immortality. From there,
China developed fire arrows, bombs, primitive guns, and cannons, and used them in war
for centuries. For a long time, China was the most advanced gunpowder power on Earth.
And then... the pace of improvement slowed.

--- WHY EUROPE PULLED AHEAD (≈0:40–1:14) ---

Now here's the leading explanation — and historians still argue about it. Think about
what Europe was for most of those centuries: a chaotic patchwork of small kingdoms,
constantly at war with each other. Survival was never guaranteed. If your cannon was
worse than your neighbor's, you might literally cease to exist. That brutal,
never-ending competition forced a relentless arms race. Every decade, someone built a
better gun, a better fortress, a better cannon — just to survive the next war. China, by
contrast, spent long stretches as a single, unified empire — vast, stable, and without
a rival of equal power on its borders pushing it. When you're the dominant giant and
mostly at peace, there's far less pressure to keep frantically reinventing your weapons.
The technology plateaued, while Europe's, forged in constant warfare, kept racing ahead.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:14–1:32) ---

So by the time European ships reached China's coast, the inventor of the gun had been
out-gunned by its own creation — not because Europe was cleverer, but because divided,
war-torn Europe simply needed better guns more. Necessity, not genius, drives the
deadliest progress.
