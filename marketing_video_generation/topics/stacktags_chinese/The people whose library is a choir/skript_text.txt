VIDEO SCRIPT
Title: This people has no writing — so their library is a choir of thousands of songs
Topic: China / culture
Target length: ~90 seconds (fits the 30s–3min window)
Language of narration: English
Format: Spoken voiceover narration only — no on-screen/visual cues.

==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
The Dong (侗族) had no traditional writing, so they encoded history, law, and
knowledge into SONG. Their "Grand Song" (侗族大歌, UNESCO heritage) is sung by a large
choir with NO conductor and NO instruments. The more songs you know, the more
"educated" and respected you are.
Punchline: a civilization whose entire library is held in voices, not books.

==================================================================
SCRIPT
==================================================================

--- HOOK (0:00–0:14) — the payoff up front ---

Imagine a people with no written language at all — no books, no records. So how do
they remember their history, their laws, everything they know? They sing it. Their
entire library is a choir.

--- WHO THEY ARE (≈0:14–0:34) ---

These are the Dong, who live in the green, terraced hills of southern China. For most
of their history, they never developed a script. And yet they held onto centuries of
knowledge — their origins, their rules, their farming wisdom, their love stories. The
trick was simple, and beautiful: they put all of it into song.

--- KNOWLEDGE AS MUSIC (≈0:34–1:04) ---

Among the Dong, a song isn't just entertainment — it's a storage medium. History
becomes a ballad. A law becomes a verse. Advice for farming or marriage becomes a
melody you can't forget. And the masterpiece of it all is the Grand Song. It's choral
music sung by a whole village at once — many voices, layered into rich harmony. But
here's the astonishing part: there's no conductor keeping time, and no instruments at
all. The singers cue each other, weaving multiple melodies together purely by ear —
some even imitating the sounds of cicadas and birds. It's so remarkable that UNESCO
recognized it as a treasure of human heritage.

--- THE TWIST (≈1:04–1:22) ---

And because the songs are the knowledge, knowing them is being educated. Among the
Dong, the more songs you can sing, the wiser and more respected you are. A person rich
in songs is, quite literally, a person rich in learning. Your memory is your degree.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:22–1:34) ---

We keep our knowledge in books and hard drives, and forget how to hold it ourselves.
The Dong kept theirs alive the oldest way there is — in voices, passed mouth to mouth,
a living library that has to be sung to survive.
