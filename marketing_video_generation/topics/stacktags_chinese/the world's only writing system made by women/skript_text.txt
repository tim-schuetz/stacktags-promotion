==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
Nüshu (女书, "women's script"), from Jiangyong, Hunan — the only known writing system
created and used exclusively by women, who were shut out of the formal schooling that
taught Hanzi. Crucially, it's PHONETIC / syllabic (~600–700 symbols, each = a
syllable), unlike the logographic Hanzi men used. Used for songs, letters between
"sworn sisters," and bridal "third-day" booklets. Learn: 女书; logographic vs phonetic;
writing as power.
Punchline: when people build a script from the outside, they build it to be shared.

NOTE (honesty): "forbidden to read" is a dramatization — women were largely EXCLUDED
from formal education, not always formally banned. Soften to "shut out / denied
access."

SCRIPT
==================================================================

--- HOOK (0:00–0:14) — the payoff up front ---

Shut out of the schools that taught Chinese characters, women in one corner of China
did something extraordinary: they invented their own secret writing. And it worked in
a completely different way from the men's.

--- THE EXCLUSION (≈0:14–0:36) ---

For most of imperial China, formal education — and the thousands of prestige
characters that came with it — was largely a man's world. Most women were simply never
taught to read or write the official script. They were locked out of the page.

--- THE INVENTION (≈0:36–1:00) ---

But in one rural county in Hunan province, women created a workaround: their own
script, known as Nüshu — literally, "women's writing." As far as we know, it's the
only writing system in history made by women, for women. Most men didn't even know it
existed. They wrote it in delicate, slanted strokes, often disguised as decoration on
fans and cloth.

--- THE KEY DIFFERENCE (≈1:00–1:28) ---

And here's the fascinating part. The men's characters — Hanzi — are logographic: each
symbol stands for a meaning, which is exactly why there are thousands to memorize. But
Nüshu went a completely different route. It's phonetic — each of its few hundred
symbols stands for a sound, a syllable. Around six or seven hundred symbols, and you
can write anything you can say. And that's no accident. When the people building a
script are shut out of the elite system, they tend to invent something simpler —
sound-based, and quick to learn. Denied the complex characters, women built an
accessible one.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:28–1:44) ---

Writing has always been about power — who gets to read, who gets to record their own
life. Denied the official script, the women of Hunan didn't go silent. They wrote
their own — and proved that when you build a language from the outside, you build it
to be shared.
