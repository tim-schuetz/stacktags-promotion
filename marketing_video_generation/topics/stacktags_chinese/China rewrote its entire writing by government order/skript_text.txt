Title options (pick one): China rewrote its entire writing by government order
Topic: Learn Chinese
Target length: ~100 seconds (fits the 30s–3min window)
Language of narration: English
Format: Spoken voiceover narration + [bracketed] visual cues. The brackets are NOT spoken —
they are stripped before TTS; the spoken text is exactly the un-bracketed words.

==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
To boost literacy, the PRC simplified Chinese characters (1956): fewer strokes, some merged.
"Simplified Chinese" (简体) stuck on the mainland; Taiwan/Hong Kong kept Traditional (繁体).
A radical SECOND round (二简字, 1977) went too far, was confusing and unpopular, and was
officially scrapped in 1986 — a rare reversal of a language reform.
Punchline: you can redesign a language top-down — but only as far as the people will allow.

NOTE (honesty): keep neutral, non-political.

VISUAL SPINE: instead of many disjoint single-character scenes, ONE consistent TABLE carries the
whole video and FILLS UP over time. Columns: [object image] · 繁体 (traditional) · 1956 简体 ·
1977 二简. Rows are everyday characters with an image of the thing in the left column. The 1956
column fills first (round one: 馬→马, 門→门 — and it stuck), then the 1977 column fills (round
two: 蛋→旦, 餐→歺 — too far), then the 1977 column is STRUCK OUT and removed at "scrapped".
The ending leaves two columns: 繁体 (Taiwan/Hong Kong) vs 简体 (mainland). A real LITERACY CHART
(verified data: ~20% in 1950 → ~66% in 1982 → ~91% in 2000) rises for "literacy climbed".
On-screen text only when it ADDS info — the chars themselves + 简体/繁体 + tiny year stamps.
Grey verbatim subtitles, key word turquoise. White grid bg, white + turquoise; red only as a
marker (map pin / strike / seal). Swoosh on grid moves; pop on a char/word; ticking on counts.
LINGUISTIC NOTE: the two reforms mostly targeted DIFFERENT characters (the same glyph rarely
changed twice, and the few that did use unrenderable rare Unicode) — so the table shows real
forms: round one changed some rows, round two changed others, both authentic.
Verified: 二简字 promulgated 1977-12-20, rescinded 1986-06-24 by the State Council; real
second-round forms 蛋→旦, 餐→歺.

SCRIPT

--- HOOK (0:00–0:10) — a question, answered ---

Is it actually possible to rewrite an entire language — by government order?
[Big, bold on the white grid: the question sets up. A single intricate traditional character
龍 (lóng, dragon) stands centre, fully drawn — the "language" about to be rewritten.]
Yes. It is. And yes — it can fail.
[On "Yes. It is.": 龍 snaps to the simplified 龙 (a clean transform). On "it can fail": a
skeletal over-simplified stub flickers in and is instantly struck through.]
China is proof of both.
[Slow push-in (grid converges, swoosh). The two outcomes — kept vs cancelled — implied.]

--- WHY (≈0:10–0:30) ---

In the mid-1900s, most people in China couldn't read.
[The recurring archival photo of the 1950s literacy drive (generated period image) sits low;
above it looms a dense wall of traditional characters — the people dwarfed by a writing they
can't read. The barrier IS the writing.]
The characters were beautiful — but brutally complex, often dozens of strokes each.
[Dive into ONE busy character (鬱, yù); a stroke-counter ticks up to its true count (~29),
ticking SFX — that's the wall they had to climb.]
So to lift literacy, the government did something almost no country ever does:
it simplified the writing itself — by decree.
[A turquoise official seal presses down from above = "by decree". Transition toward the table.]

--- ROUND ONE: IT WORKED (≈0:30–0:52) ---

Take a few everyday characters.
[THE TABLE builds: left column shows object images (a horse, a gate, an egg, a bowl); the next
column writes their traditional forms 馬 / 門 / 蛋 / 餐. Clean, consistent layout — this table
stays for the rest of the video.]
In the 1950s, the first reform cut them down — fewer strokes, faster to write.
[The 1956 简体 column fills in: 馬→马 and 門→门 visibly shed strokes (write-on, turquoise);
the egg and bowl carry over unchanged. Tiny "1956" stamp.]
And it worked.
[A green tick by the 简体 column; the table settles.]

Literacy climbed —
[Cut to a real LITERACY CHART: a turquoise line rises left→right over the years (the 1950s
literacy-drive photo subtly fills the area under the curve, like the thumbnail). Real points:
~20% (1950) → ~66% (1982) → ~91% (2000). Counter ticks up; the line draws on. Grounded, not slop.]
from around one in five adults who could read, to most of the country.
[The line lands near the top-right with an arrowhead; "91%" pops.]

--- ROUND TWO: TOO FAR (≈0:52–1:18) ---

So in 1977, they pushed their luck — a second round, simplifying even further.
[Back to the TABLE. The 1977 二简 column fills in: the egg 蛋→旦 and the bowl 餐→歺 collapse to
skeletal stubs (the famous scrapped forms). Tiny "1977" stamp.]
But this time, they went too far.
[The new stubs look almost nothing like the originals — visibly stripped.]
The new forms were stripped down so much they lost their meaning — confusing, and unpopular.
[Clarity collapse: 旦 already means "dawn", 歺 is a bare stub — a baffled "?!" reaction; the
object images no longer match their over-simplified glyph.]
People just refused to use them.
[Hands/marks shove the 1977 column; it shakes.]

So in 1986, the government did something rare:
it admitted the reform had failed, and scrapped the entire second round.
[A red strike crosses the whole 1977 二简 column and it crumbles away / is removed. Tiny "1986"
stamp. The table is left with 繁体 + 1956 简体 only.]

--- ENDING: TWO SYSTEMS (≈1:18–1:38) ---

Today, that first simplification is what the mainland writes — 简体, "simplified".
[The 简体 column highlights turquoise; label 简体 / "Mainland". The real china-map.js can flash
the mainland here.]
But Taiwan and Hong Kong never adopted it — they kept the original 繁体, "traditional".
[The 繁体 column highlights; label 繁体 / "Taiwan · Hong Kong" (small red pins on the map).]
One language... two ways to write it —
[The table holds both surviving columns side by side: 繁体 vs 简体.]
redesigned from the top down, but only as far as the people would allow.
[Callback: the literacy-drive people return briefly — the ones who set the limit.]
