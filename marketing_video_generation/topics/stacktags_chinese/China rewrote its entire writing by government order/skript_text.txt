VIDEO SCRIPT
Title options (pick one): China rewrote its entire writing by government order
Topic: Learn Chinese
Target length: ~95 seconds (fits the 30s–3min window)
Language of narration: English
Format: Spoken voiceover narration + [bracketed] visual cues. The brackets are NOT spoken —
they are stripped before TTS; the spoken text is exactly the un-bracketed words.

==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
To boost literacy, the PRC simplified Chinese characters (1950s–60s): fewer strokes,
some characters merged. "Simplified Chinese" stuck (mainland), while Taiwan/Hong Kong
kept Traditional. A radical SECOND round (二简字, 1977) went too far, was confusing
and unpopular, and was officially scrapped in 1986 — a rare reversal of a language
reform.
Punchline: you can redesign a language top-down — but only up to the point where the
people writing it push back.

NOTE (honesty): keep neutral, non-political. Frame as literacy-driven simplification
+ the failed/reverted 1977 reform; note merging blurred some once-distinct characters.

VISUAL SPINE (anti-slop): ONE recurring hero "workshop" — a single 田字格 practice cell
centre-stage carries the whole video. Real characters are written into it from REAL
makemeahanzi stroke data (stroke-by-stroke, 100% authentic) and are reshaped BY DECREE:
they shed strokes (round one works), then shed too many (round two breaks), then get
reverted. Same cell, same layout throughout = consistency, not a new scene per sentence.
Visual rhyme: a faint TOP-DOWN turquoise seal = "by government order". A recurring HUMAN
thread runs underneath the glyph spine — a real archival photo of the 1950s literacy drive
(generated period image, not a drawn figure): the people are first dwarfed by the wall of
writing (can't read), then can read it once it's simplified, and at the very end THEY set the
limit. The mass reform itself is shown as the recreated official 1956 scheme document.
On-screen text only when it ADDS info — the only "words" are
the Chinese characters themselves (the viewer actually learns: 愛→爱 lost its heart, 彩→采
collided) + tiny year stamps (1956 / 1977 / 1986). Grey verbatim subtitles, key word turquoise.
White grid bg, white + turquoise only; red only as a map pin. Swoosh SFX only when the grid
moves / a default element animates; pop on a character/word; ticking on the stroke counter.
Verified facts baked in: 二简字 promulgated 1977-12-20, rescinded 1986-06-24 by the State
Council; real second-round forms 蛋→旦, 餐→歺, and the clarity-killing merger 彩→采 (color → pick).

SCRIPT

--- HOOK (0:00–0:14) — the payoff up front ---

[HERO 田字格 cell, centred on the white grid. A genuinely intricate traditional character
龍 (lóng, "dragon", 16 strokes) writes itself ON, stroke by stroke (no figure — pure glyph).
Bottom subtitle only.]
In the 20th century, China did something almost no country ever does: it rewrote its
own writing — by government order.
[On "by government order": a faint TOP-DOWN hand/seal presses in from above; the dragon's
strokes crumble and fall away, snapping to the simplified 龙 (5 strokes). Grid jolts → swoosh.
A tiny turquoise "1956" stamp ghosts in by the cell.]
The first time, it stuck.
[龙 settles solid; a small turquoise tick.]
The second time, it was such a disaster they had to cancel it.
[A skeletal, over-simplified ghost glyph flickers into the cell and is instantly struck
through and crumbles to nothing.]
Here's the story of how you redesign a language — and how it can backfire.
[Slow camera push-in: grid converges (zoom-in), cell recedes a touch. Swoosh.]

--- WHY (≈0:14–0:38) ---

Back in the mid-1900s, most people in China couldn't read.
[Faux-3D depth move (grid moves → swoosh). A dense, intimidating WALL of traditional
characters looms across the top; BELOW it, a real archival photo of the 1950s mass literacy
drive (people learning to read) — the people dwarfed by a wall of writing they can't read.
The barrier IS the writing. (generated period photo, framed; the recurring "the people" motif)]
The characters were beautiful — but complex, often with dozens of strokes each.
[Camera dives into ONE busy character in the cell (鬱, yù) and it draws on stroke by stroke
while a small stroke-counter ticks UP to its true count (~29) — ticking SFX.]
So to lift literacy, the government decided to simplify the writing itself — officially
cutting strokes and streamlining thousands of characters.
[HERO example 愛 → 爱 in the cell: a turquoise 心 ("heart") marker lifts out and fades as the
strokes simplify — love literally loses its heart. Then for "thousands", cut to the recreated
official 1956 scheme document (《汉字简化方案》, State Council): rows of 繁→简 pairs reveal and a
red 印章 seal stamps it = the mass act, by decree (not a literal char cascade).]

--- ROUND ONE: IT WORKED (≈0:38–1:00) ---

That first simplification, through the 1950s and 60s, largely worked. Characters got
faster to write,
[The 1956 scheme document holds and its red seal stamps "adopted" — the few-stroke 简 forms
ARE the proof it became faster to write.]
literacy climbed —
[Callback to the WHY scene: the SAME wall of writing, now SIMPLIFIED and turquoise (legible),
and the literacy-drive photo BRIGHTENS — the barrier lowered, the people can finally read.]
and "Simplified Chinese" is still what the mainland uses today, while Taiwan and Hong
Kong kept the original, traditional forms.
[Reusable china-map.js, true-scale: the MAINLAND fills turquoise showing 国 (simplified);
small red pins on Taiwan + Hong Kong flip to 國 (traditional) beside them. Real geography.]
So far, a rare success in engineering a language. Then they pushed their luck.
[Ominous beat: map recedes, the cell re-centres, the top-down hand returns from above. Swoosh.]

--- ROUND TWO: TOO FAR (≈1:00–1:25) ---

In 1977 came a second round, simplifying characters even further.
[The cell character is simplified AGAIN to a REAL 二简字 form (蛋→旦, then 餐→歺) — now skeletal,
stripped to a stub. Tiny turquoise "1977" stamp.]
But this one went too far. The new forms were confusing, stripped of so much that they
lost their clarity —
[Clarity collapse made literal: show two DIFFERENT words — 彩 (cǎi, "colour") and the existing
采 (cǎi, "pick") — then the second round deletes the 彡 from 彩, and now BOTH are 采. Two
meanings, one identical shape. A second small figure looks between them, baffled ("?" bubble).]
and people hated them.
[Figures shove the skeletal glyph away / cross it out.]
It was so unpopular that in 1986, the government officially scrapped the entire second
round.
[The top-down hand returns but REVERSES: a turquoise ✗ strikes the 二简字 forms and they snap
back to the round-one forms (旦→蛋, 歺→餐). "scrapped" = crumple/delete. Tiny "1986" stamp. Swoosh.]
A rare, public admission that a language reform had simply failed.
[The cell holds the restored, readable simplified form, steady.]

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:25–1:38) ---

Most languages drift and evolve slowly, on their own.
[A character in the cell morphs gently, organically along a slow drifting timeline — no hand,
no jolt. Calm.]
China engineered its writing by decree — proving you really can redesign a language
from the top down...
[The top-down hand/seal returns and firmly reshapes the glyph from above (clean, deliberate).]
but only up to the point where the people writing it push back.
[Payoff of the human thread: the SAME literacy-drive photo (the people) returns beneath the
glyph, and the over-simplified form springs back to the readable form — the people who were
given literacy are the ones who set the limit. Decree from above vs. the people below, resolved.]

Wanna actually start learning Chinese?
Discover thousands of free exercises and more learning content on stacktags.io.
[Default OUTRO element: stacked-cube logo between turquoise chevrons, "stacktags" wordmark,
grey "stacktags.io" line. Assemble swoosh.]
