==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
The chengyu 围魏救赵 ("besiege Wei to rescue Zhao"): instead of marching to relieve the
besieged ally directly, Sun Bin's Qi attacked the ATTACKER's undefended capital,
forcing the enemy army to rush home — then ambushed it. The idiom now means: don't hit
the strong point; strike the weak one that forces your enemy to react. Bonus: how
4-character idioms (chengyu) are used in everyday Chinese.
Punchline: the best way to break a siege can be to start one somewhere else.

SCRIPT

--- HOOK (0:00–0:14) — the payoff up front ---

Your ally's city is under siege. The obvious move is to march your army there and fight
the attackers. The genius move, in ancient China, was the opposite: ignore that city
completely — and attack the enemy's home instead.

--- THE STORY (≈0:14–0:48) ---

This comes from a real campaign, over 2,300 years ago. The powerful state of Wei was
besieging the capital of Zhao, and Zhao begged its ally, Qi, for help. Qi's strategist
was the brilliant Sun Bin. Now, marching straight to the besieged city meant fighting
Wei's main army, at full strength, on ground it had chosen. A bloody, uncertain fight.
So Sun Bin didn't do that. He looked at the map and realized: while Wei's army was off
besieging Zhao, Wei's own capital was sitting almost undefended. So he attacked that
instead. Suddenly the Wei generals got the worst possible news: their home was under
threat. They had no choice — they abandoned the siege of Zhao and raced back to save
their own capital. And Sun Bin was waiting. He ambushed their exhausted, hurrying army
on the road and crushed it. He rescued Zhao without ever going to Zhao.

--- THE IDIOM (≈0:48–1:14) ---

That move became a four-character idiom: 围魏救赵 — "besiege Wei to rescue Zhao." And
this is the beautiful thing about Chinese. These idioms — called chengyu — are tiny,
four-character capsules that each pack an entire story. A Chinese speaker can drop
"围魏救赵" into a normal conversation, and instantly everyone understands the whole idea:
don't attack the strong point head-on — hit the weak spot that forces your opponent to
react. It's used for business, sports, arguments — anywhere you outsmart someone
sideways. The language is full of them: four little characters, one ancient story, a
lesson everyone shares.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:14–1:30) ---

So sometimes the best way to defend something is to threaten something else entirely. A
2,300-year-old battle, compressed into four characters — still quietly running through
everyday Chinese, every single day.
