VIDEO SCRIPT
Title options (pick one): Why China has a baby boom every 12 years
Topic: Learn Chinese
Target length: ~90 seconds (fits the 30s–3min window)
Language of narration: English
Format: Spoken voiceover narration + [bracketed] on-screen/visual storyboard cues.
        The [brackets] are storyboard directions for the build — they are stripped
        before TTS, so only the plain narration is ever spoken.

==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
The Dragon is the most auspicious Chinese zodiac sign, so parents time births for
Dragon years → measurable birth-rate bumps every 12 years. China's last full Dragon
year (2012) saw a clear rise (reportedly the most births in over a decade); the
effect is even sharper in Taiwan, Hong Kong, Singapore. A superstition with a real,
visible demographic fingerprint.
Punchline: belief literally bends the birth rate of the most populous region on
Earth — every twelve years, like clockwork.

NOTE (honesty): hedge the China figure ("reportedly"); the effect is CLEAREST in
Taiwan/Singapore/HK data. Dragon years: …2000, 2012, 2024.

==================================================================
VISUAL SPINE (hero device)
==================================================================
The whole video is carried by ONE persistent hero device: a ZODIAC WHEEL that
behaves like a clock — a ring of the 12 zodiac hanzi (鼠牛虎兔龙蛇马羊猴鸡狗猪).
It ticks "like clockwork", and every 12 notches it lands on the Dragon 龙, which
triggers the birth pulse. White/turquoise brand system only; no Gemini photos; the
single real-world artefact is one recreated 2012 news headline (built in HTML).
On-screen text is kept minimal — only the Chinese vocab payload (龙 / 生肖 / 龙宝宝
/ 龙年) plus the news card. Subtitles mirror the narration verbatim.

==================================================================
SCRIPT
==================================================================

--- HOOK (0:00–0:12) — the payoff up front ---

[Open on a horizontal year-ticker scrolling across the grid. As it passes 2000, 2012, 2024 (dragon years), a short burst of small turquoise cradle/baby icons pulses up out of the line — a visible, rhythmic beat. The repeating pulse IS the hook; no chart, no title card.]
Every 12 years, China — and much of East Asia — has a baby boom.
[A coin / small ▲up-chart icon slides in from the side, then gets struck through and greyed out.]
Not because of the economy.
[A government policy-document / red stamp icon slides in, then gets struck through and greyed out.]
Not because of policy.
[Both crossed-out icons drop away; the 12-hanzi ZODIAC WHEEL spins into the centre (swoosh as the grid pulls in).]
Because of the zodiac.
[The wheel rotates and locks onto 龙 at the top; 龙 glows turquoise and scales up. Small label appears: 龙年 lóng nián · "Year of the Dragon".]
Parents want their child born in the Year of the Dragon.

--- THE BELIEF (≈0:12–0:35) ---

[Camera settles on the full wheel. The 12 zodiac hanzi pop in one-by-one around the ring (soft pop per glyph). Centre label: 生肖 shēngxiào · zodiac.]
The Chinese zodiac runs on twelve animals, one per year.
[The other 11 hanzi desaturate to grey; only 龙 stays turquoise, lifts out of the ring to centre with a glow. Traits stay in the SUBTITLE only — no text pills on screen.]
And the Dragon is the most prized of them all — tied to strength, luck, ambition, success.
[Vocab payload: 龙 grows a small swaddled-baby mark beside it; label morphs to 龙宝宝 lóng bǎobao · "dragon baby".]
A "dragon child" is seen as destined for great things.
[Two cartoon parent figures (default figure element) slide in and point up at the 龙 slot on the wheel — a light, human beat.]
So couples actually plan around it — trying to time a birth for a dragon year.

--- THE DATA (≈0:35–1:02) ---

[Transition to a births line/area chart that draws itself in across the years (graph-chart element). Faint turquoise vertical bands + tiny 龙 ticks mark 2000 / 2012 / 2024; the line bumps up at each band.]
And here's the wild part: you can see it in the numbers. In dragon years, births rise.
[Zoom to the 2012 band; the bump highlights and a counting headline value ticks up (ticking sound). A recreated, realistic NEWS-HEADLINE card slides in — styled like a real article: "2012: China's dragon-year baby boom — most births in over a decade" (small dateline so it reads as a report, covering the "reportedly" hedge).]
China's last full dragon year, 2012, brought a clear bump — reportedly the most births in over a decade.
[The chart re-draws even sharper for Taiwan / Hong Kong / Singapore: a tall spike at the dragon year, immediately followed by a dip below the baseline the very next year. Small TW · HK · SG chips label it.]
And the effect is even sharper in places like Taiwan, Hong Kong, and Singapore, where dragon years produce a visible spike — followed by a dip the very next year.
[Zoom out to the full multi-decade series; the regular, repeating spikes read as a clean rhythmic pattern. Caption: "every 12 years".]
A superstition, leaving a hard fingerprint on a country's demographics.

--- THE TWIST (≈1:02–1:20) ---

[The 龙宝宝 dragon-baby returns, then multiplies — it duplicates into a crowd of identical dragon-kids that cram into the frame, competing. A quick crowded progression: school desk → university gate → job, each overflowing with the same kids.]
There's even a catch for the dragon kids themselves. Being born in a boom year means a bigger generation — so more competition for school places, university spots, jobs, their whole lives.
[Contrast beat: split feeling — the auspicious 龙 glow on one side vs. the overcrowded class of identical kids all reaching for one single spot on the other.]
A lucky sign... and a very crowded class.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:20–1:32) ---

[Synthesis: the zodiac wheel becomes a literal ticking clock over a faint China silhouette (country-map element). Each time the hand sweeps onto 龙, the birth line below pulses up in sync — belief visibly "bending" the curve, every twelve ticks.]
We tend to think of superstition as harmless. But the wish for a dragon child literally bends the birth rate of the most populous region on Earth — every twelve years, like clockwork.
