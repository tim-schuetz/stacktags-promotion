SCRIPT

--- HOOK (0:00–0:14) — the payoff up front ---

China's richest man didn't get there with tech, or factories, or real estate. He got
there selling the most basic thing on the planet: bottled water. Plain water — turned
into one of the largest fortunes on Earth.

--- THE UNLIKELY BACKGROUND (≈0:14–0:42) ---

His name is Zhong Shanshan, and his path is almost comically ordinary. He dropped out of
school young. He worked construction. He was a newspaper reporter. He even farmed
mushrooms — and failed at a few businesses along the way. He didn't find real success
until his forties. Then he started a bottled water company called Nongfu Spring. And his
big idea wasn't some complex technology. It was branding. He sold the water as natural,
pulled from pristine mountain springs and lakes — with the famous line: "We don't
produce water. We are just porters of nature." Premium image, on a product that
literally falls from the sky.

--- THE SCALE (≈0:42–1:08) ---

Here's why it works so spectacularly. Water costs almost nothing to source. The magic is
volume and margin: sell billions of bottles, each at a small profit, to a country of 1.4
billion thirsty people, every single day. That turns a humble bottle into a money
machine. When Nongfu Spring went public, the stock soared — and Zhong's stake made him,
at times, the richest person in all of Asia. His wealth got an extra boost from another
company he controls — in vaccines and medical testing — which surged during the pandemic.
But the foundation, the thing that made him a household fortune, was water.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:08–1:24) ---

So while the world chases the next big, complicated thing, the richest man in China
quietly proved an old truth: you don't need to invent something new. You can take the
simplest product that exists — water — brand it well, sell it everywhere, and build an
empire out of something people once got for free.
