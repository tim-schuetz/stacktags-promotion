==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
Until ~1920, 他 (tā) covered he/she/it. Translating Western literature created
pressure to mark "she." Poet/linguist Liu Bannong coined and popularized 她 (tā,
"she") around 1920 by swapping the "person" radical 亻 in 他 for the "woman" radical
女. Shows radical-swap as word-building. (It was controversial at the time.) Learn:
她/他; how characters are built from parts.
Punchline: the Chinese word for "she" has an author — and a birth year.

==================================================================

==================================================================

Brand: white + turquoise (#35A292 / #119271 emphasis), Inter, grey subtitles (#8a949d)

text only when it teaches a real word (CJK + pinyin); never echo the English narration.

--- HOOK (0:00–0:12) — the payoff up front ---

[SCENE 1 — the writing cell appears centred on the white grid; 他 writes itself in
stroke by stroke (makeHanzi writeOn). Three small inline-SVG icons fade in around it —
a man, a woman, a cat (the "thing") — each with a thin leader line pointing back to the
SAME single 他.]
Just a hundred years ago, Chinese had no word for "she." A single word covered he,
[On "no word for 'she'": the WOMAN icon's leader line ends in an EMPTY cell with a
blinking turquoise "?" — there is literally no character for her. The man + cat lines
still land on 他.]
she, and it. Then a poet sat down and literally built a new one.
[On "built a new one": a brush nib slides in from the right and just touches the empty
cell (teaser) — then we transition (grid zoom). swoosh on the camera move.]

--- THE OLD WAY (≈0:12–0:36) ---

[SCENE 2 — 他 centred large, pinyin "tā" appears below it (teach the word). The three
icons (man / woman / cat) line up in a row beneath, each connected to the one 他.]
For most of its history, Chinese got by with one pronoun — 他, tā — for he, she, and
it. And spoken, it doesn't matter: tā is tā, no matter who you mean.
In writing, that one character did the whole job. Nobody felt anything was missing.
[On "in writing … did the whole job": the speech bubbles fade, leaving only the written
cell. On "nothing missing": a calm small ✓ next to the cell; it sits content.]

--- THE PRESSURE (≈0:36–0:58) ---

[SCENE 3 — an HTML-recreated open Western novel page slides in (paper card, soft shadow,
real English-looking lines) with "he said" and "she said" highlighted — NOT a generic
book pile.]
Then, in the early twentieth century, Chinese writers started translating a flood of
Western books — full of "he said," "she said."
[On "he said / she said": two arrows leave those phrases — one from "he said", one from
"she said" — and BOTH collide on the same single 他 (teal clash / knot, no red). The
empty "she" cell from the hook returns beside it, still blank.]
And suddenly, that one catch-all pronoun was a problem. To translate faithfully, they
needed a way to tell "he" and "she" apart on the page.
[On "a problem": a small translator figure gives a frustrated shrug at the two-into-one
collision (light humour). On "tell he and she apart": the blank "she" cell pulses —
urgently needed now. Transition to the invention (grid zoom, swoosh).]

--- THE INVENTION (≈0:58–1:24) ---

[SCENE 4 — the centrepiece. Liu Bannong appears as a cropped, dashed-outline period
portrait card on the left when his name is spoken, then slides to the edge so the cell
stays the focus.]
So, around 1920, a poet and linguist named Liu Bannong did something elegant.
[On his name: portrait card pops in (cut-out + dashed outline).]
He took the existing character for "he" — 他 — which is built with a little "person"
piece on the left.
[On "built with a little 'person' piece on the left": 他 splits into two LEGO-style
blocks — the left block 亻 (label "person") highlights teal, the right block 也 stays.]
And he simply swapped that piece out, replacing the "person" part with the character
for "woman," 女. The result was 她 — a brand-new character, meaning "she."
[On "swapped that piece out": the 亻 block lifts OUT and slides away; a 女 block (label
"woman · nǚ") slides into the empty slot and snaps in (pop sfx) → the cell recomposes
into 她. The "she" cell that was blank since the hook is now FILLED with 她.]
And that shows you something about how Chinese actually works. Characters are built
from parts — so you can engineer a whole new word just by changing one component.
Person, plus the rest, equals "he." Woman, plus the rest, equals "she."
[On "Person, plus the rest, equals 'he'": a clean slot-equation builds — 亻 + 也 = 他
(he). On "Woman, plus the rest, equals 'she'": directly below — 女 + 也 = 她 (she); only
the left block differs, 也 ("the rest") is shared and stays put.]
Same sound, new meaning, built like Lego.
[On "same sound": both 他 and 她 show pinyin "tā" — identical — pulse them together. The
blocks click with a subtle stud/puzzle look (pop).]

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:24–1:38) ---

It wasn't loved by everyone — some argued that giving women a separate pronoun, while
men kept the original "default" one, was itself a statement.
[On "default one": brief side-by-side tension — 他 labelled the old "default", 她 the
new one; a small debate spark, kept light, no clutter.]
But it stuck. Today 她 is everywhere.
[On "今天 … everywhere": many small 她 pop across the field / into a wash of modern text.]
So the Chinese word for "she" is younger than the lightbulb —
[On "younger than the lightbulb": a lightbulb icon stamped "1879" next to 她 "1920" — 她
is the younger one (concrete comparison).]
and unlike almost every other word, it has an author.
[On "it has an author": 她 gets a small brush byline "刘半农 · 1920" — like a signature on
the character. The hook's promise (author + birth year) paid off.]
