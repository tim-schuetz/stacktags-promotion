==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
The repeating crossbow (诸葛弩 / Zhuge nu, traditionally linked to Zhuge Liang): a
magazine-fed crossbow that fired bolts in rapid succession. Each bolt was short, weak,
and short-ranged — but the murderous rate of fire (and often poisoned tips) made it
devastating, especially for defense.
Punchline: not power per shot — overwhelming volume. The ancient idea behind the
machine gun.

NOTE (honesty): the attribution to Zhuge Liang is traditional/legendary; repeating
crossbows certainly existed and were used for centuries. Say "traditionally credited."

SCRIPT

--- HOOK (0:00–0:12) — the payoff up front ---

China had something close to a machine gun two thousand years ago. Each shot was almost
pathetically weak — and it still wiped out almost everyone who charged it. Here's the
trick.

--- THE WEAPON (≈0:12–0:38) ---

It's called the repeating crossbow — traditionally credited to the legendary strategist
Zhuge Liang. A normal crossbow is brutal but slow: you fire one bolt, then spend long
seconds hauling the string back to reload. This one solved that. It had a magazine of
bolts mounted on top and a lever you just worked back and forth. Each pump dropped a
new bolt into place and fired it. So instead of one shot every several seconds, you
could send a stream of bolts downrange — ten in the time it took to fire one from an
ordinary bow.

--- THE TRADE-OFF (≈0:38–1:02) ---

But there was a catch. To make it fire that fast, each bolt was small, light, and
launched with much less force. On its own, a single bolt wasn't very powerful and
didn't fly far. A trained archer's arrow would outrange and outhit it easily. So how do
you make a weak weapon deadly? Volume. You don't rely on one perfect shot — you bury the
enemy under a wall of them. And to cover the lack of power, the bolt tips were often
coated in poison, so even a shallow hit could kill. Picture a mass of attackers rushing
a wall, and a blur of poisoned bolts pouring into them, nonstop.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:02–1:16) ---

It's the same logic that would define warfare two thousand years later, with the actual
machine gun: forget the perfect single shot. Win with rate of fire. China just figured
it out with wood, string, and a clever little lever.
