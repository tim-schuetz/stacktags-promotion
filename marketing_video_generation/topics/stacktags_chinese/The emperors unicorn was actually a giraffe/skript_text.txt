==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
In 1414, Zheng He's treasure fleet brought a live giraffe (originally from East Africa,
via Bengal) to the Ming court. The court interpreted it as a qilin (麒麟) — the
auspicious "unicorn" said to appear only under a truly virtuous ruler — and used it as
heavenly proof to legitimize the Yongle Emperor, who had seized the throne by force.
Punchline: a usurper laundered a giraffe into a divine endorsement.

NOTE (honesty): the qilin↔giraffe identification is well documented; the Somali/Swahili
"giri" → qilin link is a plausible folk-etymology, mention lightly if at all.

--- HOOK (0:00–0:14) — the payoff up front ---

In 1414, an admiral sailed a mythical unicorn into the emperor's court — living proof,
everyone agreed, that heaven blessed his reign. There was just one problem. It wasn't a
unicorn. It was a giraffe.

--- THE GIFT (≈0:14–0:38) ---

This was the era of Zheng He, China's great eunuch admiral, whose treasure fleet sailed
as far as Africa. On one voyage, the fleet brought back the most astonishing animal the
Chinese court had ever seen: a towering creature with a impossibly long neck, gentle
eyes, and a strange, deer-like head — a giraffe, originally from East Africa, that had
made its way to the fleet through Bengal. To a court that had never seen one, it looked
like a creature out of legend.

--- THE "QILIN" (≈0:38–1:06) ---

And there was a perfect legend waiting for it. In Chinese myth, there's a sacred beast
called the qilin — often translated as the Chinese unicorn. Gentle, magical, and most
importantly: it was said to appear only when the empire was ruled by a truly wise and
virtuous emperor. A living sign of heaven's approval. So when this long-necked wonder
walked into the palace, the court declared it a qilin. Heaven, they announced, had sent
its blessing.

--- WHY IT MATTERED (≈1:06–1:24) ---

And that mattered enormously — because of who was on the throne. The Yongle Emperor had
taken power by overthrowing his own nephew in a brutal civil war. A usurper. His rule
always carried a whiff of illegitimacy. So a divine unicorn, arriving as proof that
heaven endorsed him? That was political gold. The "qilin" became propaganda — evidence,
on four legs, that he was the rightful Son of Heaven.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:24–1:36) ---

So one of history's great power grabs got blessed by a case of mistaken identity. A
emperor who seized his throne by force — legitimized by a giraffe everyone agreed to
call a unicorn.
