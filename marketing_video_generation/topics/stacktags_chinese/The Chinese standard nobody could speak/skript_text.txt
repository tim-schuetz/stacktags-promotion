THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
The first official Mandarin standard (老国音, "Old National Pronunciation," set
~1913) was a north–south COMPROMISE that fit no real dialect — it kept an extra tone
(the entering tone, 5 tones total) and sounds like v- and ng- that Beijing speech
lacked. Essentially no one spoke the whole system natively. By the late 1920s/1932 it
was scrapped for actual Beijing Mandarin (today's Putonghua). Learn: 老国音; how
standards get constructed.
Punchline: the "fair to everyone" compromise produced a language no human could speak;
in the end they had to pick a real one.

NOTE (honesty): "no human could speak it" = no native speakers of the whole blended
system; teachers learned it from records/charts. Resolve in the body.

SCRIPT
==================================================================

--- HOOK (0:00–0:14) — the payoff up front ---

The first official "standard Chinese" was a language that literally nobody spoke. It
was stitched together from different dialects into a kind of Frankenstein accent that
fit no real place on the map — and eventually, they had to throw it out.

--- THE PROBLEM (≈0:14–0:36) ---

In the early 1900s, China wanted one standard spoken Chinese — a single pronunciation
the whole country could share. But which one? The north spoke very differently from
the south, and nobody wanted to simply hand the crown to one region.

--- THE COMPROMISE (≈0:36–1:04) ---

So in 1913, a commission did something that sounded logical, and turned out
disastrous. They built a compromise. They took some features from the north, some
from the south, and blended them into one official "correct" pronunciation — called
老国音, the Old National Pronunciation. The result fit no actual dialect anywhere. It
had five tones — hanging on to an old "entering tone" that northern Mandarin had
already lost. It used sounds that Beijing speech didn't have. It was, in effect, a
designed accent — a language with no native speakers at all. People had to learn this
"standard" from gramophone records and charts, because there was no city you could
visit to just hear it spoken.

--- THE REVERSAL (≈1:04–1:24) ---

And predictably, it didn't work. You can't ask an entire nation to speak an accent
that doesn't exist. So within about a decade, they gave up on the blended compromise
and re-based the standard on something real: the actual speech of Beijing. And that
is essentially the Mandarin standard — Putonghua — that's used today.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:24–1:38) ---

It's a perfect little lesson in how standards get made. The "fair" compromise — a bit
of everyone, offending no one — produced a language no human could actually speak. In
the end, they had to stop designing, and just pick a real one.
