THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
In Chinese you can't put a number directly on a noun — you need a "measure word"
(classifier): "two beers" → 两瓶啤酒 (two bottles-of beer). There are 150+ measure
words tied to an object's shape/type, but the general one 个 (gè) covers ~94% of
cases. Bonus subtlety: "two" before a measure word is 两 (liǎng), not 二 (èr).
Punchline: in Chinese you must know WHAT KIND of thing you're counting before you can
count it.

SCRIPT

--- HOOK (0:00–0:12) — the payoff up front ---

Try to order "two beers" in Chinese, and you've just made a grammar mistake. You
literally cannot say "two beer." You need a special little word in between — and
Chinese has more than a hundred of them. Here's the rule.

--- THE RULE (≈0:12–0:38) ---

In Chinese, you can't stick a number straight onto a noun. No "two beer," no "three
dog," no "five book." Between the number and the thing, you need a "measure word" —
a classifier. So "two beers" becomes, literally, "two bottles beer" — 两瓶啤酒. That
little 瓶, "bottle," is the measure word. Leave it out, and it's broken Chinese.

--- IT GETS TRICKIER (≈0:38–1:05) ---

And it goes deeper. Even the word "two" is special here. Normally "two" is 二, èr —
but right before a measure word, it changes to a different word, 两, liǎng. So even
counting to two trips people up. And then there's the real mountain: there are over
150 of these measure words — and which one you use depends on the shape or type of
thing. Flat things, long things, animals, books, machines — many have their own.

--- THE SHORTCUT (≈1:05–1:22) ---

But here's the relief. One general measure word — 个, gè — works for the huge
majority of cases. By some counts, around 94% of the time. So if you blank on the
exact one, 个 will usually save you. Not always perfect — but understood.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:22–1:34) ---

In English, "two" just works on everything. In Chinese, you have to know what kind
of thing you're counting before you can count it. Which is why ordering two beers is
secretly a grammar exam.
