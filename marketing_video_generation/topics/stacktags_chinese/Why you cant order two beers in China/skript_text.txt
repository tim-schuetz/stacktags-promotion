==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
In Chinese you can't put a number directly on a noun — you need a "measure word"
(classifier): "two beers" → 两瓶啤酒 (two bottles-of beer). There are 150+ measure
words tied to an object's shape/type, but the general one 个 (gè) covers ~94% of
cases. Bonus subtlety: "two" before a measure word is 两 (liǎng), not 二 (èr).
Punchline: in Chinese you must know WHAT KIND of thing you're counting before you can
count it.

VISUAL CONCEPT — "THE COUNTING GAP" (persistent hero)
==================================================================

The empty measure-word SLOT (□) is the hero. We fill it, multiply it, and re-fill it
through every section. White grid background, turquoise accents, Inter + Noto Sans SC,
grey subtitles with turquoise keywords. Two real cut-out beer bottles (dashed outline)
are the noun 啤酒. Camera = faux-3D grid push/zoom; swoosh only when the grid moves,
pop on tiles/vocab.

--- HOOK (0:00–0:12) — the payoff up front ---

Try to order "two beers" in Chinese, and you've just made a grammar mistake.
[Hard switch to Chinese: the "beers" tile becomes 啤酒 with two real cut-out beer
bottles (dashed turquoise outline) sitting behind it. The number tile "2" lunges toward

SLOT □ that pulses turquoise, demanding a word.]
You literally cannot say "two beer."
[The "2" tries again and bounces off the empty slot; tiny shake.]
You need a special little word in between —
[The empty slot □ glows; a faint stack of many ghost-tiles fans out behind it for a beat

--- THE RULE (≈0:12–0:38) ---

[The equation is centred and steady. Highlight the impossible adjacency: the "2" and
啤酒 lean toward each other across the empty slot, repelling like same-pole magnets.]
In Chinese, you can't stick a number straight onto a noun.
[Three quick attempts pop in a vertical stack to the side, each with the empty slot and

No "two beer," no "three dog," no "five book."
[The stack fades; camera returns to the hero equation. The empty slot □ pulses and a

Between the number and the thing, you need a "measure word" — a classifier.
[瓶 (píng) drops from the top INTO the slot (pop). The slot snaps shut around it; the
"2" relaxes to 两 and the whole line locks: 两 瓶 啤酒. Soft turquoise ✓.]
So "two beers" becomes, literally, "two bottles beer" — 两瓶啤酒.
[Zoom the middle tile: 瓶 enlarges; a vocab chip pops — 瓶 / píng / "bottle (measure
word)". The two beer bottles nod/clink lightly.]
That little 瓶, "bottle," is the measure word.
[Pull 瓶 back OUT of the slot — the slot goes empty again, the line breaks apart and the
bottles dim. "broken Chinese" = grey, lifeless.]
Leave it out, and it's broken Chinese.
[瓶 slots back in; line re-locks 两 瓶 啤酒, bottles brighten.]

And it goes deeper. Even the word "two" is special here.
[Show 二 alone, big, with "èr" — a neutral "normal two".]

[A measure word (瓶) re-appears to the right of the slot. The instant it does, 二 FLIPS

but right before a measure word, it changes to a different word, 两, liǎng.
[A tiny stumble beat: a little figure/number trips over the 二→两 swap (light humor),

So even counting to two trips people up.
[Camera ZOOMS OUT of the single slot (swoosh). The one slot multiplies into a WALL/shelf

and which one you use depends on the shape or type of thing.
[A row of the categories lights one by one as named.]
Flat things, long things, animals, books, machines — many have their own.

Not always perfect — but understood.
