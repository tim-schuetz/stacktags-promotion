==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
"Biáng" — one of the most complex Chinese characters (~58 strokes), used only for
biángbiáng noodles (a Shaanxi dish). So obscure/complex it wasn't in standard
dictionaries and wasn't in Unicode until 2020 (Unicode 13.0) — so for years you
literally couldn't type it. Its origin is folklore (the "biang" = the slap of dough
on the table).
Punchline: a character too complicated for the digital age, invented for a bowl of
noodles.

NOTE (honesty): stroke count is usually cited ~57–58 (varies by counting); origin
is legend, so say "as the story goes." Unicode 13.0 (2020) added it.

PERSISTENT HERO: ONE biáng glyph living inside a single 田字格 practice cell carries the
whole video. The cell stays put on the white grid; only the context around it changes
(stroke counter, component labels, comparison characters, a phone, a noodle bowl). No
new scene per sentence — scenes flow into one another (see anti-slop example 4).
biáng is rendered from the public-domain Wikimedia SVG (no per-stroke data exists), so
the reveal is an INK WRITE-ON (a brush wipe-mask filling the glyph) plus a stroke
counter ticking up — not a true stroke-by-stroke animation.

SCRIPT

--- HOOK (0:00–0:12) — the payoff up front ---

[Open already on the empty 田字格 practice cell, centered on the white grid. On the first
words the biáng glyph BRUSH-WRITES itself in: a soft ink wipe-mask floods the huge glyph
from top-left to bottom-right (ticking sound), while a small counter at the cell's corner
races up 1 → 58. The glyph is so dense it reads like a tiny ink painting. No title text —
the character IS the hook.]
This is one single Chinese character. It has around 58 strokes, it means one
specific kind of noodle —
[On "no computer on Earth could type it": a small phone / chat input field slides up over
the lower grid; we type and the message shows the glyph as a missing-glyph TOFU BOX □
(not the character). The □ blinks once. This is the through-line we pay off at the end.]
and until 2020, no computer on Earth could type it.

--- THE CHARACTER (≈0:12–0:38) ---

[Phone slides away. Camera settles back on the glyph in its cell; the pinyin "biáng"
(with the á tone mark) pops in just under the cell — the one piece of on-screen text that
teaches.]
Meet "biáng."
[Three SIMPLE characters pop in small along the top, each with its tiny stroke count in a
turquoise chip: 大 ·3, 中 ·4, 你 ·7 (pop sound per character). They look calm and sparse
next to the dense biáng below.]
Most Chinese characters have a handful of strokes — five, ten, maybe
fifteen.
[The simple characters shrink/fade; biáng zooms up to fill the cell. The "58" counter
re-emphasizes (gentle pulse). The contrast: a wall of ink vs. the little ones.]
This one crams nearly sixty strokes into a single character. It's so packed
it looks less like writing and more like a tiny painting.
[COMPONENT DECOMPOSITION — the anti-slop core ("show, don't tell"): faint turquoise
highlight boxes sweep across the glyph one by one, each with a small leader label naming
a REAL character hiding inside it — 馬 horse, 心 heart, 月 moon, 言 speak, 長 long, 辶 walk.
The point lands visually: biáng is whole words crammed together. Highlights fade back out,
leaving the full glyph.]
And after all that
complexity, it means... just one thing.

--- WHAT IT'S FOR (≈0:38–1:00) ---

[The biáng glyph slides to the upper third; from the bottom of the frame a REAL, cropped,
dashed-outlined photo of a bowl of thick hand-pulled biángbiáng noodles rises in (bottom-
anchored, swoosh as it enters). Small turquoise label "biángbiáng" by the bowl.]
Biángbiáng noodles — a thick, hand-pulled noodle from the Shaanxi region of China.
That's it.
[Beat: the giant glyph above, the humble single bowl below, joined by a thin dotted
turquoise connector — "all THIS, for THAT." (visual rhyme, like the chopstick video).]
One of the most complicated characters ever, for one bowl of food.
[A dictionary page slides in; a search bar types "biáng" and returns an empty result /
"no entry" — the glyph is conspicuously absent from the ordered list of normal entries.]
It doesn't even appear in standard dictionaries.
[Humor beat: a quick stylized hand slaps a rope of dough onto a wooden table; on the slap
a bold "biáng!" onomatopoeia pops out of the impact (pop sound). Ties the name to the
sound of the dough — the folk origin.]
As the story goes, it was dreamed up
around a noodle stall — the "biang" being the slapping sound of the dough
hitting the table.

--- THE COMPUTER PROBLEM (≈1:00–1:18) ---

[Back to the glyph. On "didn't exist in Unicode": a long scrolling vertical list of code
points / characters streams past (the "master list"), each a real glyph in its slot — but
where biáng should be there's an empty gap / a TOFU BOX □. Small caption chip "Unicode".]
And because it's so rare and so complex, for years it simply didn't exist in
Unicode — the master list of every character computers can display.
[Three quick states cycle in the phone, each showing the □ where biáng should be: a typed
message (□), then a digital menu line "□ noodles  ¥28" (□). Show, don't narrate.]
So you couldn't
type it, text it, or put it on a digital menu.
[A printed paper menu where the dish name is a hand-drawn biáng scribble / a pasted little
image patch taped into the line — the real-world workaround. Slightly janky on purpose.]
Restaurants had to draw it by hand or
paste in a picture.
[On "2020": a "Unicode 13.0 · 2020" rubber-stamp slams down; the TOFU BOX □ in the phone
dissolves and RESOLVES into the real biáng glyph (it finally renders). Subtle relief.]
Only in 2020 did it finally get added — so computers could at
last render a single bowl of noodles.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:18–1:30) ---

[Final composed shot: the full biáng glyph back in its cell, and the little noodle bowl
nested beside/below it — the whole story in one frame. Calm, settled.]
A character too complicated for the digital age — invented for a plate of noodles.
[A neat row of simple emojis lines up beside the towering biáng for one last size gag —
the emojis tiny and tidy, biáng absurdly dense.]
Proof that even in a world of emojis, some words are just too gloriously complex to
type.
