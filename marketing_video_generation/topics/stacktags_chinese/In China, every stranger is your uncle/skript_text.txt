VIDEO SCRIPT
Title options (pick one): In China, every stranger is your uncle
Topic: Learn Chinese
Target length: ~90 seconds (fits the 30s–3min window)
Language of narration: English
Format: Spoken voiceover narration + [bracketed] on-screen storyboard cues.
NOTE: Lines/spans in [brackets] are visual directions only — they are stripped before TTS,
so the spoken narration is every non-bracket line, verbatim.

==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
Chinese has no single word for "uncle"/"aunt" — it specifies paternal vs maternal,
by blood vs by marriage, and relative age (father's older brother, father's younger
brother, mother's brother, the husband of a father's/mother's sister, etc.). YET
Chinese politely addresses strangers with kinship terms (uncle/aunty/big sister). So
"uncle" is untranslatable as one word, but everyone gets a family title.
Punchline: a language incredibly precise about family — that treats the whole world
like family.

==================================================================
VISUAL CONCEPT (persistent hero + two visual rhymes)
==================================================================
Persistent hero object = a turquoise KINSHIP MAP (family tree) that fills in with real
Hanzi+pinyin nodes. Two anti-slop visual rhymes:
 (1) the three street STRANGERS from the hook physically RETURN in the flip-side;
 (2) the SAME word 叔叔 is the precise map term "father's younger brother" AND the warm
     greeting for a random older man — the punchline is SHOWN, not told.
People = flat cartoon/vector figures (no photoreal faces → avoids stock-uncle slop).
Three scenes connected by soft depth-transitions: #sc-people → #sc-map → (back to) #sc-people → #sc-outro.
On-screen text limited to Hanzi+pinyin chips + tiny axis labels (real learning value); no sentence overlays.

==================================================================
SCRIPT
==================================================================

--- HOOK (0:00–0:14) — the payoff up front ---

[SCENE #sc-people: white grid. Three flat cartoon strangers stand in a row, each in a tiny
iconic setting that reads their role instantly: a SHOPKEEPER behind a little stall counter,
a BUS DRIVER framed in a bus window, a NEIGHBOR in a doorway. Calm, friendly, brand colours.]
In China, the man at the shop, the bus driver, your neighbor — they're all your
[on "shop / bus driver / neighbor", a turquoise kinship chip pops above each figure in turn
(springy pop + soft pop-sound): 叔叔 over the shopkeeper, 叔叔 over the bus driver, 阿姨 over the
neighbor. Pinyin shū-shu / ā-yí tiny under each.]
"uncle" or "aunty." But here's the irony: there's no single Chinese word for "uncle"
[the three chips fly together to screen-centre and fuse into ONE English box reading "UNCLE ?".]
at all. There are at least eight. Let me explain.
[DEPTH ZOOM-OUT (grid converges, swoosh): the "UNCLE ?" box recedes and we transition into
SCENE #sc-map.]

--- THE PROBLEM WITH "UNCLE" (≈0:14–0:48) ---

[SCENE #sc-map: the "UNCLE ?" box sits center. ]
English has one lazy word — "uncle" — for a whole crowd of different people.
[behind/under the single box, a CROWD of identical little grey stick-men peek out and shuffle —
visual gag: English crams them all into one word. They overflow the box.]
Chinese refuses to be that vague. It needs to know exactly who you mean.
[the box cracks; the crowd FANS OUT and snaps onto a structured kinship map: a faint turquoise
tree with two columns — left "父 father's side", right "母 mother's side" — and rows for older/younger.]
Is he your father's brother, or your mother's?
[the two side-columns (father's side ⟷ mother's side) light up as they're named.]
Is he older than your parent, or younger?
[an older/younger axis highlights within the father's column.]
Is he blood, or married in?
[a "by blood ⟷ married-in" distinction flickers (married-in nodes get a small ring icon).]
Every combination is a different word.
Father's older brother, father's younger brother, mother's brother,
[each phrase lights its node with Hanzi + pinyin + tiny English gloss, in order:
 伯伯 bóbo (father's older brother) → 叔叔 shūshu (father's younger brother) →
 舅舅 jiùjiu (mother's brother). Springy pop each.]
the husband of your father's sister — all completely separate terms.
[node 姑父 gūfu (husband of father's sister) lights up. Other faint nodes already on the map
(姨父 yífu, 姑姑 gūgu, 姨 yí, 婶婶 shěnshen) make the "at least eight" honest.]
There's no neutral "uncle" to fall back on.
[the empty CENTRE slot — where a generic "uncle" would sit — shows a turquoise ✗ / stays struck-through empty.]
The word itself tells you the exact relationship.

--- WHY (≈0:48–1:05) ---

[stay on #sc-map, the full tree now visible.]
It's because family structure mattered so much that the language baked it right in —
which side, which generation, which order of birth.
[three small axis labels sweep across the map one at a time as they're spoken:
 哪边 which side · 哪辈 which generation · 排行 birth order.]
You don't just say "uncle."
You say precisely where he sits in the family.
[a single node on the tree pulses to show "his exact seat".]

--- THE FLIP SIDE (≈1:05–1:25) ---

[DEPTH transition BACK to SCENE #sc-people (swoosh) — the SAME three strangers return (visual rhyme).]
And yet — Chinese uses these very same family words for total strangers.
[precise terms FLY OUT of the map and land as warm greetings on the strangers.]
You call an older man "uncle," an older woman "aunty," a young woman "big sister" —
[on each: 叔叔 shūshu lands on the older man (note: the SAME 叔叔 from the map — briefly echo it),
 阿姨 āyí on the older woman, 大姐 dàjiě on a young woman. Pop each.]
as a sign of warmth and respect. Everyone gets slotted into the family.
[faint family-tree branches sketch outward from each stranger — they get pulled into the tree.]

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:25–1:38) ---

So Chinese can't translate "uncle" with a single word — it has a whole map of them.
[left: a compact precise mini-map of the nodes.]
But it'll happily call a stranger on the street your uncle.
[right: the strangers/crowd. The map's branches reach out and gently wrap the strangers.]
A language incredibly precise about family... that treats the entire world like one.
[the precise map and the wrapped strangers settle side by side — the contrast holds for a beat.]

[DEPTH lift to SCENE #sc-outro: default brand outro element.]
Wanna actually start learning Chinese?

[outro: stacked-cube logo between two turquoise chevrons, "stacktags" wordmark, grey stacktags.io line.]
