VIDEO SCRIPT
Title Why all of China runs on one clock
Topic Geography
Target length ~95 seconds (fits the 30s–3min window)
Language of narration English
Format Voiceover script. Spoken lines are plain text. [Bracketed lines] describe
what is on screen AT THE SAME TIME as the narration next to them.

==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
China spans ~5,000 km east-west — geographically about 5 time zones — but the
whole country officially runs on ONE Beijing time (UTC+8), set in 1949 for
national unity. In the far west (Xinjiang) the clock is badly out of sync with
the sun (winter sunrise ~10 a.m.), so many people quietly live on an unofficial
Xinjiang time (two hours behind Beijing).
Punchline one nation, one clock — but the sun doesn't take orders from Beijing.

KEY VISUALS
- A map of China with the ~5 natural time-zone bands, all overwritten by a single
  Beijing Time stamp.
- A western city where the clock says 7 a.m. but the sky is still dark.

==================================================================
SCRIPT
==================================================================

--- HOOK (000–012) — the payoff up front ---

China is almost 5,000 kilometers wide — wide enough to cross five time zones. But
the entire country runs on a single clock Beijing time.
[The map shows five natural time bands; a giant BEIJING TIME stamp slams over all of them.]
Which means that out in the far west, the sun sometimes doesn't come up until ten
in the morning.
[A western city a clock reads 930 a.m., the sky still pitch dark.]

--- ONE COUNTRY, ONE TIME (≈012–042) ---

Geographically, China is about as wide as the United States — which uses four
separate time zones. So China should have roughly five.
[The US with its 4 zones, side by side with China's would-be 5.]
But in 1949, the new government scrapped them all and put the whole nation on one
time — Beijing time — as a symbol of national unity. One country, one clock.
[Five clocks merge into a single clock face.]

--- WHERE IT BREAKS (≈042–115) ---

Near Beijing, that's fine. But thousands of kilometers west, in Xinjiang, the
official time is wildly out of step with the sky. In winter, sunrise can come
around 10 a.m. — and the sun can still be up late into the evening.
[Split screen Beijing noon (sun overhead) vs Xinjiang noon (sun barely risen).]
So people out there quietly run their lives on an unofficial Xinjiang time —
two hours behind Beijing. The buses, the shops, when you meet for dinner...
[Two clocks side by side Beijing time and local time, two hours apart.]
Ask someone the time, and you might have to ask back Beijing time, or our time

--- PUNCHLINE  OUTRO (≈115–128) ---

A single time zone keeps the country on the same page.
[The one-clock map, calm and unified.]
But the sun doesn't take orders from Beijing — so half a continent away, people
just quietly made their own time anyway.
