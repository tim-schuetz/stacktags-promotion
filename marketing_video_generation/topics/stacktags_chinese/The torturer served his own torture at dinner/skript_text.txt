==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
The chengyu 请君入瓮 ("please step into the jar"). Under Empress Wu Zetian, the cruel
official Lai Junchen was told to investigate a colleague, Zhou Xing — himself a brutal
interrogator. Over dinner, Lai casually asks Zhou for his best method to break a
stubborn prisoner. Zhou: seal them in a big jar with fire all around it. Lai then has
such a jar heated and says, "Please — step into the jar yourself." Zhou confesses
instantly.
Punchline: the cruelest interrogator was trapped by his own favorite method — and the
phrase survives as an idiom for it.

--- HOOK (0:00–0:14) — the payoff up front ---

One of China's most feared torturers was caught — and the man sent to break him simply
asked him, over dinner, for his best torture method. Then used it on him. There's a
whole idiom for this moment.

--- THE SETUP (≈0:14–0:42) ---

This is during the reign of Wu Zetian, China's only female emperor — a time of brutal
secret-police officials. Two of the worst were named Lai Junchen and Zhou Xing. Both
were notorious for torturing confessions out of anyone. Then one day, an accusation
came in against Zhou Xing himself. And the job of investigating him fell to his old
colleague — Lai Junchen. Now, Zhou was an expert at resisting interrogation. So Lai
didn't barge in with threats. He invited Zhou to a friendly dinner.

--- THE TRAP (≈0:42–1:08) ---

Over food and wine, Lai casually says: "I've got this prisoner who just won't confess.
You're the master — what would you do?" Flattered, Zhou happily shares his favorite
trick: "Easy. Get a giant jar. Put the prisoner inside. Then light a fire all the way
around it. Trust me — they'll admit to anything." Lai nods. Then he calls for servants
to bring in a huge jar, and to start a fire around it, right there. He turns to his
old friend and says the line that became famous: "请君入瓮" — "Please, sir... step into
the jar." Zhou Xing went pale, dropped to his knees, and confessed to everything on the
spot.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:08–1:22) ---

To this day, Chinese has that exact phrase — "please step into the jar" — meaning: to
catch someone using their own trick, to give them a taste of their own medicine. A
2,000-year-old reminder that the trap you design... might be the one you fall into.
