==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
"Mandarin" is a name FOREIGNERS gave it (via Portuguese "mandarim" = the imperial
officials, 官话 = "officials' speech"). The Chinese name today is just 普通话,
pǔtōnghuà = "common / ordinary speech." The arc: China had dynasties long before
Rome → centuries of war between rival states → unification, and the Han dynasty
becomes so dominant it names the people (Han) and the language (Hanyu) → writing
was unified, but SPEECH wasn't, so officials adopted one shared spoken standard →
the West named it "Mandarin" → modern China renamed it the humble "common speech."
Punchline: the grand foreign name "Mandarin" just means "normal talk."

NOTE (honesty): early dates are simplified. Qin unified China & standardized the
SCRIPT in 221 BC; the HAN dynasty that followed gave its name to the people and
language. "Mandarin" is a European exonym; 普通话 was standardized in the 20th c.

KEY VISUALS:
- A timeline that scrolls deep into the past (past "ROME").
- A map of warring states collapsing into one empire.
- Word reveal: 官话 → "MANDARIN" → 普通话 ("common speech").

SCRIPT

--- HOOK (0:00–0:10) — the payoff up front ---

Over a billion people speak Mandarin — but almost none of them call it that.
["MANDARIN" on screen; a red ✗ crosses the word out.]
"Mandarin" is a name foreigners gave it. And the real story of how it took over
China begins before Rome was ever an empire.
[A timeline rockets backwards, past "ROME," into ancient China.]

--- THE ANCIENT SPLIT (≈0:10–0:38) ---

Because long before Rome ruled anything, China had already risen and fallen through
dynasties going back more than three thousand years.
[Ancient dynasty names flash by on a deep timeline: Shang, Zhou...]
But "China" back then wasn't one country. It was a patchwork of rival states,
locked in near-constant war —
[A map of warring states, borders clashing.]
dozens of kingdoms, with different rulers, different armies, and different spoken
tongues.

--- UNIFICATION & THE HAN (≈0:38–1:05) ---

Then, in 221 BC, one state finally conquered the rest, forced them into a single
empire — and standardized the writing, so everyone wrote the same characters.
[The warring map collapses into one; mismatched characters snap into one uniform script.]
That first empire didn't last long. But the dynasty after it did: the Han.
[The character 汉 "Han" stamps across the map.]
The Han ruled for four centuries, and became so foundational that, to this day,
most Chinese call themselves "Han" — and their language "Hanyu," the language of
Han.

--- WRITING ISN'T SPEECH (≈1:05–1:32) ---

But here's the catch: unifying the writing is not the same as unifying the
speech.
[Everyone writes identical characters — yet their speech bubbles all sound different.]
For two thousand years, Chinese across the empire wrote alike but spoke languages
they couldn't understand out loud. So to actually govern, the officials settled
on one shared spoken standard, based on the northern, capital dialect.
[Robed officials speaking a single tongue across the whole map.]
They called it 官话 — "the speech of the officials."

--- WHERE "MANDARIN" COMES FROM (≈1:32–1:52) ---

And that's where our word is born. When Portuguese traders reached China in the
1500s, they called those robed officials "mandarins" —
[A Portuguese ship docks; the word "mandarim" appears.]
so in Europe, the officials' language became "Mandarin." A foreign name for the
language of power.
[官话 flips into "MANDARIN".]

--- MODERN "COMMON SPEECH" + PUNCHLINE (≈1:52–2:12) ---

Then, last century, that same northern standard was formalized, taught in every
school, and pushed through radio and TV — until it became the shared voice of
1.4 billion people. And they gave it a new, surprisingly humble name: 普通话.
["MANDARIN" flips into 普通话.]
Which means, simply... "common speech." Everyday, ordinary talk.
[The grand word "MANDARIN" shrinks beside the plain "普通话 — common speech".]
So the exotic, powerful name the West knows — to a billion speakers back home,
it's just normal language.
