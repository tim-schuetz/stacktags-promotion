--- HOOK (0:00–0:14) — the payoff up front ---

When you think "Chinese," you think of one language: Mandarin. But China is actually
home to around 300 different languages. And right now, dozens of them are quietly
dying out — some down to a handful of elderly speakers.

--- 300 LANGUAGES (≈0:14–0:40) ---

Mandarin gets all the attention, but it's just one voice in a huge chorus. Across
China there are hundreds of distinct languages — not just accents or dialects, but
separate tongues, many not even related to Mandarin. Spoken by minorities in the
mountains, the deserts, the borderlands. Most people outside China have never heard
of a single one.

--- WHY THEY'RE VANISHING (≈0:40–1:08) ---

But over the last few decades, that diversity has been collapsing. As standard
Mandarin became the language of school, work, TV, and the internet, the smaller
languages got squeezed out. Parents started raising kids in Mandarin to give them a
better shot in life. And a language only survives if children still speak it. So one
generation at a time, the old tongues stop being passed down — until the last fluent
speakers are grandparents, and then no one. When the last speaker of a language dies,
it's not just words that are lost. It's songs, jokes, history, an entire way of
seeing the world — gone for good.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:08–1:24) ---

It's one of the quietest mass extinctions on the planet — not of animals, but of
human voices. A country that holds 300 languages... slowly sliding toward one.
