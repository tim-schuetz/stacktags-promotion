THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
矛盾 (máodùn) = "contradiction," literally "spear-shield." From the Han Feizi: a
merchant brags his spear pierces ANY shield, and his shield stops ANY spear. A
bystander asks: so what happens if your spear hits your shield? Silence. The word for
"contradiction" is the fossil of that 2,300-year-old joke.
Punchline: every time a Chinese speaker says "contradiction," they're quoting an
ancient salesman who got owned.

--- HOOK (0:00–0:12) — the payoff up front ---

The Chinese word for "contradiction" is, literally, "spear-shield." 矛盾, máodùn —
spear, shield. And the reason why is a 2,300-year-old story about a salesman who talked
himself into a corner.

--- THE STORY (≈0:12–0:48) ---

It comes from an ancient book of philosophy, the Han Feizi. In it, a merchant is at the
market selling weapons. He holds up a spear and shouts: "This spear is so sharp, it can
pierce absolutely any shield in the world!" Then he holds up a shield and shouts: "And
this shield is so strong, no spear on Earth can pierce it!" Great sales pitch — until
someone in the crowd asks the obvious question. "Okay... so what happens if I take your
spear, and throw it at your shield?" And the merchant just stands there. Silent. Because
both claims can't be true at once. If the spear wins, the shield's a lie. If the shield
wins, the spear's a lie.

--- THE WORD (≈0:48–1:06) ---

That little trap stuck so hard in the Chinese imagination that the two objects became
the word itself. Put "spear" and "shield" together — 矛盾 — and you get the word for a
logical contradiction. Two things that cannot both be true.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:06–1:18) ---

So in Chinese, you don't just point out a contradiction — you literally say "spear-
shield." Every time, you're quoting a 2,300-year-old joke about a salesman who got
caught in his own pitch. The whole language remembers the punchline.
