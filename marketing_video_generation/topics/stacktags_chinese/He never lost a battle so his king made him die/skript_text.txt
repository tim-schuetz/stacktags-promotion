==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
Bai Qi, the Qin general who never lost a battle (incl. Changping), fell out with the
king and chancellor; he refused to lead a campaign he judged doomed, was demoted, then
ordered to commit suicide (~257 BCE). The greatest weapon Qin had was destroyed by Qin
itself — out of fear and politics.
Punchline: being undefeated made him too dangerous to keep alive.

NOTE: companion to the Changping video (same general, different angle — his downfall).

==================================================================
SCRIPT
==================================================================

--- HOOK (0:00–0:14) — the payoff up front ---

China's greatest general never lost a single battle. He won so much, and became so
feared, that in the end his own king ordered him to kill himself. Being undefeated is
what got him killed.

--- THE UNBEATABLE GENERAL (≈0:14–0:42) ---

His name was Bai Qi, and he fought for the state of Qin — the one that would go on to
unite China. Over a long career, he won battle after battle, crushing rival states and
killing enemy armies on a staggering scale. He was the hammer that smashed Qin's path
to power, and his reputation alone could make an enemy city surrender. By the records,
he simply never lost.

--- THE FALLING-OUT (≈0:42–1:08) ---

But power makes people nervous — especially the people above you. Bai Qi clashed with
the king's chief minister, who grew jealous and wary of him. Then came a campaign Bai
Qi thought was a mistake — the timing wrong, the army exhausted, the target too strong.
So he refused to lead it. He told the king, in effect: this attack will fail. The king
sent the army anyway, with another general — and it failed, badly, just as Bai Qi
warned. But instead of being vindicated, Bai Qi was now a problem. A general who'd
publicly refused orders, been proven right, and was beloved by the troops. To a
suspicious king, that's not an asset. That's a threat.

--- THE END (≈1:08–1:26) ---

So he was stripped of rank, exiled from the capital — and then a royal messenger caught
up with him on the road, carrying a sword and an order: end your own life. And he did.
The most successful general in Chinese history, undone not by any enemy, but by the very
king he'd won everything for.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:26–1:38) ---

It's an ancient lesson that never gets old: sometimes the most dangerous thing you can
be... is too good at your job, standing too close to a frightened king.
