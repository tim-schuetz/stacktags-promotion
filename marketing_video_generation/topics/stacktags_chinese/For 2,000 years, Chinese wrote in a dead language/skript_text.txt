==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
For most of its history the WRITTEN language was 文言文 (Classical/Literary Chinese),
frozen around ancient speech and long diverged from everyday spoken Chinese — a
diglossia: you wrote one language and spoke another (白话). Hu Shi's vernacular
movement (from 1917) made spoken baihua the written norm within ~a decade. Learn:
文言文, 白话, diglossia.
Punchline: it took a 20th-century revolution just to let people write the way they talk.

NOTE (honesty): "nobody spoke it for 2,000 years" is the hook's hyperbole — Classical
Chinese was based on speech from ~2,000+ years earlier and hadn't matched everyday
speech for many centuries. Resolve honestly in the body.

--- HOOK (0:00–0:14) — the payoff up front ---

[SCENE: HOOK — persistent hero figure (figure kit) centered on the white grid, a brush
in hand, a blank paper/scroll in front of it. Faint, friendly idle.]

For most of its history, China wrote in a language that nobody actually spoke — and
hadn't spoken for well over a thousand years. You'd speak one kind of Chinese... and
write a completely different one.

[ON "speak one kind of Chinese": a SPEECH BUBBLE pops from the figure's mouth (pop SFX)

different one": the brush draws DENSE, different 文言文 onto the scroll via hanzi

snaps in between the mouth-bubble and the hand-scroll. Hero's face = mild confusion.

--- THE SPLIT (≈0:14–0:48) ---

[SCENE: DRIFT DIAGRAM (custom hero element). Camera depth-moves out from the hook into a
two-lane track. Both lanes START FUSED at a single left origin dot labelled "antiquity

Until about a century ago, written Chinese was 文言文 — Classical, or Literary,
Chinese. It was based on the language of the ancient classics, from over two thousand
years ago. And it basically froze there.

[ON "written Chinese was 文言文": the TOP (written) lane gets its marker — hanzi 文言文
writes on (text-popup) with pinyin "wényánwén" + small gloss "literary Chinese". ON "it
basically froze there": the top lane FREEZES — frost tint + a small 🔒/ice glaze creeps
over the 文言文 marker, and it STOPS advancing while a faint year-ticker keeps counting
up past it. No swoosh (grid still); soft frost tick.]

Meanwhile, spoken Chinese kept evolving, century after century. So the written language
and the spoken language drifted further and further apart — until they were effectively
two different languages.

[ON "spoken Chinese kept evolving": the BOTTOM (spoken) lane keeps MOVING forward, its
glyph morphing through 2–3 evolving forms (hanzi morph) = the living language changing.
ON "drifted further and further apart": the two lanes visibly SEPARATE vertically, the
gap between frozen-top and moving-bottom widening; a thin dashed "gap" measure grows.]

You spoke everyday Chinese — 白话, "plain speech" — but to write, you had to use this
ancient, compressed, classical form almost no one actually spoke.

[ON "白话, plain speech": the bottom lane gets its label — hanzi 白话 (text-popup) +
pinyin "báihuà" + gloss "plain speech". End state held: TOP = frozen 文言文 (iced,
locked), BOTTOM = living 白话. This split diagram is the anchor the rest references.]

--- WHAT THAT MEANT (≈0:48–1:14) ---

[SCENE: DIGLOSSIA. The two lanes reframe into two domains: STREET (left, a couple of
small everyday people figures = 白话) and PAGE (right, a scroll/book = 文言文). The word
"diglossia" pops in centered (text-popup) — this is the linguistics term we teach.]

Linguists have a name for this: diglossia — one language for the street, another for
the page.

[ON "one language for the street, another for the page": briefly tag the STREET side
with the living 白话 glyph and the PAGE side with the frozen 文言文 glyph (reuse the two
markers, no new prose text).]

And it meant that being literate didn't just mean knowing the characters. It meant
mastering a near-dead language on top of your own.

[ON "mastering a near-dead language on top of your own": one ordinary figure has to
carry a SECOND heavy scroll (the dead language) stacked on top — visible strain / humor.]

Which made reading and writing brutally hard, and kept them locked to a small,
scholarly elite. Everyday speech simply wasn't considered "proper" to write down.

[ON "locked to a small, scholarly elite": the PAGE sits behind a closed GATE with the
same 🔒 lock from the frozen lane (callback). Only ONE scholar figure (robe + cap) passes
through; the ordinary STREET figures walk up and BOUNCE off the gate (light humor). swoosh
on the gate slam.]

--- THE REVOLUTION (≈1:14–1:34) ---

[SCENE: HU SHI. A stylized reformer figure SLIDES IN from the top (like the "official"

1917 STAMP slams down (stamp SFX = swoosh). No photo — stylized vector figure.]

Then, starting around 1917, a scholar named Hu Shi led a movement with a radical
idea: write the way you actually speak. Use the living, spoken language — baihua — as
the written standard.

[ON "write the way you actually speak": the brush now writes 白话 DIRECTLY onto the page;

payoff of the drift diagram). swoosh on the reconverge.]

It caught on fast. Within about a decade, the vernacular had replaced classical Chinese
in schools and newspapers.

[ON "within about a decade": a quick 1917 → 1927 counter ticks (ticking SFX). ON "schools
and newspapers": a recreated NEWSPAPER masthead (built in HTML, period look) flips its
body text from dense 文言文 columns to clean 白话 — one crisp before→after switch, not a
montage.]

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:34–1:48) ---

[SCENE: PAYOFF — return to the hero figure. Now the speech bubble (白话) and the written

So for two thousand years, China wrote in a language that time had left behind — and
it took a twentieth-century revolution just to let people write the way they talk. We
take it for granted that we write our own language. For most of Chinese history, that
wasn't even allowed.
