==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
For most of its history the WRITTEN language was 文言文 (Classical/Literary Chinese),
frozen around ancient speech and long diverged from everyday spoken Chinese — a
diglossia: you wrote one language and spoke another (白话). Hu Shi's vernacular
movement (from 1917) made spoken baihua the written norm within ~a decade. Learn:
文言文, 白话, diglossia.
Punchline: it took a 20th-century revolution just to let people write the way they talk.

REWRITE NOTE (2026-06-28): the previous cut stated the gap but never EXPLAINED it, so viewers
asked "how can writing freeze while speech changes?". This version grounds it with a concrete
example (the everyday word for "you" kept shifting in speech, but writing kept the ancient form)
and spells out that 文言文 differed in words AND grammar, not just in being "hard characters".

NOTE (honesty): "nobody spoke it for 2,000 years" is the hook's hyperbole — Classical
Chinese was based on speech from ~2,000+ years earlier and hadn't matched everyday
speech for many centuries. Resolved honestly in the body.

--- HOOK (0:00–0:18) — the payoff up front, now grounded ---

[SCENE: HOOK — the hero (generated photo of an ordinary person) centered on the white grid.]

For most of its history, China wrote in a language that almost nobody actually spoke. You'd say
something out loud... and then write it down in a completely different language. Same meaning — but
different words, and different grammar.

[ON "say something out loud": a SPEECH BUBBLE pops with plain spoken 白话 → 我不知道 ("I don't
know"). ON "write it down in a completely different language": a brush writes the terse classical
文言文 → 吾不知 onto paper (hanzi write-on). ON "different words, different grammar": a big turquoise
"≠" snaps in between the bubble and the paper, tiny SPOKEN / WRITTEN tags. Hold this as the hook.]

--- THE SPLIT (≈0:18–0:58) — explain HOW the gap opened ---

[SCENE: DRIFT (teaching spine). Two columns under a small kicker "the everyday word for ‘you’":
LEFT = 文言文 (written), RIGHT = 白话 (spoken). Both start with the SAME card 汝 (rǔ).]

Here's how that happened. The written language was called 文言文 — classical Chinese — and it was
based on the way people spoke more than two thousand years ago. Then it basically froze. Scholars
kept writing in that same ancient style, century after century. But the spoken language never stopped
changing.

[ON "based on the way people spoke 2,000 years ago" / "froze": establish the LEFT column — 文言文
label + card 汝 (rǔ) — and a plain grey track dropping straight down (frozen). Establish the RIGHT
column — 白话 label + the same card 汝 — with a teal track.]

Take a simple, everyday word — like the word for "you." Over the centuries, the way people said it
slowly shifted. But to write it, you were still expected to use the ancient form.

[ON "the way people said it slowly shifted": down the RIGHT teal line, cards pop in turn — 汝 (rǔ) →
爾 (ěr) → 你 (nǐ), the modern spoken form. ON "you were still expected to use the ancient form": the
LEFT bottom card pops — still 汝 (rǔ), unchanged — directly across from the evolved 你.]

Do that across the whole language, and the Chinese people spoke and the Chinese they wrote drifted
into two different worlds — one alive and changing, the other frozen in time.

[Hold the contrast: RIGHT column 汝→爾→你 (alive), LEFT column 汝…汝 (frozen). This is the answer to
"how can writing freeze while speech changes".]

--- WHAT THAT MEANT (≈0:58–1:24) — why it was so costly ---

[SCENE: DIGLOSSIA — the term "diglossia" pops; STREET (generated commoner crowd) with 白话 vs PAGE
(a scroll) with 文言文, a dashed divide between them.]

Linguists have a name for this: diglossia — one language for talking, another for writing. And it
made being literate brutally hard.

[ON "diglossia": term pops centered, then the two domains slide in — crowd + 白话 (the street),
scroll + 文言文 (the page).]

You didn't just have to learn the characters — you had to master what was almost a dead language, on
top of the one you already spoke. So reading and writing stayed locked to a small, educated elite.
For everyone else, the way they actually talked just wasn't considered proper to write down.

[SCENE: ELITE — a single scholar-official (generated photo) raised high holding 文言文; far below, a
big crowd (generated photo) with 白话; a dashed divide between high and low = literacy locked to a
tiny elite. No padlock.]

--- THE REVOLUTION (≈1:24–1:46) ---

[SCENE: HU SHI — a generated vintage portrait of Hu Shi slides in; label 胡适 "Hu Shi"; a turquoise
1917 seal presses in.]

Then, around 1917, a scholar named Hu Shi pushed a radical idea: simply write the way you speak. Use
the living, spoken language — baihua — as the written standard.

[ON "write the way you speak": the brush now writes 白话 directly. ON "as the written standard":
cut back to the DRIFT — the frozen written card 汝 sheds and rewrites to 你, matching the spoken
form (the two columns reunite).]

And it caught on fast. Within about a decade, schools and newspapers had traded classical Chinese
for the language people actually used.

[ON "within about a decade": a quick 1917 → 1927 counter (ticking). ON "schools and newspapers": a
recreated 新青年 (New Youth) masthead flips its body from cramped classical columns to clean
vernacular lines.]

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:46–2:00) ---

[SCENE: PAYOFF — back to the hero; now the spoken bubble (我不知道) and the written paper (我不知道)
MATCH, and the hook's "≠" flips to a turquoise "=".]

So for two thousand years, China wrote in a language that time had left behind — and it took a
twentieth-century revolution just to let people write the way they talk. We take it for granted that
we write our own language. For most of Chinese history, that simply wasn't allowed.
