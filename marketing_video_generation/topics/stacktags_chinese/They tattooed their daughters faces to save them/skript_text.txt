==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
Derung (独龙族) girls in Yunnan's Dulong valley received a painful facial tattoo
around age 12–13 (thorn needles + soot). The most commonly cited explanation: weaker
groups deliberately made their women look "unattractive" so raiders from stronger
neighbors wouldn't abduct them. The custom ended around the 1960s; fewer than ~40
tattooed women are still alive.
Punchline: a beauty mark that was actually armor.

NOTE (honesty): the "to avoid abduction" reason is the popular/most-cited theory but
it's debated — other explanations include coming-of-age, tribal identity, and beauty.
Frame it as "the most common explanation."

SCRIPT
==================================================================

--- HOOK (0:00–0:14) — the payoff up front ---

In one remote corner of China, girls as young as twelve had their entire faces
tattooed — in a slow, painful ritual. And the most common explanation for why is
heartbreaking: it was meant to make them ugly... so they wouldn't be stolen.

--- WHO THEY WERE (≈0:14–0:36) ---

This was the Derung people, in a deep river valley in Yunnan, near the borders with
Myanmar and Tibet. So isolated that for much of history, the only way in was over
mountain passes that snow shut for half the year. And among the Derung, when a girl
reached about twelve or thirteen, she went through a rite that marked her for life.

--- THE RITUAL (≈0:36–1:00) ---

An elder woman would prick a pattern across her face with thorn needles, then rub
soot deep into the wounds. As it healed, it left a permanent blue-black tattoo,
spreading over the nose, cheeks, and chin. It was agonizing, and it took days. Every
Derung woman wore one.

--- WHY (≈1:00–1:22) ---

So why do it to a child? The explanation most often given is grim. The Derung were a
small, weak group, surrounded by more powerful neighbors who raided them — and carried
off their women as slaves or brides. So, the story goes, the Derung made their own
daughters less desirable to outsiders on purpose. A tattooed face, they hoped, was a
face no raider would take. Beauty, deliberately hidden, as a kind of armor.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:22–1:36) ---

The custom faded out around the 1960s, as the raids ended and the world arrived. Today
fewer than forty of these tattooed women are still alive — the last faces of a
tradition where a mark of pain was really a mark of protection.
