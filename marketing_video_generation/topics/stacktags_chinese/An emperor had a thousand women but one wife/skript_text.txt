==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
Explainer on concubines. In old China a man had ONE legal wife (嫡) but could take
multiple concubines (妾) of lower rank. Concubines' children were legitimate but ranked
below the wife's. The emperor had a vast, tiered harem. The wife-vs-concubine line
decided status, inheritance, and succession — it could decide who became emperor.
Punchline: it wasn't about how many women — it was about which one "counted."

SCRIPT
==================================================================

--- HOOK (0:00–0:14) — the payoff up front ---

A Chinese emperor could have hundreds, even thousands of women in his palace. But only
one of them was his wife. And that single distinction — wife versus concubine — could
decide who inherited an empire.

--- WIFE VS CONCUBINE (≈0:14–0:44) ---

Here's how it worked. A man of status didn't have many wives. He had exactly one — his
legal, primary wife, the head of the household. But he could also take concubines:
additional partners, recognized and living in the home, but ranked clearly below the
wife. A concubine wasn't a secret mistress — she had an official place. She just didn't
have the top place. And that ranking flowed down to the children. The kids of both the
wife and the concubines were legitimate — but a wife's son outranked a concubine's son.
Same father, very different futures.

--- THE EMPEROR'S HAREM (≈0:44–1:08) ---

Now scale that up to the emperor. He sat at the center of an enormous harem — one
empress at the top, then a whole pyramid of consorts and concubines below her, sorted
into formal ranks, each with titles, quarters, and privileges. Hundreds, sometimes
thousands of women, all carefully ordered. And it wasn't just about pleasure. It was
about heirs. Producing the next emperor was the whole point — and the higher a woman's
rank, the stronger her child's claim to the throne.

--- WHY IT MATTERED (≈1:08–1:24) ---

Which is why palace life was so ruthless. A concubine who bore the emperor a son could
rocket up the ranks — even become empress. Whole dynasties were shaped by which woman
"counted," and whose son was declared the true heir. Succession wars were often, at
heart, fights between a wife and a concubine.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:24–1:36) ---

So the harem wasn't really about a thousand women. It was about one line — the line
between the wife and everyone else — and which child got to stand on the right side of
it.
