--- HOOK (0:00–0:12) — the payoff up front ---

There's a people in China who made their clothes out of fish. Not fish leather they
bought — actual fish skin, peeled, tanned, and sewn into coats. About fifty fish for a
single outfit.

--- WHO THEY ARE (≈0:12–0:34) ---

They're the Hezhe — one of the smallest ethnic groups in all of China, living up in the
cold northeast, along the great rivers near the Russian border. They lived by fishing.
And in a freezing land with few other resources, they turned to the one thing they had
in abundance: fish. Not just for food — for everything, including what they wore. It
earned them an old nickname: the fish-skin people.

--- HOW THEY DID IT (≈0:34–1:00) ---

Making a fish-skin coat was real craftsmanship. They'd catch big river fish — salmon,
carp — and carefully peel off the skins in whole pieces. Then came the work: scraping,
drying, and pounding the skins for a long time, often softening them with fish oil,
until the stiff, scaly hide turned into something supple, like a soft leather. Then they
stitched dozens of these prepared skins together — around fifty fish for one full set of
clothes — into robes, trousers, even boots. And fish skin has a hidden advantage up
north: it's surprisingly tough, and it's water-resistant. Perfect for a life spent on
rivers and ice.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:00–1:14) ---

Today, almost nobody still wears it — but a few Hezhe artisans keep the craft alive as
heritage. A whole wardrobe, pulled straight out of the river — proof that with enough
skill, even a fish can become a winter coat.
