VIDEO SCRIPT
Title: Mandarin is broken — and you hear it in every pop song
(working title, DE: "Mandarin ist sprachlich kaputt – und du hörst es in jedem Pop-Song")
Topic: Learn Chinese
Target length: ~105 seconds (fits the 30s–3min window)
Language of narration: English
Format: Voiceover script. Spoken lines are plain text. [Bracketed lines] describe
what is on screen AT THE SAME TIME as the narration next to them.

==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
Over ~1,000 years, Mandarin shed most of its sounds: the hard final consonants
-p, -t, -k, -m all disappeared, and the syllable count collapsed (counter:
~1200 → ~400). Fewer sounds → rhyme pool collapses → you hear it in pop music.
Cantonese kept the old endings + tones, so Tang-dynasty poems still rhyme there.
Punchline: Mandarin won the country — but lost the sound.

NOTE (honesty): "broken" is the provocative hook, not a literal verdict — the
real story is erosion / simplification, resolved by the punchline (a trade-off,
not a defect). Syllable numbers are rounded for the on-screen counter.

KEY VISUALS (as requested):
- A live syllable counter that ticks DOWN: 1200 → 400.
- Before/after example words shown in BIG CAPITALS (e.g., 月: NGWAT → YUÈ).
- GLOBE SVG spins and zooms onto Hong Kong / Guangdong.

==================================================================
SCRIPT
==================================================================

--- HOOK (0:00–0:06) — just state the title ---

Mandarin is a broken language — and you can hear it in every pop song.
[Title card; underneath, a Mandarin pop track plays, its waveform all long, open vowels.]

--- THE TANG GOLDEN AGE (≈0:06–0:35) ---

It wasn't always this way. Rewind to the Tang dynasty — China's golden age of
poetry. Back then the language had roughly three to four times as many distinct
syllables as Mandarin does now.
[A syllable counter appears, glowing at ~1200.]
And words ended in crisp, hard consonants — p, t, k, and m. Take the word for
"moon," 月.
[Hero card: the character 月.]
In Tang Chinese, it sounded like this —
[Big capitals slam on: NGWAT]
"ngwat." A hard stop, right on that -t.

--- THE COLLAPSE (≈0:38–1:05) ---

Now fast-forward to Mandarin. That same word?
[The capitals morph: NGWAT → YUÈ.]
"Yuè." The hard ending — gone. And it isn't just this one word.
[Rapid trio, old sound → Mandarin, each ending dropping off:]
Ten: "sap" became "shí". Six: "luk" became "liù". Heart: "sam" became xin.
Every one of those final consonants — p, t, k, m — vanished from Mandarin
completely.
[The counter now ticks DOWN live: 1200 ... 800 ... 400.]
The result: a language of 1.4 billion people, running on only about 400 syllables.

--- WHY YOU HEAR IT IN POP (≈1:05–1:25) ---

And when you cram that many words into so few sounds, the rhyme pool collapses.
Everything starts to sound the same.
[A web of different words all sliding onto identical syllables.]
That's why so much Mandarin pop leans on long, open vowels — ahh, eyy, ohh,
wayy. There are no crisp endings left to snap a line shut.
[Pop waveform again: long, held, open vowels.]

--- THE SOUTHERN TIME CAPSULE (≈1:25–1:50) ---

But head south — to Hong Kong and Guangdong —
[GLOBE SVG spins and zooms down onto Hong Kong / Guangdong.]
and you hit the language that kept everything. Cantonese still has six tones, and
every one of those old endings — p, t, k, m — fully intact.
[Cantonese syllables snapping shut on hard consonants: "yut," "sap," "luk."]
Which leads to something wild: thousand-year-old Tang poems, the ones that sound
slightly off in Mandarin —
[A classical poem; in Mandarin, some line-endings don't rhyme — red ✗.]
still rhyme perfectly in Cantonese.
[Same poem read in Cantonese; the line-endings click into rhyme — green ✓.]
The southern dialect is basically a time capsule of how this poetry was meant to
sound.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:50–2:02) ---

So here's the irony. Mandarin won —
[Map of China fills in with Mandarin; the counter rests at 400.]
it became the voice of a nation. But while the language won, the sound was lost.
[The ghost of "1200" fades behind the lonely "400."]
