==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
The Mosuo, around Lugu Lake (Yunnan/Sichuan), are matrilineal and have no marriage.
"Walking marriage" (走婚): a man visits his partner only at night and returns to his
own mother's house by morning; children belong to and stay in the MOTHER's clan for
life; the household is headed by a matriarch; property passes through women.
Punchline: a society that runs on mothers, where "husband" isn't even a role.

NOTE (honesty): "matriarchy" is the popular label; strictly it's matrilineal +
matrifocal (women hold the household, but it isn't pure female rule). ~40,000 Mosuo;
the tradition is fading with tourism/modernization.

SCRIPT
==================================================================

--- HOOK (0:00–0:12) — the payoff up front ---

There's a society in China where there is no marriage, fathers don't live with
their kids, and the family is run entirely by the women. They're called the Mosuo —
and they may be the last matriarchy on Earth.

--- WHO THEY ARE (≈0:12–0:34) ---

The Mosuo live around Lugu Lake, high in the mountains between Yunnan and Sichuan.
And their whole idea of family is built upside-down from ours. Here, the household
isn't led by a husband. It's led by a matriarch — the grandmother or eldest woman —
and everyone in it is related through the women.

--- THE WALKING MARRIAGE (≈0:34–1:04) ---

The most striking part is how couples work. The Mosuo practice what's called a
"walking marriage." A woman has her own room, and a partner she chooses. He visits
her — but only at night. By morning, he walks back to his own mother's house, where
he lives, eats, and works his whole life. The two never share a home, never merge
finances, never "marry." And when children are born, they don't go to the father.
They belong to the mother's family, raised by her, her sisters, and her brothers —
who act as the father figures. The biological dad might be just down the road, a
welcome visitor, but never a member of the household.

--- WHY IT'S REMARKABLE (≈1:04–1:24) ---

Think about what that erases. No marriage, so no divorce. No splitting of property,
because property simply passes down through the women. A child always knows exactly
where home is: with mom's clan, for life. For centuries it gave Mosuo women a kind of
independence almost unheard of in the old world.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:24–1:36) ---

It's not pure female rule — men still do plenty — but it's about as close to a
mothers'-world as any society on the planet. A place where "husband" was never even
a job. And with roads and tourism arriving, it's quietly disappearing.
