==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
Dungan: descendants of Hui (Chinese Muslims) who migrated to Central Asia (now
Kyrgyzstan/Kazakhstan) in the 1800s. They speak a Mandarin-descended language but
write it in the CYRILLIC alphabet (since the Soviet era) — no Chinese characters at
all. It's living proof that a Sinitic language CAN be written alphabetically, despite
the common claim that characters are essential.
Punchline: how a language sounds and how we choose to write it are two different
things.

KEY VISUALS:
- A map: Hui migrating west from China to Central Asia.
- A Mandarin phrase shown in Chinese characters → then the same phrase in Cyrillic.

==================================================================
SCRIPT
==================================================================

--- HOOK (0:00–0:12) — the payoff up front ---

There's a form of Chinese written with no Chinese characters at all — in the same
alphabet as Russian.
[A Mandarin phrase in characters flips into Cyrillic letters.]
It's spoken in Central Asia, and it quietly proves something a lot of people insist
is impossible.

--- WHO THEY ARE (≈0:12–0:38) ---

In the 1800s, a group of Chinese Muslims — the Hui — fled unrest and migrated west,
settling in what's now Kyrgyzstan and Kazakhstan.
[A map: people moving from western China into Central Asia.]
Their descendants are called the Dungan. And they still speak a language descended
from Mandarin Chinese — close enough that a Mandarin speaker can largely follow it.
[Two speakers, mostly understanding each other.]

--- THE TWIST (≈0:38–1:05) ---

But here's the twist. Cut off from China, and living in the Soviet world, they
stopped using Chinese characters entirely.
[Characters fading away.]
Today, the Dungan language is written in Cyrillic — letters, just like Russian.
[A full Dungan sentence in Cyrillic script.]
Which matters, because people often say Chinese can't be written with an alphabet —
that it NEEDS characters, because too many words sound alike. Dungan is living proof
that it can. A complete, working Sinitic language, written phonetically, with letters.

--- THE BIGGER IDEA (≈1:05–1:22) ---

And that quietly reveals something. The characters and the language aren't the same
thing.
[A "language" box and a "writing system" box, clearly separate.]
The spoken Chinese language could, in principle, be written in letters — and the
Dungan have been doing exactly that for generations.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:22–1:34) ---

So somewhere in Central Asia, people are speaking a kind of Chinese — and writing it
in the alphabet of Russian.
[The Cyrillic Dungan text glowing.]
A reminder that how a language sounds, and how we choose to write it down, aren't the
same thing at all.
