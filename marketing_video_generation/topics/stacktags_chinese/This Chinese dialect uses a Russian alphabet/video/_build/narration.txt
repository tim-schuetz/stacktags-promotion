There's a form of Chinese written with no Chinese characters at all — in the same alphabet as Russian. It's spoken in Central Asia, and it quietly proves something a lot of people insist is impossible.

In the 1800s, a group of Chinese Muslims — the Hui — fled unrest and migrated west, settling in what's now Kyrgyzstan and Kazakhstan. Their descendants are called the Dungan. And they still speak a language descended from Mandarin Chinese — close enough that a Mandarin speaker can largely follow it.

But here's the twist. Cut off from China, and living in the Soviet world, they stopped using Chinese characters entirely. Today, the Dungan language is written in Cyrillic — letters, just like Russian. Which matters, because people often say Chinese can't be written with an alphabet — that it needs characters, because too many words sound alike. Dungan is living proof that it can. A complete, working Sinitic language, written phonetically, with letters.

And that quietly reveals something. The characters and the language aren't the same thing. The spoken Chinese language could, in principle, be written in letters — and the Dungan have been doing exactly that for generations.

So somewhere in Central Asia, people are speaking a kind of Chinese — and writing it in the alphabet of Russian. A reminder that how a language sounds, and how we choose to write it down, aren't the same thing at all.
