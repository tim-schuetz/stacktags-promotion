==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
The Qin army (3rd c. BCE) mass-produced weapons with interchangeable parts —
standardized bronze crossbow triggers that could swap between weapons — and ran quality
control by ENGRAVING the maker's (and overseer's) name into each part (物勒工名). If a
part failed, they could trace exactly who made it. It's standardized production +
accountability, ~2,000 years before the industrial assembly line.
Punchline: the Qin ran a factory-style supply chain with names carved in for blame.

SCRIPT

--- HOOK (0:00–0:14) — the payoff up front ---

Over 2,000 years ago, China was already mass-producing weapons with interchangeable
parts — and every single part had the maker's name carved into it, so if it failed,
they knew exactly who to blame. A factory supply chain, in the age of bronze.

--- INTERCHANGEABLE PARTS (≈0:14–0:42) ---

We usually credit interchangeable parts and assembly-line thinking to the Industrial
Revolution, a couple hundred years ago. But look at the army of China's first emperor,
the Qin — the one buried with the Terracotta Warriors. Archaeologists studying their
weapons found something stunning: the bronze trigger mechanisms of their crossbows were
standardized. The parts were made so consistently that you could take a piece from one
crossbow and drop it into another, and it would just... work. That's interchangeable
parts — the core idea behind mass production — two thousand years early.

--- ENGRAVED LIABILITY (≈0:42–1:10) ---

But here's the part that's really ahead of its time: quality control. Each weapon and
its components were engraved with names — the worker who made it, the foreman who
supervised, all the way up the chain. The Chinese called this principle "inscribe the
work with the maker's name." So if a crossbow trigger snapped in battle, officials could
read the inscription and trace the failure straight back to the exact craftsman and his
boss. Make junk, and your name was on it. It's the ancient version of a serial number
and a quality stamp — accountability built right into the metal, to keep standards
brutally high across thousands of weapons.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:10–1:24) ---

Standardized parts. A traceable supply chain. Names carved in for liability. That's
modern manufacturing thinking — running an empire's war machine two thousand years
before the first factory ever opened its doors.
