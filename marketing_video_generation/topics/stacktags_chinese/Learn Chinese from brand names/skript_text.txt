THE BRANDS → THEIR CHINESE NAMES
==================================================================
Western brands localized into Chinese (sound + flattering meaning):
1. BMW         — 宝马   bǎomǎ      — "treasure horse"
2. Mercedes    — 奔驰   bēnchí     — "to gallop / speed"
3. Coca-Cola   — 可口可乐 kěkǒu kělè — "tasty + enjoyable" (delicious happiness)

Chinese brands that pick a meaning ON PURPOSE (no foreign sound to copy):
4. Li Auto     — 理想   lǐxiǎng    — "the ideal"
5. Nio         — 蔚来   wèilái     — homophone of 未来 = "the future"
6. BYD         — 比亚迪 bǐyàdí      — runs the same play in ENGLISH: "Build Your Dreams"

Recurring visual device — "the flip":
A familiar logo flips over to reveal its Chinese name and literal meaning.
For the Chinese brands, it flips the other way: characters → English meaning.

--- HOOK (0:00–0:10) — the payoff lands in the first 5 seconds ---

In China, BMW isn't BMW, it's 宝马, "treasure horse." Mercedes is 奔驰,
"galloping speed." And Coca-Cola? — "delicious happiness."
[Each Western logo flips to reveal its Chinese name + literal meaning.]

None of those are accidents. When a brand enters China, it doesn't just copy
the sound — it picks characters that sound right AND mean something flattering.
[The three logos line up, glowing.]
Here's how the biggest names did it — and how Chinese brands now play the same
game even harder.

--- THE WESTERN BRANDS (≈0:10–0:50) ---

Start with the cars. BMW became 宝马.
[FLIP: BMW roundel → 宝马; 宝 and 马 highlight.]
宝 means "treasure," 马 means "horse." It still sounds like "BMW" — but now it
also promises you a prized, powerful steed.

Mercedes-Benz became 奔驰.
[FLIP: the three-pointed star → 奔驰.]
It means "to gallop, to speed." Sounds like "Benz," means pure motion. The name
is the marketing.

And the all-time masterpiece: Coca-Cola — 可口可乐.
[FLIP: the Coca-Cola script → 可口可乐.]
It copies the sound almost perfectly — and it means "tasty, and enjoyable." A
soda literally named "delicious happiness."

--- THE TWIST: CHINESE BRANDS (≈0:50–1:20) ---

Plenty of brands pick a name for their image. But Chinese brands take it to
another level —
[Western logos fade out; sleek Chinese EV logos rise in their place.]
especially the new electric-car makers. They're not translating a foreign sound
at all. They just reach into the language and grab a word that IS their mission.

One brand is simply called 理想.
[FLIP: 理想 → "THE IDEAL".]
The whole name just means "the ideal."

Another you know in the West as Nio. In Chinese it's 蔚来 —
[FLIP: 蔚来 → 未来.]
which sounds exactly like 未来: "the future." A car company named, literally,
"The Future."

And here's the kicker — they run the same play in English, too. Take BYD.
[Background: the letters "BYD" stretch and expand, one word per letter...]
It actually stands for "Build Your Dreams."
[...resolving into "BUILD YOUR DREAMS" filling the screen.]
An entire aspiration, baked right into the logo. Bold? A bit much? We'll let you
be the judge.

--- OUTRO / CTA (≈1:30–1:42) ---

A treasure horse, a galloping star, building your dreams — every one of these
names is a tiny Chinese lesson hiding in plain sight.
[Quick montage: 宝马, 奔驰, 可口可乐, 理想, 蔚来, BYD flash by with their meanings.]
That's branding as a free language lesson. [short pause in the speach]
