==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
The Wa (佤族) of the Yunnan–Myanmar border were headhunters: a freshly taken human
head, offered to the grain/crop spirit, was believed to guarantee the rice harvest.
Heads were hunted mainly in the sowing season (roughly Feb–June); skulls were
displayed on "skull avenues." The practice was suppressed only in the mid-20th c.
Punchline: for the Wa, a good harvest didn't start with rain — it started with a head.

NOTE (honesty): real, documented practice ended ~1950s under the PRC; keep it factual,
not gratuitous.

SCRIPT
==================================================================

--- HOOK (0:00–0:14) — the payoff up front ---

Less than a hundred years ago, there were farmers in China who believed they needed
one specific thing for a good rice harvest — and it wasn't rain, or fertilizer. It was
a freshly cut human head.

--- WHO THEY WERE (≈0:14–0:36) ---

This was the Wa people, in the rugged mountains along the border of Yunnan and what's
now Myanmar. And for generations, headhunting wasn't random violence — it was
agriculture. Part of the farming calendar.

--- THE BELIEF (≈0:36–1:02) ---

The Wa believed the spirit of the grain demanded a blood offering. A human head,
given to that spirit, would make the crops grow tall and the harvest rich. So heads
were hunted on a schedule — mostly in the sowing season, roughly spring into early
summer, exactly when the new crop needed blessing. The victim was usually a stranger
from another village — ideally one with a thick beard, which was thought to bring an
even better yield. And the skulls didn't get buried. They were displayed — lined up
in special groves and along "skull avenues" at the edge of the village, proof of a
harvest secured.

--- THE END OF IT (≈1:02–1:22) ---

This continued, openly, into the twentieth century. It was only in the 1950s that the
practice was finally stamped out, as the new Chinese state pushed into these remote
hills and banned it. Within a generation, headhunting went from sacred duty to
history.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:22–1:34) ---

We think of farming as the gentlest thing humans do. But for the Wa, the whole cycle
of life in the fields once began with the taking of a life. A harvest that started
with a head.
