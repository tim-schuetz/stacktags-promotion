VIDEO SCRIPT
Title options (pick one): How emperors created and deleted characters on a whim
Topic: Learn Chinese
Target length: ~100 seconds (fits the 30s–3min window)
Language of narration: English
Format: Spoken voiceover narration + [bracket] visual-direction cues (cues are stripped before TTS).

==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
Rulers reshaped the written language by decree. (1) DELETE — the naming taboo (避讳):
characters in an emperor's personal name were forbidden. Tang Emperor Taizong = Li
Shimin (李世民), so the bodhisattva 觀世音 (Guanshiyin) dropped the 世 → 觀音 (Guanyin),
the name of the goddess of mercy to this day. (2) CREATE — Wu Zetian, China's only
female emperor, invented new "Zetian characters," most famously her own name 曌 (zhào)
= sun 日 + moon 月 over sky 空. Learn: 避讳; 觀世音→觀音; 曌.

NOTE (honesty): the Guanyin renaming is the traditional, widely-cited explanation
(naming taboo). Most of Wu Zetian's characters fell out of use except 曌.

==================================================================
VISUAL SYSTEM (read first — applies to every scene)
==================================================================
- White background + thin grey checkered grid (stk-grid-bg), Inter + Noto Sans SC for
  CJK, brand colours white / turquoise (#35A292 / #119271). No borders.
- PERSISTENT HERO = the imperial SEAL of decree: a turquoise rounded-square chop (印,
  NOT red) that presses down from the top. DELETE (a character crumbles away) and CREATE
  (a new character writes itself on) bind the video.
- Chinese is rendered from real per-stroke vector data (makeHanzi: write-on / shed) so
  characters animate; the FIGURES are REAL generated images — classical Chinese court
  portraits (Tang Taizong, Wu Zetian) and a Guanyin statue — cut out + turquoise dashed
  outline. (No flat vector people, no generic stock photos.)
- Subtitles mirror the narration exactly (grey, key words turquoise). On-screen TEXT is
  kept to taught vocab + short example words + pinyin/gloss + name cards.

SCRIPT

--- HOOK (0:00–0:08) — the payoff up front ---

[SCENE 1 — HOOK / establish the seal. Open on a single hanzi in a 田字格 cell.]
In imperial China, a ruler could delete a word from the language —
[The turquoise imperial SEAL presses down; on "delete" the character crumbles away
(shedAll), leaving an empty cell. Grid kick + swoosh.]
or invent a brand-new one —
[The seal presses again; a brand-new character writes itself on (writeOn). Pop.]
just by decree.
[The seal settles — the recurring hero.]

--- DELETE: THE NAMING TABOO (≈0:08–0:46) ---

[SCENE 2 — THE RULE. Zoom-out transition.]
For centuries, there was a strict rule called the naming taboo — 避讳.
[避讳 writes on big, pinyin "bìhuì" + gloss "naming taboo" below.]
You were not allowed to write the characters that appeared in the emperor's personal
name.
[The seal hovers over a sample character = "forbidden".]
Out of respect, they had to be avoided, swapped, or left with a missing stroke.
[SHOW the mechanism: the sample character loses ONE stroke (shed a single stroke) and
sits visibly incomplete.]
The emperor's name literally edited the language.
[The seal presses once more (edit motion).]

[SCENE 3 — TAIZONG + example words. Real court portrait of Tang Taizong (cut-out, dashed).]
Take the most famous case. The Tang emperor Taizong was named Li Shimin — 李世民 —
[Taizong portrait (top) + name card 李世民. Each character is an everyday character —
example-word chips cascade in and STAY: 李 → 李子 ("plum"), 世 → 世界 ("world"),
民 → 人民 ("people"); the shared character in each is turquoise.]
and his name contained the character 世, shì.
[世 in 李世民 lights up turquoise (pinyin "shì"); the 世 in 世界 pulses too — it is the
forbidden one.]

[SCENE 4 — GUANYIN RENAME = the LAST example. Real Guanyin statue (cut-out, dashed) below.]
Now, there was a hugely beloved figure of Buddhist mercy, called 觀世音 — Guanshiyin.
[觀世音 writes on as the final example chip (pinyin "Guānshìyīn", gloss "goddess of
mercy") — another famous word carrying the forbidden 世.]
But her name contained that forbidden 世.
[Its middle 世 lights up as the same forbidden glyph (visual rhyme with the others).]
So it was dropped.
[The middle 世 crumbles away (shedAll).]
觀世音 became 觀音 — Guanyin.
[觀 and 音 slide together into 觀音; pinyin morphs "Guānshìyīn" → "Guānyīn".]
And the shortened name stuck.
To this day, the goddess of mercy worshipped across China is called Guanyin —
[The Guanyin statue scales up / glows — still worshipped today.]
renamed, essentially, by an emperor's name, over a thousand years ago.

--- CREATE: AN EMPRESS'S CHARACTERS (≈0:46–1:22) ---

[SCENE 5 — WU ZETIAN. Real court portrait of Wu Zetian (cut-out, dashed) + name 武則天.]
And it ran the other way, too — rulers could invent characters.
You might wonder: there was a female emperor?
[Wu Zetian portrait enters; a playful "?" beat.]
Yes — and it was the only one in China's five-thousand-year history of dynasties: Wu
Zetian.
[Name card 武則天 writes on, "Wu Zetian" caption.]
And as women are creative in design, she used her powers to invent a whole set of new
characters.
[A few of her real invented characters (圀, 埊, 瞾, …) pop on across the TOP of the frame
and STAY there — her "whole set". On-screen only, not spoken.]

[SCENE 6 — 曌 ASSEMBLY appears BELOW the row of inventions.]
The most famous was her own name: 曌, zhào —
[Below the row of inventions: 日 (sun) + 月 (moon) dock as the upper half, 空 (sky) writes
on below; tiny glosses sun / moon / sky.]
built from the characters for sun and moon, shining over the sky.
[The parts merge into the single glyph 曌 (static Noto SC), pinyin "zhào".]
"Sun and moon in the heavens."
[Gloss: "曌 — sun & moon over the sky".]
A character she designed, for herself.
[The seal stamps 曌 = minted.]
Most of her inventions vanished after she died —
[The whole top row of invented characters now crumbles away (shedAll); only 曌 remains.]
but the idea behind them was very real: the writing itself was the ruler's to shape.
[曌 holds, the seal hovers above it.]

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:22–1:36) ---

[SCENE 7 — PUNCHLINE. The enduring Guanyin.]
And sometimes, like with Guanyin, that royal edit is still on everyone's lips,
[The Guanyin statue returns, centred + prominent, with 觀音 / Guānyīn beneath it — the
edit that's still alive today.]
a thousand years later.
