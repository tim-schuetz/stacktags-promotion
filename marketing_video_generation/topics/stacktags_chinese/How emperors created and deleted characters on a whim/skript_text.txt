VIDEO SCRIPT
Title options (pick one): How emperors created and deleted characters on a whim
Topic: Learn Chinese
Target length: ~105 seconds (fits the 30s–3min window)
Language of narration: English
Format: Spoken voiceover narration + [bracket] visual-direction cues (cues are stripped before TTS).

==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
Rulers reshaped the written language by decree. (1) DELETE — the naming taboo (避讳):
characters in an emperor's personal name were forbidden. Tang Emperor Taizong = Li
Shimin (李世民), so the bodhisattva 觀世音 (Guanshiyin) dropped the 世 → 觀音 (Guanyin),
the name of the goddess of mercy to this day. (2) CREATE — Wu Zetian, China's only
female emperor, invented new "Zetian characters," most famously her own name 曌 (zhào)
= sun 日 + moon 月 over sky 空. Learn: 避讳; 觀世音→觀音; 曌.
Punchline: for much of history the language belonged to whoever sat on the throne.

NOTE (honesty): the Guanyin renaming is the traditional, widely-cited explanation
(naming taboo) — say "largely." Most of Wu Zetian's characters fell out of use except
曌.

==================================================================
VISUAL SYSTEM (read first — applies to every scene)
==================================================================
- White background + thin grey checkered grid (stk-grid-bg), Inter + Noto Sans SC for
  CJK, brand colours white / turquoise (#35A292 / #119271). No borders.
- PERSISTENT HERO = the imperial SEAL of decree: a turquoise rounded-square chop (印,
  NOT red) that presses down from the top (auto-retracts). It does exactly two things
  that bind the whole video: DELETE (a character crumbles away, stroke by stroke) and
  CREATE (a new character writes itself on, stroke by stroke). Every act is one of these.
- All Chinese is rendered from real per-stroke vector data (makeHanzi: write-on / shed)
  so characters animate; specific rulers are flat vector figures (figSVG), turquoise
  accent + headgear. NO realistic stock-photo people.
- Subtitles mirror the narration exactly (grey, key words turquoise). On-screen TEXT is
  kept to a minimum: ONLY the taught vocab (避讳, 觀世音/觀音, 曌, 日/月/空), their pinyin
  and a short gloss, plus name cards. No floating word-pills that merely re-state the VO.
- Swoosh SFX only when the grid actually moves / a default element animates; pop per
  character/object appearing.

SCRIPT
==================================================================

--- HOOK (0:00–0:16) — the payoff up front ---

[SCENE 1 — HOOK / establish the seal. Open on a single hanzi resting in a 田字格 cell on
the grid.]
In imperial China, a ruler could delete a word from the language —
[The turquoise imperial SEAL presses down from the top; on the word "delete" the
character crumbles away stroke by stroke (shedAll), leaving an empty cell. Grid kicks +
swoosh.]
or invent a brand-new one —
[The seal presses again; a brand-new character writes itself on, stroke by stroke
(writeOn). Pop on the strokes.]
just by decree.
[The seal settles; a small "印 / by decree" feel — seal stays as the recurring hero.]
One emperor's name quietly renamed China's most beloved goddess forever.
[Quick teaser card slides in (ghosted): a small goddess silhouette + the glyph 觀音.]
And an empress designed a character for herself that still exists today.
[Second teaser card slides in beside it: an empress silhouette + the glyph 曌. Both fade
as we dive into Act 1.]

--- DELETE: THE NAMING TABOO (≈0:16–0:52) ---

[SCENE 2 — THE RULE. Zoom-out transition.]
For centuries, there was a strict rule called the naming taboo — 避讳.
[避讳 writes on big (stroke animation), pinyin "bìhuì" + gloss "naming taboo" below.]
You were not allowed to write the characters that appeared in the emperor's personal
name.
[The seal hovers over a sample character = "forbidden".]
Out of respect, they had to be avoided, swapped, or left with a missing stroke.
[SHOW the mechanism: on "missing stroke" the sample character loses ONE stroke (shed a
single stroke) and sits there visibly incomplete — not just stated, demonstrated.]
The emperor's name literally edited the language.
[The seal presses over the character once more (edit motion).]

[SCENE 3 — TAIZONG. Drop-in transition.]
Take the most famous case. The Tang emperor Taizong was named Li Shimin — 李世民 —
[Emperor figure (figSVG, cap, teal accent) enters; name card 李世民 writes on, "Li
Shimin / Tang Taizong" caption.]
and his name contained the character 世, shì.
[The middle character 世 lifts out / pulses turquoise; pinyin "shì" appears under it. The
seal stamps 世 as forbidden (a turquoise "taboo" mark).]

[SCENE 4 — GUANYIN RENAME. The key transformation.]
Now, there was a hugely beloved figure of Buddhist mercy, called 觀世音 — Guanshiyin.
[觀世音 writes on (3 glyphs), pinyin "Guānshìyīn", gloss "goddess of mercy".]
But her name contained that forbidden 世.
[The middle 世 lights up as the same forbidden glyph from Scene 3 (visual rhyme).]
So it was dropped.
[The middle 世 crumbles away (shedAll) and is gone.]
觀世音 became 觀音 — Guanyin.
[觀 and 音 slide together into 觀音; pinyin morphs "Guānshìyīn" → "Guānyīn".]
And the shortened name stuck.
To this day, the goddess of mercy worshipped across China is called Guanyin —
[OPTIONAL anti-slop anchor: a real, cut-out Guanyin statue photo (dashed outline) rises
at the lower frame — authentic artifact, cuttable. Otherwise keep 觀音 + a soft halo.]
renamed, essentially, by an emperor's name, over a thousand years ago.

--- CREATE: AN EMPRESS'S CHARACTERS (≈0:52–1:24) ---

[SCENE 5 — WU ZETIAN. The seal "flips" from delete- to create-mode (grid kick).]
And it ran the other way, too — rulers could invent characters.
[The seal mints a tiny new glyph to signal the mode flip.]
The boldest case: Wu Zetian, the only woman ever to rule China as emperor in her own
right.
[Empress figure (figSVG, distinct headdress, teal) enters; name card 武則天 writes on,
"Wu Zetian" caption. Quick beat: a row of ink emperor silhouettes, only hers is teal —
the lone woman.]
She created a whole set of brand-new characters.

[SCENE 6 — 曌 ASSEMBLY. Component build = the create counterpart to the rename.]
The most famous was her own name: 曌, zhào —
[Component assembly: 日 (sun) writes on top-left, 月 (moon) writes on top-right (they dock
as the upper half), 空 (sky) writes on below. Tiny glosses 日 sun / 月 moon / 空 sky.]
built from the characters for sun and moon, shining over the sky.
[The three parts merge/scale into the single glyph 曌 (static Noto SC for the unified
form). Pinyin "zhào".]
"Sun and moon in the heavens."
[Gloss settles: "曌 — sun & moon over the sky".]
A character she designed, for herself.
[The seal stamps 曌 = minted. Gloss "she designed it for herself".]
Most of her inventions vanished after she died —
[SCENE 7: several of her invented glyphs appear in a row, then most crumble away
(shedAll); only 曌 remains standing.]
but the idea behind them was very real: the writing itself was the ruler's to shape.
[曌 holds, the seal hovers above it.]

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:24–1:40) ---

[SCENE 8 — PUNCHLINE. The seal/throne motif centred; dual recap.]
We tend to think a language belongs to its people.
For much of Chinese history, it also belonged to whoever sat on the throne —
someone who could erase a character, or mint one, with a word.
[Dual recap around the seal: on "erase" 世 dims out on the left; on "mint" 曌 glows on
the right.]
And sometimes, like with Guanyin, that royal edit is still on everyone's lips,
[觀音 / Guanyin returns to centre and pulses — still here.]
a thousand years later.

Wanna actually start learning Chinese?
