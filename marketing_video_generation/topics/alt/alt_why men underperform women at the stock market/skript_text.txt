Title: why men underperform women at the stock market
Topic: Investing / behavioral finance
Target length: ~95 seconds (fits the 30s–3min window)
Language of narration: English
Format: Voiceover script. Spoken lines are plain text. [Bracketed lines] describe
what is on screen AT THE SAME TIME as the narration next to them.

==================================================================
THE CORE FACTS (source: Barber & Odean, landmark studies of US
individual investors in the 1990s — present as a classic, NOT as fresh data)
==================================================================
- Disposition effect: investors realize GAINS ~50% more often than LOSSES
  → tax-wise exactly backwards (selling winners triggers tax; harvesting
  losses would save it).
- Not a rookie thing: even professional funds do it ~21% more often.
- The most active 20% of traders earned ~11.4%/yr NET vs ~18.5% for buy-and-hold.
- Men's turnover ~80%/yr vs women's ~50% → men trade ~45% more → underperform.
- KEY HONESTY POINT: the gap is COST-driven (net returns). Their stock PICKS
  weren't worse — the trading costs were.

Recurring visual device — "the two lines":
A busy "Trader" portfolio line vs a calm "Buy-and-Hold" line. Every trade takes
a tiny fee-bite out of the Trader's line; the calm line just keeps climbing.

==================================================================
SCRIPT
==================================================================

--- HOOK (0:00–0:12) — the payoff lands in the first 5 seconds ---

Men underperform women at the stock market — and it's not because they pick
worse stocks. It's because they trade too much.
[Split screen: a "Men" line and a "Women" line; the women's line finishes higher.]
The more you trade, the more you quietly bleed to costs — every single time.
[Each trade on the men's line takes a tiny fee-bite out of it.]
In one of the most famous studies in finance, the investors who traded the most
badly lost to the ones who did almost nothing.
[A busy line and a calm line; the calm one pulls ahead.]
And no — this isn't a rookie mistake. Even professional fund managers fall for
it. Here's the psychology.

--- WHY #1: THE DISPOSITION EFFECT (≈0:12–0:42) ---

It starts with one deeply human glitch: we love selling our winners, and we
can't let go of our losers.
[A portfolio: green "winner" stocks get sold fast; red "loser" stocks get clutched.]
Across tens of thousands of US investors, people cashed in their gains about
50% more often than their losses — locking in what's working, babysitting what
isn't.

And here's the kicker: tax-wise, that's exactly backwards.
[A "TAX BILL" stamp hits the sold winner; the held loser glows "missed deduction".]
Selling a winner hands you a tax bill; selling a loser would actually save you
money. We do the opposite — on pure instinct.

Think the pros are above this? They're not.
[A "PROFESSIONAL FUNDS" badge appears over the same behavior.]
Even professional funds realize their gains about 21% more often than their
losses. It's wired in.

--- WHY #2: OVERTRADING & COST (≈0:42–1:08) ---

Now stack the second mistake on top: overtrading.
[Back to the two lines.]
Every trade costs — fees, spreads, taxes — and those tiny leaks compound. In
that 1990s US data, the most active 20% of investors earned just 11.4% a year.
[11.4% drops onto the busy line.]
Buy-and-hold investors? Over 18%.
[18.5% drops onto the calm line, far above.]
Same market. The difference wasn't worse stock-picking — their picks were about
as good. It was the cost of all that activity.
[Source line appears small: "Barber & Odean."]

--- WHY #3: THE MEN-VS-WOMEN GAP (≈1:08–1:28) ---

Which brings us back to the title.
[Two turnover gauges fill up: Men ~80%, Women ~50%.]
Men churned their portfolios around 80% a year; women, around 50%. Men traded
roughly 45% more — fueled by overconfidence — and that extra activity is exactly
what dragged their net returns below women's. Less trading, more keeping.

--- OUTRO / CTA (≈1:28–1:40) ---

So the uncomfortable secret of the market: doing less beats doing more.
[The calm buy-and-hold line quietly overtakes the busy one for good.]
The best investors aren't the busiest — they're the most patient.
