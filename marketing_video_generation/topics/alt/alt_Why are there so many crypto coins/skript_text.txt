VIDEO SCRIPT
Title: Why are there so many crypto coins?
Topic: Crypto
Target length: ~105 seconds (fits the 30s–3min window)
Language of narration: English
Format: Voiceover script. Spoken lines are plain text. [Bracketed lines] describe
what is on screen AT THE SAME TIME as the narration next to them.

==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
There are now millions of crypto coins because each wave fixed a limit of the last
AND made minting new coins easier:
1. Bitcoin — great money, but slow and can basically only do one thing.
2. Ethereum — added "smart contracts," so blockchains could run apps; anyone could
   create a token in a few lines of code.
3. Rival chains (Solana, BNB, etc.) — launched to be faster/cheaper than a
   congested Ethereum; each brings its own coin.
4. Memecoin explosion — platforms like Pump.fun let anyone mint a token in seconds
   for almost nothing → millions of joke/speculative coins.
Punchline: crypto kept fixing its own limits, and each fix made it easier to spit
out the next coin.

NOTE (honesty): the vast majority of these coins have no real use and are highly
speculative or outright scams — quantity isn't quality.

KEY VISUALS:
- A timeline/tree: 1 Bitcoin → Ethereum branching into many tokens → rival chains →
  an explosion of memecoins flooding the screen.

==================================================================
SCRIPT
==================================================================

--- HOOK (0:00–0:12) — the payoff up front ---

There are now millions of different cryptocurrencies. Not thousands — millions.
[A counter spinning up into the millions; coins flooding the screen.]
And it all started because the very first one, Bitcoin, was simply too slow.
[A single Bitcoin, moving sluggishly.]

--- WAVE 1: BITCOIN (≈0:12–0:32) ---

Bitcoin was the original — brilliant as digital money. But it's limited. It's slow,
and it can basically do just one thing: move bitcoin.
[Bitcoin doing one job, a single rail.]
You can't really build anything on top of it. So people wanted more.

--- WAVE 2: ETHEREUM (≈0:32–0:55) ---

That's where Ethereum came in. It added "smart contracts" — little programs that
run on the blockchain itself.
[Ethereum as a platform; small app-blocks running on it.]
Suddenly a blockchain could host apps, not just payments. And crucially, anyone
could create their own token on top of Ethereum with just a few lines of code.
[A token minted from a snippet of code; then several; then many.]
The floodgates cracked open.

--- WAVE 3: THE RIVALS (≈0:55–1:18) ---

Then Ethereum got so popular it became slow and expensive to use.
[Ethereum congested, fees rising.]
So a wave of rival blockchains launched, each promising to be faster or cheaper —
Solana, BNB, and many more. And every single one comes with its own coin.
[Competing chains light up, each with its own token.]

--- WAVE 4: THE MEMECOIN FLOOD (≈1:18–1:40) ---

And finally, it became trivial to make a coin at all. Platforms like Pump.fun let
anyone launch a token in seconds, for almost nothing.
[A "mint" button; tokens pouring out by the thousand.]
The result: a flood of memecoins — coins based on jokes, dogs, celebrities — most
with no purpose beyond speculation, and most worthless within days.
[A tidal wave of joke coins; most fading to zero.]

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:40–1:54) ---

So why are there millions of coins? Because crypto kept fixing its own
limitations —
[The tree: each fix branching into more.]
and each fix made it easier for anyone to mint the next one. We went from one
careful experiment... to a button that spits out a new currency in seconds. Just
remember: almost none of them are worth anything.
