==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
Ctrl+Alt+Delete was created ~1981 by IBM engineer David Bradley as an INTERNAL
developer shortcut to quickly reboot the IBM PC during testing — never meant for end
users. He deliberately made it three keys, spread apart, so it couldn't be pressed
by accident. It leaked out and became iconic; Microsoft later made it the Windows
login/management combo (Bill Gates reportedly regretted it).
Punchline: the world's most famous shortcut was a backstage tool, built to be
awkward, that accidentally became the front door to the PC.

NOTE (accuracy): Bradley's quip — "I may have invented it, but Bill made it famous."
Keys were positioned so two hands / deliberate effort were needed (no accidents).

KEY VISUALS:
- The three keys lighting up far apart on a keyboard; two hands needed to press them.
- A "developers only" backstage door; the combo slipping out to a billion users.

--- HOOK (0:00–0:12) — the payoff up front ---

The most famous keyboard shortcut on Earth — Control, Alt, Delete — was never meant
for you.
[The three keys glowing on a keyboard.]
It was a secret developer hack. And the keys were placed far apart on purpose.
Here's the story.
[Two hands stretching to hit all three at once.]

--- THE ORIGIN (≈0:12–0:38) ---

Rewind to around 1981, when IBM was building the very first PC. Engineers needed a
fast way to restart the computer during testing — without the slow, full
power-off-and-on.
[A developer waiting impatiently for a full reboot.]
So an engineer named David Bradley wired up a hidden shortcut: press Control, Alt,
and Delete together, and the machine instantly reboots.
[The combo pressed; the PC snaps into a quick restart.]
It was a tool for developers — a quick reset hidden inside the keyboard. Regular
users weren't supposed to know it existed.

--- THE DELIBERATE AWKWARDNESS (≈0:38–1:00) ---

And Bradley made it awkward on purpose. He chose three keys, spread apart, so you'd
need two hands and real intent to hit them.
[The three keys highlighted at distant corners; one-handed press = impossible.]
The whole point was that you could never trigger a reboot by accident — never just
rest your hands on the keyboard and wipe out your work. The clumsiness was a safety
feature.

--- IT ESCAPED (≈1:00–1:20) ---

But the secret got out. The combo became widely known — and then Microsoft made it
the way you log in and manage Windows.
[A "developers only" door bursting open; the combo flooding out to millions of PCs.]
A developer's hidden reset button was suddenly placed in front of a billion users.
Bill Gates later admitted that was a mistake. As Bradley himself joked: "I may have
invented Control-Alt-Delete — but Bill Gates made it famous."

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:20–1:32) ---

So the most iconic shortcut in computing was really an inside joke that got loose —
[The three keys, glowing, center screen.]
a backstage tool, built to be deliberately awkward, that accidentally became the
front door to the PC.
