==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
Recommendation systems predict what you'll like, mainly via (1) collaborative
filtering ("people similar to you liked X") and (2) content-based filtering ("this
item is like what you already liked"), trained on your behavior (clicks, watch
time, skips). Modern versions turn users and items into numeric "embeddings" and
match the closest ones at huge scale. Honest catch: they optimize for engagement,
not your wellbeing.
Punchline: the algorithm doesn't understand you — it knows your patterns, and the
millions of people who share them.

KEY VISUALS:
- A grid of users; "taste twins" highlighted; an item one twin liked sliding to you.
- Items turning into dots in a "similarity space," you matched to the nearest dots.

SCRIPT

--- HOOK (0:00–0:12) — the payoff up front ---

Ever feel like the app knows you better than your friends do?
[A feed scrolling, eerily perfect picks.]
That's a recommendation algorithm. And its core trick is simpler than you think:
it doesn't actually understand you at all. It just notices who you're similar to.
[The viewer surrounded by look-alike "taste twin" profiles.]

--- THE JOB (≈0:12–0:30) ---

The problem it solves is overload. There are millions of videos, products, songs —
and you'll only ever want a handful. The algorithm's only job is to guess which
handful.
[Millions of thumbnails; a spotlight narrowing to a few.]
And it mostly does that in two ways.

--- WAY 1: PEOPLE LIKE YOU (≈0:30–0:58) ---

The big one is called collaborative filtering. The system finds users who behaved
like you — same things watched, liked, skipped.
[The viewer grouped with their "taste twins."]
Then, if thousands of those taste-twins loved something you haven't seen yet — it
shows it to you. "People like you liked this."
[A video one twin loved slides over to the viewer.]
Notice: it doesn't need to know what that video is about. It just needs to know
who shares your taste.

--- WAY 2: THINGS LIKE WHAT YOU LIKED (≈0:58–1:20) ---

The second way looks at the items themselves. If you've watched a pile of cooking
videos, it finds more with similar tags — same topic, creator, style.
[Cooking videos clustering together; new similar ones pulled in.]
"This is like what you already enjoyed." And every signal feeds it — what you
click, how long you watch, what you skip, what you replay. Watch time most of all.
Just by using it, you're constantly training it.
[Each tap and watch-bar feeding back into the model.]

--- UNDER THE HOOD + THE CATCH (≈1:20–1:45) ---

Modern systems do this with machine learning — turning you and every item into a
long list of numbers, and matching you to the closest items in that space, at
massive scale, updating in real time.
[Users and items become dots; the viewer snaps to the nearest cluster.]
But here's the honest catch: it's optimizing for engagement — whatever keeps you
watching — which isn't always what's good for you. That's why a feed can feel
addictive, or pull you toward the extreme.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:45–1:58) ---

So the algorithm doesn't know you.
[The viewer at the center of a web of look-alikes.]
It knows your patterns — and the millions of people who share them. Predicting
what you'll want next is, at heart, just very fast pattern-matching.
