VIDEO SCRIPT
Title: How modern hacking works — 3 ways into a system
Topic: Software Development / security awareness
Target length: ~110 seconds (fits the 30s–3min window)
Language of narration: English
Format: Voiceover script. Spoken lines are plain text. [Bracketed lines] describe
what is on screen AT THE SAME TIME as the narration next to them.

==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
Real breaches rarely look like the movies. Most use three reliable doors:
(1) Social engineering / phishing — trick a human; (2) Unpatched vulnerabilities —
exploit software people forgot to update; (3) Stolen / reused credentials — leaked
passwords tried everywhere. Frame: AWARENESS & DEFENSE — explain how attacks
happen and how to shut each door. NOT an operational how-to.
Punchline: hackers rarely "break in" — they log in. And the same three doors are
the ones you can lock.

NOTE (responsibility): keep it conceptual + defensive. No step-by-step exploit
instructions — focus on the pattern and the fix for each.

KEY VISUALS:
- A house with three doors labeled "Phishing," "Unpatched," "Reused passwords";
  each one being shut with a lock as the defense is named.

==================================================================
SCRIPT
==================================================================

--- HOOK (0:00–0:12) — the payoff up front ---

Most hacking looks nothing like the movies — no glowing green code, no genius
typing furiously in a hoodie.
[A "Hollywood hacker" image gets crossed out.]
Real attackers almost always get in the same three boring ways. And the number one
way isn't even technical — it's tricking a person.
[Three doors appear on a building: "Phishing," "Unpatched," "Reused passwords."]

--- WAY 1: TRICK A HUMAN (≈0:12–0:42) ---

The most common door is social engineering — phishing. Instead of breaking the
lock, you get someone to open it for you.
[A fake "urgent" email; a hand reaching for the link.]
A message that looks completely legit — from your bank, your boss, your IT team —
nudging an employee to click a link, type their password, or approve a login they
shouldn't. The weakest point in almost any system isn't the software. It's the
human.
[Door 1 swings open... then a lock snaps shut on it.]
The defense: slow down, verify who's really asking, and turn on multi-factor
login so one stolen password isn't enough.

--- WAY 2: THE UNLOCKED WINDOW (≈0:42–1:08) ---

The second door: unpatched software. Every program has bugs, and some are security
holes. When a fix — a patch — gets released, it basically announces the hole to the
world.
[A software "update available" notice ignored; a crack appearing in a window.]
So attackers race to hit everyone who hasn't updated yet. Old, un-updated systems
are just open windows waiting.
[Door 2 / window... then it's sealed shut.]
The defense is almost annoyingly simple: update. Patch fast. Those "install update"
nags are security, not nagging.

--- WAY 3: THE KEY UNDER THE MAT (≈1:08–1:34) ---

The third door: stolen and reused passwords. Billions of passwords have leaked in
past breaches, and people reuse them everywhere.
[A giant pile of leaked passwords; the same one being tried on many sites.]
So attackers take a password leaked from one site and just try it on all your
other accounts — banking, email, work. A reused password is a key left under the
mat.
[Door 3... then locked.]
The defense: a unique password for everything — ideally a password manager — and
again, multi-factor login.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:34–1:48) ---

Notice what none of these needed: movie-style genius.
[The three locked doors side by side.]
Hackers rarely break in. They log in — with a trick, a missed update, or a
password you reused. The good news? Those same three doors are exactly the ones
you can lock.
