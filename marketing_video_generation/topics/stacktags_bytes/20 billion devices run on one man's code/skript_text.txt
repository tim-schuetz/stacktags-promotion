--- HOOK (0:00–0:14) — the payoff up front ---

About 20 billion devices run a piece of software called curl — your phone, your car,
your TV, the servers behind your favorite apps.
[A wall of countless devices, each quietly stamped "curl".]
And for decades, it's been kept alive mostly by one man. In 2026, artificial
intelligence nearly buried him in fake work. Here's the story.

--- WHAT CURL IS (≈0:14–0:38) ---

curl is a tiny, invisible tool whose job is simply to move data between a device and
the internet — fetching, sending, downloading.
[A little pipe shuttling data in and out of a device.]
It's so basic and so reliable that it got baked into roughly twenty billion devices
worldwide. You've never seen it, never clicked it — but you rely on it constantly,
every single day.

--- THE ONE MAN (≈0:38–1:02) ---

And for over 25 years, this backbone of the digital world has been led and
maintained largely by one developer: Daniel Stenberg. Often in his spare time. Often
for free.
[One person at a desk; the entire wall of devices balanced above him.]
It's the dirty secret of modern tech: enormous amounts of it stand on tiny,
unpaid, open-source projects — sometimes held up by a single volunteer. And in 2026,
that fragile setup met a brand-new threat.

--- THE AI FLOOD (≈1:02–1:32) ---

To keep curl safe, the project rewarded people for reporting real security bugs — a
"bug bounty." But it started drowning in AI-generated reports: confident,
official-looking write-ups of bugs that simply... didn't exist.
[An inbox flooding with slick "AI-generated vulnerability report" emails.]
Each fake looked plausible enough that a human had to stop and investigate it —
burning the exact time that keeps the software secure. Buried under the slop,
Stenberg shut the bug-bounty reporting down.
[The developer, exhausted, switching off the program.]
The tool securing billions of devices wasn't threatened by hackers. It was nearly
drowned by AI-generated noise.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:32–1:48) ---

It's a preview of a strange new problem. AI can now produce endless, plausible
nonsense — faster than humans can ever debunk it.
[A tide of fake reports rising against a few tired volunteers.]
And it's landing right on the small group of people quietly holding up the internet.
They used to just fight bugs. Now they fight an ocean of fake ones, written by
machines.
