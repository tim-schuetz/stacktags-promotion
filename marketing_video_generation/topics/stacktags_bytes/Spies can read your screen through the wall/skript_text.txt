==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
Screens (and most electronics) leak faint electromagnetic emissions as they work.
With the right equipment, an eavesdropper can pick up those stray signals from a
distance — even through a wall — and reconstruct what's on your screen, with no
cable, malware, or network access. This is "Van Eck phreaking" (Wim van Eck, 1985);
the defense standard is TEMPEST, used for shielded government equipment.
Punchline: your devices quietly broadcast their secrets into the air — and someone
might be listening.

NOTE (honesty): it needs specialized equipment + relative proximity; modern displays
leak less than old CRTs but the threat is real — that's why shielding exists.

KEY VISUALS:
- A monitor emitting faint radio waves; a receiver in the next room rebuilding the
  same image, ghostly, through the wall.
- A "TEMPEST-shielded" PC in a metal-lined room.

SCRIPT
==================================================================

--- HOOK (0:00–0:12) — the payoff up front ---

Your screen is secretly broadcasting everything on it — like a tiny radio station.
[A monitor with faint waves rippling off it into the air.]
And with the right equipment, someone in the next room could rebuild your screen —
through the wall — without ever touching your computer. Here's how.
[A receiver next door; a ghostly copy of the screen forming on it.]

--- THE LEAK (≈0:12–0:40) ---

Every electronic device gives off faint electromagnetic waves as it runs —
unintentional, invisible radio signals. And your monitor, as it draws each image,
leaks a signal that echoes exactly what it's showing.
[The screen's content rippling outward as waves.]
You can't see it. You can't feel it. But that signal is escaping into the air around
you, all the time.

--- THE ATTACK (≈0:40–1:05) ---

In the 1980s, a researcher named Wim van Eck proved you could catch those emissions
from a distance — with surprisingly simple gear — and reconstruct the picture on the
screen.
[A receiver tuning in; a fuzzy image sharpening into the victim's screen.]
No cable. No malware. No network connection at all. Just listening to the leak.
It's called Van Eck phreaking. And because radio waves pass through walls, the
eavesdropper doesn't even need to be in the room. The next office, or a van outside,
is enough.
[A van on the street; the target's screen mirrored inside.]

--- THE DEFENSE (≈1:05–1:25) ---

This threat is taken so seriously that governments shield their sensitive computers
and entire rooms against it — a set of standards known as TEMPEST.
[A metal-lined room; a shielded PC; the waves hitting the walls and dying.]
Special shielded machines, shielded walls, so the emissions simply can't escape.
That's how real this is: there are computers built specifically so they can't be
read through the wall.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:25–1:38) ---

You think your screen is private because nobody's looking over your shoulder.
[A person alone at their desk, feeling safe.]
But your devices are quietly shouting their secrets into the air — and sometimes,
someone is listening.
