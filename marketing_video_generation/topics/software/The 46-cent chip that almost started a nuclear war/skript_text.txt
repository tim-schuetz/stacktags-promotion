==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
In 1980, NORAD's computers showed a full-scale Soviet nuclear attack — thousands of
incoming missiles. The cause: a single faulty 46-cent computer chip that was
randomly inserting wrong numbers into the "number of missiles" field, flickering
between 2, 200, 2,000. The developer lesson: never blindly trust an input — validate
that it's even plausible.
Punchline: a half-dollar part nearly triggered the apocalypse, because no system was
asking "wait, is this number even possible?"

NOTE (honesty): the 46-cent faulty chip is the well-documented cause of the 1980
false alarms; humans caught it because the data didn't match other sensors. Keep
that — it's the whole "validate plausibility" point.

KEY VISUALS:
- A dark war room at 3 AM; a screen flashing "2,000 INCOMING."
- A tiny chip glowing red; the missile count glitching 2 → 200 → 2,000.

--- HOOK (0:00–0:14) — the payoff up front ---

In 1980, a computer told the United States that two thousand Soviet missiles were
in the air, headed straight for it.
[A dark war room; a red screen: "2,000 INCOMING."]
The cause wasn't a war, or a launch, or a hacker. It was a single broken chip worth
forty-six cents. Here's how a half-dollar part nearly ended the world.

--- THE ALARM (≈0:14–0:42) ---

Deep in NORAD's command center, the early-warning system suddenly lit up with a
full-scale nuclear attack. Every screen said the same thing: missiles inbound,
launched by the Soviet Union.
[Officers snapping to attention; klaxons; bombers being readied.]
This is the scenario the entire Cold War was terrified of — minutes to decide
whether to launch back. And the clock was running.

--- THE 46-CENT CULPRIT (≈0:42–1:08) ---

But something was off. The number of missiles kept... changing. One moment two.
Then two hundred. Then two thousand. Flickering, nonsensical.
[The missile count glitching wildly: 2 → 200 → 2,000 → 0.]
The whole thing was traced back to one tiny, failing computer chip — a part that
cost about forty-six cents.
[A circuit board; a single chip glowing red.]
It had started spitting out random numbers, and dumping them straight into the
field for "number of incoming missiles." The system simply printed whatever the
broken chip said — as gospel.

--- THE LESSON / PUNCHLINE (≈1:08–1:30) ---

And here's the part every engineer should burn into memory. The computer never
once asked the obvious question: "Is this number even plausible?"
[A validation gate appearing: "Does this make sense?" — that was missing.]
It trusted a single broken input, completely. What saved the world that day was
humans — noticing the data didn't match any other sensor, and refusing to believe
one impossible screen.
[Officers cross-checking other systems; standing down.]
The lesson outlives the Cold War: never blindly trust an input. Always check that
it's even possible. Because sometimes, the difference between a normal night and the
apocalypse is one cheap part — and whether anyone bothered to sanity-check it.
