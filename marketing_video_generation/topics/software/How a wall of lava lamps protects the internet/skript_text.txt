==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
Computers are deterministic — they can't produce TRUE randomness, only predictable
"pseudo-random" numbers. But strong encryption needs unpredictable randomness, or
keys become guessable. So Cloudflare films a wall of ~100 lava lamps in its lobby;
the chaotic, unpredictable wax (plus light, shadows, passersby) is turned into real
random data that helps seed cryptographic keys protecting a huge share of HTTPS
traffic.
Punchline: a piece of 1970s décor is real, serious security infrastructure.

NOTE (honesty): it's ONE entropy source among several (Cloudflare also uses a
pendulum, a radioactive source elsewhere) and it "helps seed" randomness — say
"helps." Keep the "half the web" claim vivid but fair (Cloudflare serves a big
slice of web traffic).

KEY VISUALS:
- A camera panning a glowing wall of lava lamps → the image becoming a stream of
  numbers → a padlock / encryption key.

--- HOOK (0:00–0:14) — the payoff up front ---

A big chunk of the secure, encrypted internet is protected by... a wall of lava
lamps.
[A camera pans across a glowing wall of ~100 lava lamps in an office lobby.]
This isn't decoration. It's one of the strangest, and most serious, pieces of
security infrastructure in the world. Here's why.

--- THE PROBLEM: COMPUTERS CAN'T BE RANDOM (≈0:14–0:42) ---

Encryption depends on randomness. To lock your data, a computer needs to generate
keys that nobody could ever guess.
[A padlock built from a long random key.]
But here's the catch: computers are terrible at being random. They're machines that
follow exact rules — so anything they "make up" actually follows a hidden pattern.
[A "random" number generator quietly repeating a pattern.]
And if an attacker can figure out that pattern, they can predict your keys — and
your encryption falls apart. So you need randomness from the real, messy, physical
world.

--- THE LAVA LAMP SOLUTION (≈0:42–1:10) ---

Which is where the company Cloudflare got creative. In their lobby sits a wall of
around a hundred lava lamps.
[The wax blobs drifting, splitting, merging — never the same twice.]
A camera films them constantly. And that slow, chaotic churn of wax — plus the
light, the shadows, even people walking past — is impossible to predict.
[The video feed turning into a torrent of unpredictable pixels.]
They turn that footage into a stream of genuinely random numbers, and feed it into
how their cryptographic keys are made — keys that help secure a huge slice of the
world's web traffic.
[Pixels → numbers → a freshly minted encryption key, padlock snapping shut.]

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:10–1:30) ---

So a slab of 1970s decor became real security infrastructure.
[The lamp wall, glowing, with a tiny "SECURE" padlock pulsing over it.]
Because the safest randomness isn't something a computer can invent — it has to be
borrowed from the chaos of the physical world. And it turns out, nothing is more
beautifully unpredictable than melting wax.
