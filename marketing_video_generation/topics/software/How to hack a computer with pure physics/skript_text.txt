==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
Rowhammer: a hardware attack. DRAM cells are packed so tightly that reading one row
extremely fast and repeatedly ("hammering" it) leaks electrical charge into
neighboring rows and FLIPS their bits — without ever accessing that data. Attackers
flip the right bits to grab privileges they shouldn't have. It's a physics effect,
not a code bug, so software patches can't fully fix it.
Punchline: most hacks exploit mistakes in code; Rowhammer exploits the laws of
physics — even perfect software can be broken by the hardware beneath it.

NOTE (responsibility): conceptual / awareness only — explain the principle, not a
working exploit.

KEY VISUALS:
- Rows of memory cells; one row hammered fast; a neighboring 0 flickering to 1.
- "No code needed" stamp; electricity leaking between rows.

--- HOOK (0:00–0:12) — the payoff up front ---

Here's a way to hack a computer without writing a single line of malicious code.
[A clean terminal — no virus, no exploit script.]
You just read its memory really, really fast — and let physics flip the bits for
you. It's called Rowhammer.
[Rows of memory cells; one starts pulsing rapidly.]

--- HOW MEMORY WORKS (≈0:12–0:38) ---

First, how your RAM stores data. It's billions of microscopic cells, each holding a
tiny electric charge — charged is a 1, empty is a 0.
[A grid of cells, some glowing (1), some dark (0).]
And to fit billions of them on a chip, they're packed unbelievably close together —
row, after row, after row, almost touching.
[Zoom in: dense rows crammed side by side.]
That tight packing is exactly the weakness.

--- THE HAMMER (≈0:38–1:05) ---

Because if you read one row over and over — thousands of times a second, hammering
it —
[One row flickering furiously; faint sparks jumping to its neighbors.]
the tiny electrical pulses start leaking into the rows right next to it. Do it
enough, and a bit in the neighboring row flips — a 0 quietly becomes a 1.
[A neighboring cell: 0 → 1, all on its own.]
And here's the wild part: you never touched that data. You're not even allowed to.
But by hammering next to it, you changed it anyway — purely through physics.

--- WHY IT'S DANGEROUS (≈1:05–1:25) ---

Attackers use those flips with surgical precision. Flip exactly the right bit, and
you can hand yourself permissions you should never have — breaking straight out of a
program's locked sandbox.
[A "permission: denied" flag; one flipped bit turns it to "granted".]
And because it's not a software mistake, you can't just patch it away. It's baked
into how densely modern memory is built. The tighter we pack the chips, the worse
it gets.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:25–1:38) ---

Most hacks exploit a mistake someone made in code.
[A bug in code, crossed out.]
Rowhammer exploits the laws of physics — proving that even flawless software can be
broken by the hardware it runs on.
