==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
"Harvest now, decrypt later": agencies record your encrypted traffic today —
unreadable now — betting that a future quantum computer will crack it. Today's
encryption relies on math that's brutally hard for normal computers (e.g. factoring
huge numbers). A quantum computer, using Shor's algorithm, could solve it fast.
Big-enough quantum machines don't exist yet, but data can wait — so the world is
racing to roll out "post-quantum" encryption.
Punchline: your encrypted data isn't safe just because no one can read it today —
some of it is being saved for a future that can.

NOTE (honesty): quantum computers powerful enough to break RSA do NOT exist yet;
timeline is uncertain (years, possibly a decade+). Say so. Keep quantum explanation
intuitive, not literal "tries all answers at once."

KEY VISUALS:
- An archive vault filling with locked, scrambled data labeled "can't read… yet."
- A quantum computer powering on; a giant padlock springing open.

==================================================================
SCRIPT
==================================================================

--- HOOK (0:00–0:14) — the payoff up front ---

Right now, intelligence agencies are stockpiling your encrypted data — even though
they can't read a single word of it.
[A vault filling with scrambled, locked files: "ENCRYPTED — unreadable."]
They're betting that in a few years, a quantum computer will crack it wide open.
It's a strategy with a chilling name: "harvest now, decrypt later."

--- TODAY'S LOCK (≈0:14–0:42) ---

Almost everything private online — your messages, your bank details — is protected
by encryption built on a math problem that's brutally hard for normal computers.
[A padlock made of a huge number splitting into two prime factors.]
Think of taking an enormous number and finding the two prime numbers that multiply
to make it. For a regular computer, cracking one strong key by brute force could
take longer than the age of the universe.
[A normal computer grinding away, a "billions of years" timer.]
That impossibility is the whole reason your data is safe.

--- THE QUANTUM THREAT (≈0:42–1:15) ---

But a quantum computer doesn't work like a normal one. A normal computer checks
possibilities essentially one after another. A quantum computer uses the strange
rules of quantum physics to evaluate an enormous number of paths together —
[A maze: a normal computer trying one route at a time; a quantum one lighting up many at once.]
and there's a known method, called Shor's algorithm, that lets a powerful enough
quantum machine solve that prime-factoring problem fast. The lock that would take a
normal computer eons could fall in hours.
[The giant padlock springing open in seconds.]
Now, the honest part: quantum computers big enough to do this don't exist yet. They
may be years — even a decade or more — away.

--- WHY HARVEST IT NOW (≈1:15–1:38) ---

So why steal data you can't read? Because data can wait.
[The vault of scrambled files, sealed, labeled "open later."]
Agencies record your encrypted traffic today — pure gibberish to them now — and
just... store it. And the day a capable quantum computer finally switches on, they
unlock the entire archive at once. Your secrets from today, opened years in the
future.
[A future date; the whole vault clicking open.]
That's why the world is already racing to roll out "post-quantum" encryption — new
locks designed to survive even a quantum attack — before that day arrives.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:38–1:52) ---

So here's the unsettling truth: your encrypted data isn't necessarily safe just
because nobody can read it today.
[The vault, quietly filling.]
Some of it is being saved... for a future that can.
