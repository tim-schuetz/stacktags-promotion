==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
Caching = storing a copy of a result or data somewhere fast and close, so you
never have to fetch or compute it twice. It trades a little memory + the risk of
"stale" data for a huge speed gain. It's everywhere (CPU, browser, CDN, database).
The famous hard part: cache invalidation — knowing when the saved copy is out of
date.
Punchline: almost everything that feels "fast" on a computer is just work that was
done once, and remembered.

KEY VISUALS:
- First load: a slow trip to a distant database. Second load: pulled instantly
  from a nearby "cache" box.
- The "stale copy" problem: the original changes, the cached copy still shows the
  old value.

SCRIPT

--- HOOK (0:00–0:10) — the payoff up front ---

Why does a website load slowly the first time, then instantly the second?
[A page crawling in, then snapping in instantly on reload.]
One trick sits behind almost all computer speed: caching. It's basically the art
of being lazy — never doing the same work twice.
[A "cache" box catching a result and handing back the saved copy.]

--- THE IDEA (≈0:10–0:35) ---

Some things are slow to get: reading from a faraway database, rendering a page,
loading a big image.
[A long trip out to a distant database and back.]
So the first time you do it, you save a copy somewhere fast and nearby — a cache.
The next time, you grab the saved copy instead of redoing all that work.
[Second request stops at the nearby cache box — instant.]
You did the hard work once, and just... remembered the answer.

--- IT'S EVERYWHERE (≈0:35–0:58) ---

And this happens at every layer of computing. Your browser caches a site's images
so your second visit doesn't re-download them.
[Browser pulling images from local storage.]
Your processor keeps tiny caches of data it's about to reuse. Apps cache expensive
calculations. CDNs cache web content near you. Databases cache common queries.
[Cache boxes lighting up at CPU, app, CDN, and database levels.]
Speed, almost everywhere, is really just caching.

--- THE HARD PART (≈0:58–1:25) ---

But there's a catch — and it's famous. There's an old joke that there are only two
hard things in computer science: naming things... and cache invalidation.
["Two hard things: naming, and cache invalidation."]
Here's why. When the original data changes, your saved copy is suddenly wrong —
stale.
[The real value updates; the cached copy still shows the old number.]
Knowing exactly when to throw the old copy away is genuinely tricky. Serve a stale
price, or a stale account balance, and you've got a real bug.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:25–1:38) ---

So caching is a trade: a little memory, and the risk of staleness, in exchange for
a huge jump in speed.
[The fast cached path glowing next to the slow original path.]
Almost everything that feels instant on a computer is just something that was done
once — and remembered.
