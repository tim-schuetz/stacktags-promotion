==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
"512K Day" (Aug 2014): the internet's global routing table (BGP) crossed 512,000
routes. Many older routers had a hardwired memory limit (TCAM) of exactly 512K
routes — so they dropped routes or crashed, causing outages. Operators reconfigured
them. The table keeps growing ("768K Day" loomed next). Built-in explainer: how your
request to google.com actually finds Google's servers — routers share giant tables
(BGP) and hop your packet along them.
Punchline: the internet feels infinite, but it runs on real machines with real
memory — and every so often it outgrows its own map.

KEY VISUALS:
- A request hopping router to router, each holding a giant "map" of the internet.
- A memory bar filling to "512,000" and overflowing; routers flashing red.

SCRIPT
==================================================================

--- HOOK (0:00–0:12) — the payoff up front ---

In 2014, parts of the internet started crashing — and it wasn't an attack.
[Routers flashing red across a world map; sites blinking offline.]
The internet had simply grown too big for its own routers to remember. To get why,
you first need to know how your data ever finds anything.

--- HOW YOUR REQUEST FINDS GOOGLE (≈0:12–0:42) ---

When you type google.com, your request doesn't magically know where Google is.
[A little data packet, lost, asking "which way?"]
It hops from router to router across the internet. And every one of those routers
holds a giant map — a list of which chunks of the internet live in which direction.
[Each router holding a glowing "routing table" — a map of everywhere.]
That map is called the routing table, and it's how packets get passed along, hop by
hop, until they reach Google's servers. Every router has to know a route to
basically everywhere.

--- THE 512,000 WALL (≈0:42–1:12) ---

Here's the problem. Many older routers stored that map in a special kind of
super-fast memory — with a fixed size. Room for exactly 512,000 routes.
[A memory bar with a hard line at "512,000".]
For years, fine. The internet's map was smaller than that. But the internet kept
growing — more networks, more routes, every single day.
[The route count creeping upward toward the line.]
And in August 2014, it crossed 512,000. Routers that hadn't been updated hit their
ceiling — and started dropping routes, or crashing outright.
[The bar overflows; routers go red; chunks of the map go dark.]
Pieces of the internet simply blinked out.

--- IT KEEPS HAPPENING (≈1:12–1:32) ---

The fix was almost mundane: operators reconfigured their routers to make room for
more routes. Crisis handled.
[Engineers bumping the limit up; the map relights.]
But the table never stops growing. The next threshold — call it "768K Day" — came
looming right behind it. Every few years, the internet quietly outgrows another
limit, and someone has to raise the ceiling again.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:32–1:45) ---

The internet feels infinite.
[Pull back to the whole glowing network.]
But underneath, it's running on real machines, with real memory — and every so
often, it grows too big for its own map.
