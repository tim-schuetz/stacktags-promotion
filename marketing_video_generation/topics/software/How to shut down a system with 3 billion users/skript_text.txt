==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
On Oct 4, 2021, a faulty BGP routing update erased Facebook, Instagram and
WhatsApp from the internet for ~6 hours. The twist: the same network ran their
internal tools AND the building's electronic access — so engineers were locked out
of their own data centers and reportedly had to cut into the server cages to fix
it. The deeper lesson: don't let your recovery systems depend on the very thing
that's down (circular dependency).
Punchline: the company didn't get hacked — it locked itself out of its own servers.

NOTE (honesty): the "angle-grinder / cut the cages" detail is the popular retelling
— say "reportedly." Core facts (BGP withdrawal, locked-out engineers, ~6h, billions
of users) are solid.

KEY VISUALS:
- A technician with an angle grinder at a high-security server-room door; outside,
  3 billion users hammering refresh.
- A routing command wiping Facebook off a map of the internet.

--- HOOK (0:00–0:14) — the payoff up front ---

In 2021, Facebook didn't get hacked. It locked itself out of its own servers — and
took Instagram and WhatsApp down with it, for billions of people.
[A map of the internet; "Facebook" blinks out. Outside, a sea of users hitting refresh.]
For six hours, the company couldn't even get into the building to fix it. Here's
how you accidentally delete yourself from the internet.

--- HOW IT VANISHED (≈0:14–0:42) ---

It started with routine maintenance. An engineer pushed a single faulty update to
Facebook's routing — the system that tells the rest of the internet "this is where
to find us."
[A routing table; one bad command ripples outward.]
The update was wrong. In an instant, it withdrew the directions to Facebook's
servers worldwide.
[The signposts pointing to Facebook all vanish.]
So when 3 billion people typed in the address, the internet shrugged: it no longer
had any idea where Facebook was. The company had, essentially, erased its own home
address.

--- THE TRAP (≈0:42–1:12) ---

Now here's where it went from outage to nightmare. Facebook's internal tools —
the ones engineers would use to fix the problem — ran on that same network. So
they went down too.
[Engineers reaching for their tools; everything greyed out.]
Even the badge readers and electronic door locks at the data centers were tied to
it. So the people who could fix the servers... couldn't get to the servers.
[A locked high-security door; engineers stuck outside.]
Reportedly, a team had to physically cut their way into the server cages with power
tools just to reach the machines.
[A technician with an angle grinder at the cage; sparks flying.]

--- THE LESSON / PUNCHLINE (≈1:12–1:30) ---

And that's the real lesson hiding in here. They built a system where the tools to
fix a crash depended on the very thing that had crashed.
[A loop diagram: "to fix the network, you need the network."]
It's a circular trap — and the biggest companies fall into it. The most powerful
social network on Earth wasn't taken down by hackers, or a war, or a disaster. It
was one bad line of config — and a door that wouldn't open.
