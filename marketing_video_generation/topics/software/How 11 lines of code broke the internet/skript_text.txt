==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
In March 2016, a developer (angry over a name dispute) deleted his tiny npm
package "left-pad" — 11 lines that just pad text with spaces on the left. Because
countless tools depended on it (often without knowing), builds broke across the
industry — React, Babel, and projects at huge companies suddenly wouldn't build.
The lesson: the modern software supply chain is a tower of tiny dependencies; pull
one block and the tower wobbles.
Punchline: the internet runs on millions of tiny pieces written by strangers — and
any one of them can pull theirs out.

NOTE (honesty): the dramatic "Netflix/Spotify/PayPal went down" framing → say it
broke BUILDS for countless projects "from tiny apps to huge companies." npm later
banned un-publishing popular packages.

KEY VISUALS:
- A single domino labeled "left-pad" toppling a row of giant corporate logos.
- 11 trivial lines of code on screen.

--- HOOK (0:00–0:14) — the payoff up front ---

In 2016, one developer deleted eleven lines of code — and broke software for half
the industry overnight.
[A domino labeled "left-pad" tips over a row of huge company logos.]
Not a virus. Not a hack. Just one guy, deleting one tiny file out of anger. Here's
how something this small caused chaos this big.

--- WHAT LEFT-PAD WAS (≈0:14–0:38) ---

The package was called "left-pad." And its entire job was almost laughably simple:
take some text, and add spaces on the left to make it a certain length.
[Eleven trivial lines of code; "hi" → "   hi".]
That's it. Eleven lines. The kind of thing a beginner could write in a minute. But
that little package had quietly become a building block —

--- THE TOWER OF DEPENDENCIES (≈0:38–1:05) ---

— because modern software is built out of thousands of tiny shared pieces, stacked
on top of each other. You install one tool, and it quietly pulls in dozens of
smaller ones, which pull in dozens more.
[A tower of stacked blocks, each labeled with a package name.]
And buried somewhere deep in that tower, holding up giants like React and Babel,
was little left-pad.
[Zoom into the base of the tower: one block reads "left-pad".]
Most developers using it had no idea it was even there.

--- THE COLLAPSE (≈1:05–1:25) ---

Then the developer who made it got into a dispute, and in protest, deleted all his
packages — including left-pad.
[The "left-pad" block is yanked out of the tower.]
Instantly, that block was gone. And across the world, builds that secretly depended
on it just... stopped working — from hobby projects to massive companies. All
because eleven lines vanished.
[The whole tower wobbling; "BUILD FAILED" flashing everywhere.]

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:25–1:42) ---

That's the unsettling truth about modern software. The internet runs on millions of
tiny pieces, written and given away for free by strangers —
[The tower, every block a different anonymous author.]
and the whole tower is only standing because, usually, nobody pulls their block
out. One person, eleven lines, and a moment of anger — that's all it took.
