==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
The Caspian is the largest enclosed body of water on Earth — called a "sea," but
with no link to the ocean, it's technically the world's largest LAKE. Whether it
counts as a sea or a lake decided how its huge oil/gas reserves are split among 5
bordering countries, and they argued for almost 30 years. The 2018 deal: treat
the SURFACE like a sea, divide the SEABED like a lake.
Punchline: a billion-dollar, decades-long fight — over which noun to use.

KEY VISUALS:
- The Caspian ringed by 5 flags; oil rigs; a toggle "SEA?" / "LAKE?" redrawing the
  borders each way.

==================================================================
SCRIPT
==================================================================

--- HOOK (0:00–0:12) — the payoff up front ---

Is this a sea, or a lake? It's the biggest lake on Earth — but everyone calls it a
sea.
[The Caspian on a map; a label flickers between "SEA" and "LAKE".]
And that one word — sea or lake — was worth so much money it took five countries
nearly thirty years to settle.
[Five national flags around the shoreline; oil rigs dotting the water.]

--- THE DEFINITION (≈0:12–0:35) ---

The Caspian is the largest enclosed body of water in the world. We call it a sea —
but it has no connection to the ocean. And by definition, water locked inside the
land is a lake.
[The Caspian highlighted, sealed off from all oceans.]
So it's the world's largest lake... wearing the name of a sea. Which sounds like
a harmless technicality — until you look underneath it.

--- WHY IT WAS WORTH A FORTUNE (≈0:35–1:08) ---

Because beneath the Caspian sit enormous reserves of oil and gas. And how you
divide that wealth depends entirely on the label.
[Oil and gas glowing under the seabed.]
If it's a sea, the international law of the sea applies — each country gets zones
measured from its coastline. If it's a lake, the usual approach is to split the
whole thing more evenly between the bordering states.
[Toggle: "SEA" draws coastal wedges; "LAKE" draws equal slices — very different maps.]
Same water, two completely different maps — and very different shares of the oil.
So for almost three decades after the Soviet Union broke up, five countries fought
over that single definition.

--- THE CLEVER FIX (≈1:08–1:30) ---

Then, in 2018, they signed a compromise that split the difference — literally.
[A horizontal line slices the water from the seabed.]
Treat the surface like a sea: shared, open for everyone's ships and fishing. But
divide the seabed like a lake: carved into national sectors, each country keeping
the resources under its own patch.
[Top half glows "SEA"; bottom half divided into flagged "LAKE" sectors.]
Half sea, half lake.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:30–1:42) ---

A thirty-year, billion-dollar standoff —
[The five flags finally shaking hands over the water.]
not over land, not over borders, but over which noun to use: sea... or lake.
