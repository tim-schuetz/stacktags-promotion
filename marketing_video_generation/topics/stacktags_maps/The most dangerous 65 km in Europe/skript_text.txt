VIDEO SCRIPT
Title: The most dangerous 65 km in Europe
Topic: Geography / geopolitics
Target length: ~95 seconds (fits the 30s–3min window)
Language of narration: English
Format: Voiceover script. Spoken lines are plain text. [Bracketed lines] describe
what is on screen AT THE SAME TIME as the narration next to them.

==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
The Suwałki Gap: a ~65 km stretch of the Poland–Lithuania border, the ONLY land
link between the three Baltic states (Estonia, Latvia, Lithuania) and the rest of
NATO. It's pinched between the heavily militarized Russian exclave of Kaliningrad
on one side and Russia's ally Belarus on the other. If it were ever closed, the
Baltics would be cut off by land from their allies.
Punchline: the whole map can hinge on a strip of land you could drive across in
under an hour.

NOTE (honesty): frame as a strategic vulnerability / scenario ("if," "could") —
not a prediction. Length cited as "about 65 km."

KEY VISUALS:
- A map: the narrow gap glowing red between Kaliningrad and Belarus, the Baltics
  hanging off it like a thread.

==================================================================
SCRIPT
==================================================================

--- HOOK (0:00–0:12) — the payoff up front ---

This quiet strip of farmland — about 65 kilometers long — might be the most
dangerous piece of ground in Europe.
[A map zooms to a thin corridor on the Poland–Lithuania border; it pulses red.]
Because it's the only thing on land connecting three NATO countries to the rest
of the alliance. And it's wedged between two of Russia's doorsteps.
[Kaliningrad on the left, Belarus on the right, squeezing the corridor.]

--- THE SQUEEZE (≈0:12–0:42) ---

This is the Suwałki Gap, where Poland meets Lithuania. Look what's on either side.
[Camera pans the two flanks.]
To the west: Kaliningrad — a small, intensely militarized piece of Russia, cut
off from the rest of the country. To the east: Belarus — Russia's closest ally.
The gap is the narrow band of NATO land running between them.
[Two pincers, Kaliningrad and Belarus, almost touching across the strip.]

--- WHY IT MATTERS (≈0:42–1:15) ---

And here's why planners lose sleep over it. Estonia, Latvia and Lithuania are all
NATO members — but on land, they connect to the rest of the alliance through this
one thin corridor, by way of Poland.
[The three Baltics light up, linked to NATO by a single thread through the gap.]
Everything else around them is the sea, or territory aligned with Russia. So if
Kaliningrad and Belarus ever pushed together and sealed this gap —
[The two sides clamp shut; the thread snaps.]
three NATO countries would be severed from their allies by land, in an instant.
That's the nightmare these 65 kilometers represent.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:15–1:28) ---

Wars aren't always about vast territory.
[Pull back to the whole continent, the tiny gap still glowing.]
Sometimes the entire balance of a continent rests on a strip of land you could
drive across before your coffee gets cold.
