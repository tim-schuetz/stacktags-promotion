VIDEO SCRIPT
Title: Half of South Korea lives in one city
Topic: Demographics
Target length: ~100 seconds (fits the 30s–3min window)
Language of narration: English
Format: Voiceover script. Spoken lines are plain text. [Bracketed lines] describe
what is on screen AT THE SAME TIME as the narration next to them.

==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
The Greater Seoul area (Seoul + Incheon + Gyeonggi) covers only ~12% of South
Korea's land but holds OVER HALF its population (~26 million) and the lion's share
of its economy/exports (~70%). It's an even more extreme concentration than
Japan's Greater Tokyo (~1/3 of Japan). Driven by decades of centralized growth.
Punchline: South Korea is almost a city-state with a country attached.

NOTE (honesty): figures rounded — population "more than half," land "about 12%,"
exports "around 70%." Compare to Tokyo qualitatively.

KEY VISUALS:
- Map of South Korea: the Greater Seoul region (small) glowing, with a counter
  "50%+ of people / ~12% of land."
- Side-by-side with Japan's Tokyo share to show Korea is more extreme.

==================================================================
SCRIPT
==================================================================

--- HOOK (0:00–0:12) — the payoff up front ---

More than half of South Korea's entire population lives in and around a single
city.
[Map of South Korea; the Greater Seoul area lights up small and bright.]
On just about a tenth of the country's land, you'll find over 50% of its people —
and the overwhelming majority of its economy. It's one of the most extreme
concentrations of a nation anywhere on Earth.
[A counter: "~12% of the land — 50%+ of the people."]

--- THE NUMBERS (≈0:12–0:40) ---

The city is Seoul, together with its surrounding capital region — Incheon and
Gyeonggi province.
[The three areas merge into one glowing "Greater Seoul" blob.]
Together they cover only around 12% of South Korea's land. But they hold over half
its population — roughly 26 million people — and produce a huge share of the
country's output and exports, on the order of 70%.
[Bars fill: land ~12%, people 50%+, exports ~70%.]
Half the country, packed into a corner of the map.

--- EVEN BEYOND TOKYO (≈0:40–1:05) ---

And to put that in perspective: even Japan, famous for Tokyo swallowing the
country, isn't this lopsided.
[Greater Tokyo highlighted on Japan: "~1/3 of Japan."]
Greater Tokyo holds about a third of Japan's people. South Korea crams in a full
half. It's Tokyo's pull — taken even further.
[The two shares side by side; Korea's bar clearly taller.]

--- WHY, AND THE COST (≈1:05–1:30) ---

It happened by design. Decades of fast, centralized growth pulled the jobs, the
universities, the big companies all into the capital — and everyone followed. The
rest of the country quietly emptied out as the young moved to Seoul.
[Arrows of people streaming from the regions into Seoul; small towns dimming.]
It's efficient — and fragile. Brutal housing costs, fierce competition, and a
capital so dominant that one shock there shakes the whole nation.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:30–1:42) ---

Some countries have a capital city.
[Pull back to the whole peninsula, glowing at one point.]
South Korea is, more or less, a capital city — with a country attached.

Want more numbers that change how you see the world?
