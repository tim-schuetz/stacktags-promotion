VIDEO SCRIPT
Title: The Spanish town stranded inside France
Topic: Geography
Target length: ~85 seconds (fits the 30s–3min window)
Language of narration: English
Format: Voiceover script. Spoken lines are plain text. [Bracketed lines] describe
what is on screen AT THE SAME TIME as the narration next to them.

==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
Llívia: a Spanish town that's an exclave entirely surrounded by France (~1.5 km
past the border, in the Pyrenees). In the 1659 Treaty of the Pyrenees, Spain
ceded 33 "villages" to France — but Llívia was legally a "town," not a village,
so it was exempt and stayed Spanish while everything around it became French.
Punchline: a whole town kept its country over one word — "village" vs "town."

KEY VISUALS:
- A map: a small Spanish dot floating inside French territory, linked to Spain by
  a thin road across foreign soil.

==================================================================
SCRIPT
==================================================================

--- HOOK (0:00–0:10) — the payoff up front ---

This town is part of Spain — but it's completely surrounded by France.
[A map: a lone Spanish-flagged dot, ringed entirely by French territory.]
To drive from here to the rest of your own country, you have to cross through
another one. And the reason it's Spanish at all comes down to a single word in a
360-year-old treaty.
[An old treaty document; one word glows.]

--- THE HISTORY (≈0:10–0:38) ---

This is Llívia, high in the Pyrenees. In 1659, after a long war, Spain and France
signed the Treaty of the Pyrenees, and Spain agreed to hand over thirty-three
villages in this border region to France.
[Thirty-three little dots flip from Spanish to French across the map.]
One by one, the villages around Llívia became French. But Llívia... didn't.
[Every dot turns French — except one, which stays red-and-yellow.]

--- THE LOOPHOLE (≈0:38–1:05) ---

Because the treaty said villages. And Llívia, by an old royal status, wasn't
classed as a village. It was officially a town.
["VILLAGE" stamped on the others; "TOWN" stamped on Llívia, exempting it.]
So it slipped through the wording — and stayed Spanish, even as France closed in
on every side. Ever since, it's been a piece of Spain marooned a kilometer inside
France, reached only by a road across French land.
[The thin connecting road traced from Llívia back to the Spanish border.]

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:05–1:18) ---

Borders are usually decided by armies.
[Soldiers fade out; a quill signing the treaty fades in.]
This one was decided by grammar. An entire town kept its country because of one
word: not "village"... but "town."
