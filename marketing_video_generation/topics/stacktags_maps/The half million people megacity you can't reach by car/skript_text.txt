VIDEO SCRIPT
Title: The half million people megacity you can't reach by car
Topic: Geography
Target length: ~100 seconds (fits the 30s–3min window)
Language of narration: English
Format: Voiceover script. Spoken lines are plain text. [Bracketed lines] describe
what is on screen AT THE SAME TIME as the narration next to them.

==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
Iquitos, Peru: widely considered the largest city on Earth with NO road
connection — ~470,000 people, deep in the Amazon, reachable only by boat or
plane. It grew huge during the late-1800s rubber boom (European mansions, an
"iron house" linked to Eiffel), then stayed after the boom collapsed. Everything
arrives by river or air; streets run on three-wheeled moto-taxis.
Punchline: in an age where you can drive almost anywhere, here the road simply
never arrived.

NOTE (honesty):
- "Megacity" is loose hype (the title's hook) — in narration just say "a city of
  nearly half a million."
- Say "widely considered / often called" the largest city unreachable by road.
- The Eiffel "iron house" attribution is legend — hedge it ("said to be").
- One local road reaches the nearby town of Nauta but connects to nothing beyond.

KEY VISUALS:
- Map zoom into the Amazon basin: Iquitos as a lone dot in endless green, with NO
  road network reaching it.
- A plane and a slow, multi-deck riverboat (hammocks strung on deck).
- Rubber-boom mansions; swarms of three-wheeled moto-taxis.

==================================================================
SCRIPT
==================================================================

--- HOOK (0:00–0:10) — the payoff up front ---

There's a city of nearly half a million people that you cannot reach by car. No
highway connects it to the rest of the world — not one.
[A map; roads spider across a country, then all stop dead at a wall of jungle.]
To get to Iquitos, Peru, your only options are a plane... or days on a boat. It
may be the most isolated city of its size on Earth.
[A lone dot glowing deep in the Amazon, surrounded by green in every direction.]

--- WHERE IT IS (≈0:10–0:32) ---

Iquitos sits deep in the Peruvian Amazon, on the banks of the Amazon River, with
rainforest stretching for hundreds of kilometers in every direction.
[Camera pulls out: the city, then nothing but forest and winding rivers.]
And it's widely considered the largest city in the world you simply can't drive
to. Around 470,000 people — and not a single road in.
[A "470,000" counter; then a red "0 roads in" stamp.]
There is one short highway to a nearby town — but it dead-ends in the jungle and
connects to nothing else.

--- HOW YOU GET THERE (≈0:32–0:50) ---

So how do half a million people stay connected? Two ways.
[Split screen: plane vs riverboat.]
You fly in — or you take a riverboat, several days up the Amazon, sleeping in a
hammock strung across the deck.
[A packed multi-deck boat drifting upriver, rows of hammocks swaying.]
That's it. Plane, or river.

--- THE TWIST: WHY SO BIG? (≈0:50–1:18) ---

Which raises the real question: how does a city this cut off get this big?
[The bustling modern city skyline by the river.]
Because it wasn't always a backwater. In the late 1800s, the rubber boom turned
Iquitos staggeringly rich.
[Old photos: rubber, riverboats, fortunes.]
Rubber barons poured fortunes in — importing European luxury, building tiled
mansions, even an entire iron house said to have come from Gustave Eiffel,
shipped over in pieces and bolted together in the jungle.
[Grand Belle Époque facades; the gleaming iron house.]
A pocket of European opulence, in the middle of the Amazon.

--- AND IT STAYED (≈1:18–1:35) ---

Then the rubber market crashed, and the easy money vanished. But the people
didn't. The city kept growing into the half-million-person hub it is today.
[The old grandeur fades into the busy modern streets.]
And everything you see there — every car, every brick, every bottle of soda —
had to arrive by boat or plane. The streets themselves run not on cars, but on
tens of thousands of three-wheeled moto-taxis.
[Swarms of buzzing moto-taxis flooding the streets.]

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:35–1:48) ---

So in an age where you can drive almost anywhere on Earth, here's a city of half a
million where the road simply... never arrived.
[Aerial: the city glowing, ringed by unbroken jungle and river.]
To reach it, you still do it the way people have for centuries — by the river.
