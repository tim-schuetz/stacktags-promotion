Title: A new ocean is forming in Russia
Topic: Geography
Target length: ~95 seconds (fits the 30s–3min window)
Language of narration: English
Format: Voiceover script. Spoken lines are plain text. [Bracketed lines] describe

==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
Lake Baikal (Siberia) is the deepest lake on Earth (~1,640 m). It's so deep its
floor lies more than a kilometer below sea level — the lowest point of the
continental crust. It sits on an active rift where the crust is pulling apart
(~2 cm/year). That's exactly how oceans are born, so in tens of millions of years
Baikal could widen into a sea, then an ocean.
Punchline: give this Siberian lake enough time, and it might become the next ocean.

NOTE (honesty): "could / might / over tens of millions of years" — it's a deep-
time possibility, not a forecast. Depth ~1,640 m; floor ~1.2 km below sea level.

KEY VISUALS:
- A cross-section of Baikal plunging below a sea-level line; the crust pulling
  apart with arrows; a time-lapse widening it into a sea.

==================================================================
SCRIPT
==================================================================

--- HOOK (0:00–0:12) — the payoff up front ---

At the bottom of this lake in Siberia is the lowest point of the Earth's
continental crust.
[Cross-section: Baikal's floor dropping far below a "sea level" line.]
And it's sitting on a crack that, millions of years from now, could tear open into
a brand-new ocean.
[The crust splits with outward arrows; the lake begins to widen.]
This is Lake Baikal — possibly an ocean in its infancy.

--- THE DEEPEST LAKE (≈0:12–0:38) ---

Baikal is the deepest lake on the planet — over 1,600 meters from surface to
floor.
[A depth gauge plunging past 1,000... 1,600 meters.]
It sits high up in the mountains, but it's so deep that its bed lies more than a
kilometer below sea level — the lowest point of any continent's crust on Earth.
[The floor marker glowing far beneath the sea-level line.]
So why is there a gash this deep in the middle of a continent?

--- THE CRACK (≈0:38–1:08) ---

Because Baikal sits on a rift — a place where the Earth's crust is literally
pulling apart, a couple of centimeters every year.
[Two plates drifting away from each other; the gap filling with water.]
The lake is the flooded scar of that widening gap. And that's exactly how oceans
begin: a continent tears along a rift, the crack gets wider and deeper, water
pours in —
[Time-lapse: the rift stretches, deepens, floods into a sea.]
and over tens of millions of years, it can grow into a sea, and then an ocean.
It's the same process slowly splitting East Africa apart right now.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:08–1:22) ---

We think of oceans as permanent and lakes as small and temporary.
[The serene lake, then a ghostly future ocean overlaid on it.]
But give this Siberian lake enough time — and it just might become the next one.
