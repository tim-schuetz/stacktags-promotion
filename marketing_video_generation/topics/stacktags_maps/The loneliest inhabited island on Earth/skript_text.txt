VIDEO SCRIPT
Title: The loneliest inhabited island on Earth
Topic: Geography
Target length: ~90 seconds (fits the 30s–3min window)
Language of narration: English
Format: Voiceover script. Spoken lines are plain text. [Bracketed lines] describe
what is on screen AT THE SAME TIME as the narration next to them.

==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
Tristan da Cunha: the most remote inhabited place on Earth — a tiny volcanic
island in the South Atlantic, ~240 people, a British territory. Nearest inhabited
land (Saint Helena) ~2,400 km away; no airport/runway; reachable only by a ~week-
long boat trip, with only ~9 ships a year. Settled in the 1800s, the islanders
speak a distinctive 19th-century English dialect.
Punchline: in a fully mapped, connected world, here "civilization" is still a week
away by sea.

KEY VISUALS:
- A lone island in vast empty ocean; a "nearest land: 2,400 km" ring; a supply
  ship as the only link.

==================================================================
SCRIPT
==================================================================

--- HOOK (0:00–0:12) — the payoff up front ---

This is the most remote inhabited place on the entire planet.
[A single speck of an island in endless open ocean.]
The nearest other island is 2,400 kilometers away. There's no airport. And in a
whole year, only about nine ships ever reach it.
[A "nearest land: 2,400 km" ring; a lone ship inching toward the dot.]
Welcome to Tristan da Cunha.

--- THE ISOLATION (≈0:12–0:42) ---

It's a tiny volcanic island in the middle of the South Atlantic, home to around
240 people — a British territory, marooned in the sea.
[The island in context: nothing but water in every direction.]
The nearest inhabited land, Saint Helena, is some 2,400 kilometers off. There's no
runway — you can't fly in at all. The only way to get there is by boat: roughly a
week's sailing from South Africa, and only a handful of ships make the trip each
year.
[A slow ship crossing days of empty ocean.]
Order something, and it arrives... eventually.

--- LIFE THERE (≈0:42–1:08) ---

Everyone lives in one small settlement. There are only a handful of family
surnames on the whole island, and people get by farming and fishing.
[The single village, tucked under the volcano.]
And here's the lovely part: cut off for over two hundred years — settled back in
the 1800s — the islanders speak their own English. An accent half-frozen from the
nineteenth century, found nowhere else on Earth.
[Speech bubbles with an old-fashioned lilt.]

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:08–1:20) ---

In a world that feels completely mapped and wired together —
[Pull back to the whole globe, the island a tiny dot far from anything.]
there's still a place where "civilization" is a week away by sea, and your nearest
neighbor is over the horizon, 2,400 kilometers out.

Want more places at the edge of the map?

Follow Stacktags.
