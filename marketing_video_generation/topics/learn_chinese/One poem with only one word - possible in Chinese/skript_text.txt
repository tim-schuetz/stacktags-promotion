VIDEO SCRIPT
Title: One poem with only one word — possible in Chinese
Topic: Learn Chinese
Target length: ~95 seconds (fits the 30s–3min window)
Language of narration: English
Format: Voiceover script. Spoken lines are plain text. [Bracketed lines] describe
what is on screen AT THE SAME TIME as the narration next to them.

==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
The "Lion-Eating Poet in the Stone Den," by linguist Yuen Ren Chao: ninety-odd
Classical-Chinese characters, every single one pronounced "shi" (only the tone
differs — shī / shí / shǐ / shì). Read aloud, it's an unintelligible wall of
"shi." Written down, it's a perfectly clear story.
Punchline: in Chinese, the WRITING — not the sound — carries the meaning.

GUARDRAILS (do NOT violate):
- Do NOT claim specific "distinct syllable" counts per language (e.g. the
  debunked "18 Cantonese / 15 Hokkien / 4 Mandarin"). Show homophony QUALITATIVELY only.
- Be honest: this is a deliberate, extreme stunt in Classical (literary) Chinese
  — NOT how everyday spoken Chinese works. Say so.

KEY VISUALS:
- The poem as a tall column of characters, every pinyin label reading shī/shí/shǐ/shì.
- A toggle between "READ ALOUD" (audio: a shi-shi-shi blur) and
  "ON PAPER" (the same text resolving into a clear illustrated story).

==================================================================
SCRIPT
==================================================================

--- HOOK (0:00–0:08) — the payoff up front ---

What you see here is a poem. It's more than ninety characters long. And every
single one of them is pronounced exactly the same way: "shi."
[The full poem fills the screen as a wall of Chinese characters, every pinyin label reading shī / shí / shǐ / shì; below it, just an ellipsis "…".]
In Chinese, somehow, that actually works.

--- THE POEM (≈0:08–0:30) ---

It's called "The Lion-Eating Poet in the Stone Den," written by the linguist
Yuen Ren Chao.
[Title card of the poem beside a portrait of Chao.]
Ninety-something characters — and the only thing separating them is their tone.
Now, I am not going to read it aloud, but I got some help from google translate:
shī, shí, shǐ, shì. 
Four flavors of a single syllable.
[The four tone marks animate one by one over the word "shi".]

--- READ IT ALOUD (≈0:30–0:50) ---

[Switch to "READ ALOUD" mode; audio: "Shíshì shīshì Shī shì, shì shī, shì shí shí shī..." a rapid blur.]
Shi shi shi shi shi. To your ears — to anyone's ears — it's pure nonsense. A wall
of the exact same sound. Nobody could follow it.
[A baffled listener; the subtitles just read "shi shi shi shi…?"]

--- NOW READ IT ON PAPER (≈0:50–1:18) ---

But now look at it written down.
[Switch to "ON PAPER" mode; the characters light up one by one with their meanings.]
Suddenly it's a crisp little story: a poet named Shi, who's obsessed with eating
lions, vows to eat ten of them. He goes to the market, finds ten lions, kills
them, hauls them home — and discovers they were actually ten stone lions.
[The story plays out as quick illustrations beside the characters.]
Every "shi" is a different character, with a different meaning. On paper, there's
zero confusion.

--- WHY IT WORKS (≈1:18–1:30) ---

And that's the whole trick: Chinese characters carry meaning by their shape, not
their sound.
[The poem from the very beginning is shown again — now read as meaning, not sound.]
