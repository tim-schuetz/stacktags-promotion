VIDEO SCRIPT
Title: Why two countries refuse to own this land
Topic: Geography
Target length: ~100 seconds (fits the 30s–3min window)
Language of narration: English
Format: Voiceover script. Spoken lines are plain text. [Bracketed lines] describe
what is on screen AT THE SAME TIME as the narration next to them.

==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
Bir Tawil (~2,060 km², between Egypt and Sudan) is "terra nullius" — claimed by
no state. Two old British border lines (1899 straight line vs 1902 administrative
line) disagree about TWO areas: Bir Tawil, and the bigger, coastal, valuable
Hala'ib Triangle. The border that gives you Hala'ib hands Bir Tawil to the other
country. So both claim Hala'ib and both REJECT Bir Tawil — claiming it would
weaken their claim to the prize. Not-wanting as strategy.
Punchline: Bir Tawil is unwanted not because land is worthless, but because
wanting it would cost too much.

KEY VISUALS (as requested):
- Two overlapping border lines; in the gap between them, a label blinks
  "Claimed by: nobody."

==================================================================
SCRIPT
==================================================================

--- HOOK (0:00–0:12) — the payoff up front ---

There's a patch of land the size of a small country that no nation on Earth will
claim.
[A barren desert quadrilateral; a "Claimed by: nobody" tag blinks over it.]
Not because it's worthless — but because owning it would actually cost its two
neighbors something. So Egypt and Sudan are doing the opposite of a border war:
they're both fighting NOT to own it.
[Two flags, Egypt and Sudan, each turning their back on the land.]
This is Bir Tawil. Here's the strange logic.

--- THE TWO BORDERS (≈0:12–0:42) ---

It comes down to two lines, drawn by Britain over a century ago. In 1899, they
drew a straight border. In 1902, they drew a second, different one.
[Two lines appear across the map — one straight, one bending.]
And those two lines disagree about two pieces of land: Bir Tawil — dry, empty,
landlocked — and right next to it, the Hala'ib Triangle: much bigger, sitting on
the Red Sea coast, and far more valuable.
[Bir Tawil small and brown; Hala'ib large, green, with a coastline glinting.]

--- THE TRAP (≈0:42–1:12) ---

Here's the catch. One border gives Egypt the valuable Hala'ib Triangle — but
hands Bir Tawil to Sudan. The other gives Sudan Hala'ib — but hands Bir Tawil to
Egypt.
[The lines toggle; Hala'ib and Bir Tawil swap colors back and forth.]
You can't grab both. The line that wins you the prize automatically gives away
the scrap. So both countries pick the border that claims Hala'ib — which means
both of them reject Bir Tawil.
[Both flags plant firmly on Hala'ib; both recoil from Bir Tawil.]
Claiming the worthless land would quietly weaken their claim to the valuable one.
So neither will touch it.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:12–1:25) ---

And so Bir Tawil sits there — one of the last unclaimed lands on the planet. People
have even flown in to plant flags and crown themselves king. But no real country
will have it.
[A lone flag flapping in empty desert; "Claimed by: nobody" still blinking.]
Because sometimes the smartest move on the board... is refusing to take the piece.
