==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
Today the Amazon flows WEST → EAST into the Atlantic. But before the Andes rose,
South America's drainage ran the other way — WEST, toward the Pacific. As South
America drifted west, the Nazca plate subducted beneath it and pushed up the
Andes, walling off the western exit. The trapped water pooled into a vast inland
wetland/mega-lake (the Pebas system), then broke through eastward, reversing the
river. The modern east-flowing, transcontinental Amazon dates to roughly ~10
million years ago.
Punchline: the mightiest river on Earth flows east because a continent drifted,
an ocean floor dove under it, and a mountain range shoved it the other way.

NOTE (honesty): timescales are deep and approximate, reconstructed by geologists
("over a hundred million years," "around 10 million years ago"). Keep "roughly."

KEY VISUALS:
- A map of South America with a flow ARROW that flips: Pacific ← then → Atlantic.
- A tectonic cross-section: the Nazca plate subducting, the Andes rising as a wall.
- A giant inland lake/wetland filling the basin, then bursting east.

SCRIPT
==================================================================

--- HOOK (0:00–0:10) — the payoff up front ---

The Amazon — the largest river on Earth — once flowed backwards.
[A map of South America; the river arrow snaps into reverse.]
Today it runs west to east, into the Atlantic. But for most of its history, it
flowed the opposite way — west, into the Pacific. And what turned a river this
enormous completely around was an entire continent.
[The whole continent shifts; a mountain range erupts along its edge.]
Here's the story.

--- THE AMAZON TODAY (≈0:10–0:30) ---

Right now, the Amazon is born high in the Andes, on the western side of South
America, and runs almost the entire width of the continent — thousands of
kilometers east — before pouring into the Atlantic.
[Map: source in the Andes → a fat arrow sweeping east to the Atlantic.]
It's the biggest river on the planet by far. So you'd assume it always flowed
this way. It didn't.

--- BEFORE THE ANDES (≈0:30–0:52) ---

Rewind far enough — well over a hundred million years — and there were no Andes
at all.
[The Andes flatten away; the western coast goes low and smooth.]
Back then, the land tilted the other way. So the early Amazon drained westward —
toward the Pacific.
[The arrow reverses: water flowing west, off the continent into the Pacific.]
A giant river, running the exact opposite direction. Until the ground itself
started to move.

--- THE WALL RISES (≈0:52–1:18) ---

Because South America was drifting west — and off its western coast, the floor of
the Pacific was diving down underneath it.
[Tectonic cross-section: the Nazca plate sliding beneath South America.]
That collision crumpled the edge of the continent upward, slowly raising the
Andes — the longest mountain range on land.
[The Andes push up into a towering wall.]
And that wall sealed the river's exit. The westward water suddenly had nowhere
left to go.
[The west-flowing arrow slams into the new mountains and stops.]

--- THE GREAT REVERSAL (≈1:18–1:42) ---

So it pooled. For millions of years, the heart of the continent became a vast
inland world of swamps and lakes.
[A huge inland lake/wetland floods the basin behind the mountains.]
Until, finally, all that trapped water found a new way out — to the east —
carving a path toward the Atlantic.
[The water bursts east; the arrow flips for good, racing to the Atlantic.]
The river had reversed. And roughly 10 million years ago, the Amazon we know
today — flowing west to east — was born.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:42–1:55) ---

So the mightiest river on Earth doesn't flow east because it always did. It flows
east because a continent drifted, an ocean floor dove under it, and a mountain
range rose up and turned it around.
[Pull back to the whole continent, the Andes on one side, the Amazon sweeping to the other.]
The Andes didn't just block the Amazon. They reversed it.
