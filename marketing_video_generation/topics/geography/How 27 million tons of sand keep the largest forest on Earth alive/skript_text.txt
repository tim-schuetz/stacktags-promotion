VIDEO SCRIPT
Title: How 27 million tons of sand keep the largest forest on Earth alive
Topic: Geography
Target length: ~100 seconds (fits the 30s–3min window)
Language of narration: English
Format: Voiceover script. Spoken lines are plain text. [Bracketed lines] describe
what is on screen AT THE SAME TIME as the narration next to them.

==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
The Amazon's soil constantly loses phosphorus to heavy rain. Every year, winds
carry tens of millions of tons of Saharan dust across the Atlantic — and the
PHOSPHORUS in it (~22,000 tons/year) almost exactly replaces what the rainforest
loses. Most of that nutrient comes from ONE dried-up ancient lakebed, the Bodélé
Depression in Chad. The biggest desert feeds the biggest jungle.
Punchline: the planet's two most opposite places are linked by one invisible
supply line of dust.

⚠️ GUARDRAIL: "22,000 tons" = PHOSPHORUS (the nutrient), NOT total dust (~27–28
MILLION tons). Always say "nutrient / phosphorus" for the 22,000 figure.

KEY VISUALS:
- A dust plume lifting off the Sahara and streaming across the Atlantic to the
  Amazon; a glowing source point at the Bodélé Depression.

==================================================================
SCRIPT
==================================================================

--- HOOK (0:00–0:12) — the payoff up front ---

Every year, a dried-up lakebed in the middle of the Sahara reaches clear across
an ocean — and fertilizes the Amazon rainforest.
[A dust plume rises from the desert, sweeps over the Atlantic, settles on the jungle.]
The largest desert on Earth is quietly feeding the largest jungle on Earth. And
they're nowhere near each other.
[A globe: Sahara on one side, Amazon on the other, a dust trail linking them.]

--- THE PROBLEM (≈0:12–0:38) ---

Here's the strange part. The Amazon looks impossibly lush — but its soil is
actually poor. The constant tropical rain keeps washing nutrients out of the
ground and into the rivers. Especially one: phosphorus.
[Rain hammering the forest floor; phosphorus draining away down the rivers.]
The forest is slowly leaking the very fertilizer it needs to survive. So how does
it keep going?

--- THE DELIVERY (≈0:38–1:08) ---

The answer blows in from thousands of kilometers away. Each year, winds lift tens
of millions of tons of dust off the Sahara and carry it across the Atlantic.
[Satellite-style view: a vast dust river crossing the ocean.]
And hidden in that dust is phosphorus. The amount that settles onto the Amazon —
around 22,000 tons of it a year — almost exactly replaces what the rainforest
loses to the rain.
[A balance scale: "phosphorus lost to rain" vs "phosphorus delivered by dust" — level.]
The desert isn't just dumping sand. It's restocking the exact nutrient the jungle
runs out of.

--- ONE DEAD LAKE (≈1:08–1:30) ---

And most of that phosphorus comes from a single spot: the Bodélé Depression, in
Chad —
[Zoom to one glowing point in the Sahara.]
the bed of an ancient lake that dried up long ago, packed with the phosphorus-rich
remains of microorganisms that died there. One dead lake, fertilizing an entire
continent across the sea.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:30–1:42) ---

The Amazon doesn't stand on its own.
[The forest breathing, the dust trail still glowing behind it.]
It's quietly kept alive by dust from a desert half a world away — the two most
opposite places on Earth, locked into one invisible supply line.
