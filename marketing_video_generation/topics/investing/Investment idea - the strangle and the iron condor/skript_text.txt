==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
Two neutral, premium-selling strategies that profit when a stock goes NOWHERE —
ideal for calm, low-volatility stocks/indices you expect to stay in a range:
- SHORT STRANGLE: sell an OTM call AND an OTM put → collect both premiums; profit
  if the stock stays between the strikes; but risk is large/undefined if it makes
  a big move (unlimited on the call side).
- IRON CONDOR: the same range bet, but you BUY a further-out call and put as
  "wings" → caps the max loss (defined risk), for a bit less premium.
Both profit from time decay + the stock staying still.
Punchline: most traders bet on where a stock is going; these bet on where it isn't.

NOTE (responsibility): educational, NOT advice. Honest shape — wins are small and
frequent, losses are rare but can be big (especially the naked strangle); an
earnings shock can erase months of gains.

KEY VISUALS:
- A "profit tent" between two strikes; stock inside = win, breaking out the sides = loss.
- The iron condor adding guardrails (wings) that cap each side.

--- HOOK (0:00–0:12) — the payoff up front ---

Here's how traders make money when a stock does... absolutely nothing.
[A flat, sideways chart — and a profit counter ticking up.]
Two strategies — the strangle and the iron condor — pay you to bet that a stock
stays boring and goes nowhere.
[Two labels appear. Tag: "educational — not advice."]

--- THE PREMISE (≈0:12–0:30) ---

Most trades need the stock to move. These do the opposite — they profit when it
sits still.
[A stock pinned between two lines, barely wiggling.]
Which makes them a fit for calm, low-volatility stocks or whole indices that tend
to stay in a range. You're betting on stillness.

--- THE STRANGLE (≈0:30–0:58) ---

First, the short strangle. You sell two options at once: an out-of-the-money call
above the price, and an out-of-the-money put below it — and you collect both
premiums up front.
[A call sold above, a put sold below; double premium collected.]
As long as the stock stays between your two strikes, both expire worthless and you
keep everything — helped along by time decay, every single day.
[A green "tent" between the strikes; the stock drifting harmlessly inside.]
The catch: if the stock makes a big move either way, losses can grow large — and
on the upside, with no limit. The risk is wide open on both ends.

--- THE IRON CONDOR (≈0:58–1:28) ---

So here's the safer cousin: the iron condor. Same range bet — but you add "wings."
[The same tent, now with two outer options added.]
You also BUY a further-out call and a further-out put, as insurance. Those wings
cap your maximum loss on each side. You collect a little less premium, but now your
worst case is fixed and known.
[Guardrails snap onto both edges of the tent; the open-ended risk gets a ceiling.]
It's the defined-risk version of the same idea: still betting the stock stays put,
but with a seatbelt on.

--- THE SHAPE OF THE BET (≈1:28–1:48) ---

Picture a profit tent. You win if the stock stays under it — between your strikes.
You lose if it breaks out the sides.
[The tent: inside = green, outside = red; condor caps how red it gets.]
Be honest about the trade-off: these win often, but in small amounts — and lose
rarely, but can lose big, especially the naked strangle. One surprise earnings move
can wipe out months of quiet gains.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:48–2:00) ---

Most traders bet on where a stock is going.
[Arrows pointing up and down, crossed out.]
These two bet on where it isn't — that it'll simply sit still. For once, boring is
the entire point.
