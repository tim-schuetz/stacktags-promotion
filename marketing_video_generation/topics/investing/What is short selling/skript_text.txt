==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
Short selling = borrow shares, sell them now at a high price, buy them back later
(hopefully cheaper) to return them, and keep the difference. It profits when a
stock FALLS. Critical risk: a stock can rise without limit, so a short's losses
are theoretically unlimited — and a "short squeeze" can amplify that fast.
Punchline: sell high, buy low — in that order. The upside is capped; the downside
isn't.

NOTE (responsibility): educational, NOT advice. The unlimited-loss point is the
key honest message — make it land.

KEY VISUALS:
- Four steps: BORROW → SELL ($100) → wait → BUY BACK ($60) → return + keep $40.
- A risk graphic: normal stock floors at $0; a short's loss line shooting up
  with no ceiling.

SCRIPT
==================================================================

--- HOOK (0:00–0:12) — the payoff up front ---

Short selling is how you make money when a stock goes down.
[A red chart falling; a profit line rising with it.]
And the trick sounds completely backwards: you sell the stock first... and buy it
later. Here's how you sell something you don't even own.
[A share being borrowed, then sold — before it's owned.]

--- THE STEPS (≈0:12–0:42) ---

It's four steps. One: you borrow shares from someone who owns them — your broker
arranges it. Two: you immediately sell those borrowed shares at today's price.
[BORROW → SELL at $100.]
Three: you wait. Four: if the price drops, you buy the same shares back, cheaper,
return them to the lender — and keep the difference.
[Stock falls to $60 → BUY BACK → return → "+$40" pocketed.]
Sell at a hundred, buy back at sixty, walk away with forty. You profited from the
fall.

--- WHY IT EXISTS (≈0:42–1:00) ---

It lets traders bet against companies they think are overpriced or in trouble —
and it's a core way hedge funds protect, or "hedge," their other bets.
[A short position offsetting a portfolio's losses.]
But it comes with a danger most beginners underestimate.

--- THE DANGER (≈1:00–1:28) ---

When you buy a stock, the worst case is it goes to zero — you lose 100%, and
that's the floor.
[A bought stock bottoming out at $0.]
But when you short, the stock can rise with no ceiling. If it doubles, triples,
ten-x's, your losses just keep growing — theoretically, without limit.
[The short's loss line rocketing upward, no cap.]
And if lots of shorts panic and buy back at once, they push the price even higher
— a "short squeeze." That's exactly what blew up traders during the GameStop
frenzy.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:28–1:40) ---

So short selling flips investing on its head: sell high, buy low, in that order.
[The four steps glowing.]
The upside is capped — but the downside isn't. Which is why it's very much a
professional's game.
