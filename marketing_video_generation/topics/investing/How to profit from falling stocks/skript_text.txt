==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
You can profit when stocks fall, not just when they rise. Three main tools:
(1) short selling (borrow + sell high, buy back low — unlimited risk),
(2) put options (right to sell at a fixed price — defined risk, like insurance),
(3) inverse ETFs (go up when an index goes down — simple, but decay over time).
Punchline: up isn't the only direction you can bet on.

NOTE (responsibility): educational, NOT financial advice. Flag the real risks —
shorting's unlimited downside, inverse-ETF decay, and that markets rise more often
than they fall over time. Keep an on-screen "educational only" tag.

KEY VISUALS:
- A red, falling stock chart — and a portfolio line going UP as it drops.
- Three labeled paths: "Short selling," "Put options," "Inverse ETF."

--- HOOK (0:00–0:12) — the payoff up front ---

Most people only make money when stocks go up. But professionals have ways to
profit when they crash. There are three main ones:
[A red chart dives; a second line climbs as it falls.]
short selling, put options, and inverse ETFs.
[Three labels drop in. Small tag: "educational — not financial advice."]
Here's how each one turns a falling market into a winning trade.

--- THE MINDSET (≈0:12–0:28) ---

Normally, investing is "buy low, sell high." To profit from a fall, you just flip
the order — you arrange to sell high, then buy low.
[The two steps swap places: SELL first, BUY second.]
The question is how you sell something before you own it. Three answers.

--- 1: SHORT SELLING (≈0:28–0:50) ---

First, short selling. You borrow shares you don't own, sell them now at the high
price, and aim to buy them back later, cheaper — keeping the difference.
[Borrow → sell at $100 → buy back at $60 → pocket $40.]
If the stock drops, you win. But fair warning: if it rises instead, your losses
can balloon — far more than with normal buying. (We break that down in our
short-selling video.)

--- 2: PUT OPTIONS (≈0:50–1:12) ---

Second, put options. A put is the right to sell a stock at a fixed price. So if
the stock crashes below that price, your put becomes valuable.
[A put "strike" line; the stock plunges under it, the put lights up green.]
It works like insurance that pays out when the stock falls — and the most you can
lose is the small premium you paid for it. Defined, limited risk.

--- 3: INVERSE ETFs (≈1:12–1:30) ---

Third, inverse ETFs. These are funds built to go up when an index goes down. You
just buy them like a normal share — no borrowing, no options.
[An inverse ETF ticking up as the index ticks down.]
Simple — but they're designed for short-term moves and slowly decay over time, so
they're not something you hold for years.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:30–1:42) ---

So up isn't the only direction you can bet on.
[The falling market, with a green line riding it down.]
With the right tool, a crash isn't only a danger — for some, it's the trade. Just
remember: betting against the market is harder than it looks.
