==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
Strategy: SELL (short) an out-of-the-money call on a freshly-IPO'd, high-hype tech
stock to harvest its inflated option premium. Three profit drivers:
1. Volatility crush — sky-high IPO implied volatility collapses → the option you
   sold gets cheaper to buy back.
2. Time decay (theta) — the option loses time value every day, in your favor.
3. You don't need it to fall — with an OTM strike you keep the premium if the stock
   falls, stays flat, OR rises moderately (3 of 4 outcomes).
RISK (the key honest point): a naked short call has THEORETICALLY UNLIMITED loss —
a hyped IPO can double/triple. The fix: cap it with a call credit spread (buy a
further-out call), or only do it covered. Advanced. Educational, NOT advice.
Punchline: you get paid by time, by collapsing volatility, and by the hype simply
not coming true — but if you're naked and wrong, the loss has no ceiling.

KEY VISUALS:
- A post-IPO stock with a huge "IV" gauge that then collapses.
- A profit zone below the strike (green) and an open-ended red loss zone above it.

SCRIPT
==================================================================

--- HOOK (0:00–0:14) — the payoff up front ---

Here's an advanced options trade: short selling a call option on a stock that just
went public.
[A freshly-IPO'd tech stock; a "SELL CALL" ticket.]
Done right, you can profit three different ways at once — even if the stock keeps
rising. Done wrong, your losses have no limit.
[A green profit zone, and a red loss zone with no top edge. Tag: "educational — not advice."]
Let me show you both sides.

--- THE SETUP (≈0:14–0:38) ---

A tech company just IPO'd. The hype is enormous — and so is its options' "implied
volatility," which is just a measure of how big a move the market is pricing in.
[An "IV" gauge pinned to the max.]
High implied volatility means the options are expensive. And if you're the one
SELLING an option, expensive is exactly what you want.
[Pricey option premiums glowing.]
So you sell — short — a call with a strike well above today's price, and you
collect that fat premium up front.

--- PROFIT DRIVER 1: VOLATILITY CRUSH (≈0:38–0:58) ---

Now the first way you win: the volatility crush. Once the IPO frenzy cools, that
sky-high implied volatility collapses.
[The IV gauge plunging from max toward normal.]
And falling volatility makes the option you sold cheaper to buy back — which is
pure profit for the seller. The air comes out of the hype, and you pocket it.

--- PROFIT DRIVER 2: TIME DECAY (≈0:58–1:15) ---

The second way: time decay. Every single day, an option loses a slice of its time
value — and as the seller, that decay flows straight to you.
[A clock ticking; the option's value melting downward into your account.]
Even if the stock doesn't move at all, time alone is paying you.

--- PROFIT DRIVER 3: YOU DON'T NEED A CRASH (≈1:15–1:35) ---

And the third way is the clever one: you don't even need the stock to fall. Because
you sold a call above the current price, you keep the entire premium as long as the
stock stays below your strike.
[A green zone under the strike: stock falls, flat, OR rises a little — all win.]
So you profit if it drops, if it goes nowhere, OR if it rises just moderately.
Three out of four directions go your way.

--- THE RISK (≈1:35–1:58) ---

But here's the catch that can wipe you out. A call you've sold has no upside cap.
[The red zone above the strike — stretching up with no ceiling.]
If that hyped IPO rockets — and these absolutely can double or triple in weeks —
your losses grow without limit. A "naked" short call is one of the riskiest
positions in all of trading.
[A stock blasting through the strike; the loss line shooting up off-screen.]
The standard fix: turn it into a defined-risk trade by also BUYING a further-out
call — a "call credit spread" — which caps your worst case. Never do this uncovered
unless you fully understand, and can afford, the worst outcome.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:58–2:12) ---

So selling a call is a bet that the hype is overdone. You get paid by time, by
collapsing volatility, and by the stock simply not mooning.
[The three profit drivers lined up.]
The reward is steady — but if you're naked and wrong, the risk is brutal. Reward,
and risk, are always two sides of the same trade.
