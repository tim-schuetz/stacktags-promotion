When North Korean hackers pulled off the biggest crypto heist in history — around 1.5 billion dollars — they ran straight into a problem most people never expect: in crypto, you can't actually hide the money.

Most people think crypto is anonymous — perfect for crime. It's almost the exact opposite.

And it comes down to every blockchain's most innate structure. The ledger isn't kept in one place. An identical copy of it lives on thousands of computers all around the world. Every single transaction is broadcast to all of them, and locked into the very same shared record. So the instant these hackers moved a coin, every one of those machines saw it — and kept it, forever.

So when the group known as Lazarus drained that exchange, the whole world could watch the stolen funds sitting in their wallets. Stealing it was the easy part. Spending it is the hard part.

Because the moment they try to send that money to an exchange to cash out, it gets flagged and frozen. So to have any hope of using it, they try to break the trail. They do it three ways.

One: chain hopping. They bounce the money across different blockchains, again and again, so the trail has to keep jumping networks.

Two: mixers. They throw the coins into a pot with the coins of thousands of other users who also aim to obscure their traces, may it be because of criminal activities or just because they want to uphold privacy.

Three: splitting. They chop the haul into thousands of tiny transfers across countless wallets, so there's no single stream left to follow.

And that's the irony. The only reason they need all of this is that crypto isn't anonymous at all. It's the most transparent money ever made — a permanent record, copied across the entire world. So stealing a billion is the easy part. Actually spending it is a problem most criminals never saw coming.
