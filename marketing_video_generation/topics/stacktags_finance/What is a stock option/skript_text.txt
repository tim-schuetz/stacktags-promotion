==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
A stock option is the RIGHT (not obligation) to buy (a "call") or sell (a "put") a
stock at a fixed "strike" price by a deadline, for a small fee (the premium). It
gives leveraged upside with capped downside (you can only lose the premium). The
twist (teaser): options can also be SOLD/shorted like a stock — opening up bets on
volatility and stranger strategies (→ links to the short-a-call video).
Punchline: a small bet on what a stock will do — capped loss, leveraged upside;
and once you can buy AND sell them, you can bet on up, down, or how wild it gets.

NOTE (responsibility): educational, NOT advice. Keep the example simple/illustrative.

KEY VISUALS:
- A "$110 strike" line; the stock crossing it; a small $2 premium ballooning into $20.
- CALL = right to BUY; PUT = right to SELL.

--- HOOK (0:00–0:12) — the payoff up front ---

A stock option is the right to buy or sell a stock at a fixed price — no matter
what the real price does.
[A locked "$110" price tag on a stock that's moving around it.]
It can let you control a 10,000-dollar position for a couple hundred bucks. Here's
how it works.
[A tiny premium controlling a big block of shares.]

--- THE DEFINITION (≈0:12–0:35) ---

An option gives you the right — but not the obligation — to buy or sell a stock at
a set price, called the strike, before a deadline. For that right, you pay a small
fee: the premium.
[Right (not obligation) → strike price → expiry date → premium.]
There are two kinds. A call is the right to BUY at the strike — you're betting the
stock goes up. A put is the right to SELL at the strike — you're betting it goes
down.
[CALL ↑ buy | PUT ↓ sell.]

--- A CONCRETE EXAMPLE (≈0:35–1:05) ---

Let's make it real. A stock trades at 100 dollars. You buy a call with a 110
strike, expiring in a month, for a 2-dollar premium.
[Stock $100 · strike $110 · premium $2.]
If the stock jumps to 130, you still have the right to buy at 110 — so your option
is now worth about 20 dollars. Your 2-dollar bet turned into twenty.
[The stock rockets to $130; the $2 premium balloons to ~$20.]
And if the stock never reaches 110? The option simply expires worthless, and you
lose only the 2 dollars you paid. Capped downside, leveraged upside.
[Stock stays low; the $2 premium fades to zero — and stops there.]

--- THE TWIST / TEASER (≈1:05–1:28) ---

Now here's the interesting part. Options aren't just something you buy. You can
also short-sell them — just like a stock — and take the other side of the bet.
[An option being sold short, the trade flipping sides.]
That unlocks strange strategies, where you stop betting on direction and start
betting on a stock's volatility — and a lot of other unusual wagers.
[A bet not on "up or down" but on "how wild?"]
We break one of those down in full here: "Investment idea — shorting a call
option."
[A card points to the next video.]

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:28–1:40) ---

So an option is a small bet on what a stock will do — limited loss, leveraged
upside.
[The example, capped on one side, open on the other.]
And once you can both buy and sell them, you can bet on almost anything: up, down,
or just how wild the ride gets.
