==================================================================
THE CORE IDEA
==================================================================
A Boeing 787's electrical power runs through Generator Control Units (GCUs). Each
GCU has an internal timer stored as a SIGNED 32-bit integer, counting in
HUNDREDTHS OF A SECOND. A signed 32-bit int maxes out around 2.1 billion, so:
  2^31 ÷ 100 ÷ 86,400 ≈ 248.55 days.
At that point the counter overflows, the GCU drops into "failsafe" and shuts
down. Because all four GCUs power up together, they overflow together → total
loss of AC power. The FAA's fix: power-cycle (reboot) the aircraft regularly.
Punchline: the world's most advanced airliner, fixed by "turn it off and on again."

GUARDRAILS (do NOT violate):
- Use the SIGNED 32-bit / hundredths-of-a-second framing. Do NOT say "unsigned"
  or "ticks every 5 ms" (that explanation was debunked).
- Source on screen: FAA Airworthiness Directive (2015).
- Be honest: it was caught in TESTING, never crashed a plane; AD is precautionary.

--- HOOK (0:00–0:12) — the payoff up front ---

Leave a Boeing 787 switched on for 248 days straight, and something bizarre
happens: all four of its generators shut down at the exact same instant —
[A 787; a "days powered on" counter climbs toward 248, then every generator icon flips red at once.]
and the plane loses all of its main electrical power. The official fix, straight
from the FAA? Turn it off and on again.
[A giant power button; a "REBOOT REQUIRED" stamp.]
Here's the line of code behind it.

--- THE ELECTRICAL SYSTEM (≈0:12–0:32) ---

A 787's power comes from generators bolted to its engines, and each one is run by
a small computer called a Generator Control Unit — a GCU.
[Engines → generators → four GCU boxes light up.]
Inside every GCU sits a timer, quietly counting how long the unit has been
powered on. And it counts in hundredths of a second.
[A counter ticking up fast: 0.01, 0.02, 0.03 ...]
Which sounds completely harmless. Until you look at how that number is stored.

--- THE OVERFLOW (≈0:32–1:02) ---

The counter is a signed 32-bit integer — a number with a hard ceiling of about
2.1 billion.
[2,147,483,647 appears; a bar starts filling toward it.]
So do the math. Tick that counter up every hundredth of a second, and it takes
2.1 billion ticks to fill —
[Math overlay: 2³¹ ÷ 100 ÷ 86,400 ≈ 248.55 days.]
which works out to almost exactly 248.55 days.
[The bar slams into the ceiling; the number flips to a glitchy negative.]
The instant it hits that ceiling, the number overflows — it rolls over into
nonsense the software was never built to handle. So the GCU does the only safe
thing it knows: it shuts itself down. Failsafe.

--- WHY ALL FOUR AT ONCE (≈1:02–1:25) ---

And here's the part that turns a glitch into a nightmare. When you power up the
plane, all four GCUs start their counters at basically the same moment.
[Four counters starting in perfect sync.]
So they don't fail one at a time. They reach 248.55 days together — and overflow
together. Four generators, gone in the same instant. A total loss of AC power.
[All four flip red simultaneously; the cabin lights flicker.]

--- THE FIX (≈1:25–1:48) ---

Now, to be clear: this never actually brought down a plane. Boeing caught it on
the ground, in testing.
[A lab test bench — not a crash.]
But the risk was real enough that in 2015 the FAA issued an official airworthiness
directive.
[An FAA document: "AIRWORTHINESS DIRECTIVE (2015)" — shown as the source.]
And the mandated fix, for the most advanced airliner ever built, was beautifully
dumb: power the aircraft all the way off before that counter ever gets close.
Reboot it. Boeing later patched the software for good.

--- PUNCHLINE / OUTRO (≈1:48–2:02) ---

So the most sophisticated machine humanity builds can be undone by the same thing
as your home router: a number that simply got too big to count.
[The 787, calm and steady in the sky.]
It'll fly forever — as long as someone remembers to turn it off and on again.
